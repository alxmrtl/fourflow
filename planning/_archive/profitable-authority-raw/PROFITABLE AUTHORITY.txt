CONTENT RULES

Lead with the buyer’s lived situation before introducing the framework.

Use plain team language before FourFlow language.

Do not lead with “flow,” “spirit,” “meaningful work,” or theory-heavy vocabulary.

Do not sound like the engagement industry.

Do not attack surveys, offsites, manager training, or psychological safety as useless. The stronger position is: useful tools fail when they are deployed before diagnosis.

Do not overclaim proof. Be clear that paid organizational case studies are being built.

Use sample maps, teardowns, before/after diagnoses, and anonymized patterns to demonstrate capability.

Every post should do at least one of four things: help the buyer recognize the pattern, sharpen the diagnosis, challenge a false assumption, or make the audit easier to understand.

Avoid generic claims like “build a thriving culture” or “unlock high performance.” The content should feel precise enough that it could only be for leaders dealing with capable teams producing flat work. This follows the difference between generic content and useful diagnostic content: vague claims fail, while specific audience/problem language builds trust.
