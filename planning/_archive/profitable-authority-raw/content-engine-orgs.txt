Not being able to answer "what's actually wrong" with enough precision to act.
Being measured by survey scores that do not reflect what's actually happening
in the team.
The pattern of running an offsite, getting two good weeks, and reverting.
The sense that they are doing everything right and the team is still not
producing what it should.
Strong people leaving for reasons they cannot counter because they cannot
name the actual cause.

What they wish would change:

They want to look at the problem and know exactly what to fix. Not a vague
direction like "improve culture." A specific condition. A targeted first action.
A way to tell whether the action worked.

They want to stop launching programs that feel like they might work and start

6. CORE MESSAGE

What the audience needs to understand before they buy:

The problem with their team is not what they think it is.

They have been treating this as a culture problem, a management problem,
or a motivation problem. It is almost certainly a conditions problem —
and the conditions most likely to be broken are the two layers that no
standard engagement program, survey, or offsite is designed to address.

What they currently misunderstand:

That running a better program will close the gap. It will not — not until
they know which condition the program needs to target.

That "engagement" is the right frame for the problem. Engagement is a symptom.
The conditions that produce or block engagement are the actual variable.

That this is a management problem they need to train their way out of.
Management behavior is downstream of conditions. Fixing the manager's behavior
without fixing the conditions they are operating in does not hold.

The belief that needs to shift:

From: "We need a better engagement strategy."
To: "We need a diagnosis before we run another strategy."

The reframe in one sentence:

Most organizations are not under-invested in engagement. They are over-invested
in programs and under-invested in diagnosis.

13. STRONG OPINIONS

What the market needs to hear:

Most engagement programs are deployed before diagnosis. That's why they don't work.
You are not under-invested in engagement. You are over-invested in programs
and under-invested in figuring out which condition actually needs to change.

The most broken conditions in your team are almost certainly not the ones
your engagement survey is measuring. The survey is measuring morale, manager
perception, and workplace satisfaction. The conditions most likely to produce
flat output and quiet attrition — whether people have a generative narrative
about their role, whether the actual work aligns with the organization's stated
values — are almost never what the standard diagnostic touches.

Culture is not the problem. Culture is an output. When the culture is wrong,
something upstream of culture is broken. That upstream thing is diagnosable.
But you cannot diagnose it by running a culture initiative.

The exit interview is almost never honest. Not because people are deceptive,
but because the person leaving does not have the vocabulary to name the actual
cause. "Growth opportunity" and "culture fit" are the approximate translation
of something more specific that was never named while they were still employed.

Common advice to disagree with publicly:

"Focus on psychological safety." — True and necessary, but almost always
applied as a layer-one intervention when the block is in a different layer.
You can have high psychological safety and still have flat output if Story
and Spirit are broken.

"Invest more in your managers." — Management behavior is downstream of
conditions. Improving a manager without changing the conditions they are
operating in produces temporary behavioral change and then regression.

"Run an engagement survey to understand the problem." — Surveys measure
how people feel about their situation. They do not identify which condition
is creating that feeling. You cannot diagnose root cause from sentiment data.

"The solution is better communication." — Communication is a symptom as
often as it is a cause. Teams that cannot communicate well usually have
a broken condition upstream of the communication — in meaning, direction,
or coherence — that the communication problem is reflecting.
