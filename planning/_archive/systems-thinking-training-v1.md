# Systems Thinking Training — Full Deck & Facilitator Guide

**Session**: Systems Thinking: Seeing the Whole Picture  
**Duration**: 90 minutes  
**Audience**: Planning and consulting team, beginner-level  
**Spine**: The Four Dimensions Every System Lives In  
**Frameworks covered**: Lean, Root Cause Analysis (5 Whys + Fishbone), Shingo Prize Model  

---

## The Spine: Four Dimensions (backstage vs. onstage)

The session is built on four dimensions that every working system has to satisfy. The **internal architecture** (FourFlow — never spoken aloud) gives the model its coherence. The **audience-facing labels** are plain business language.

| Internal (backstage — never named) | Audience label (onstage) | The question | Anchor frameworks |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SELF** — Reception | **PEOPLE** | Are the people resourced to perform? | Shingo: *Respect Every Individual* · cognitive load · psychological safety |
| **SPACE** — Transmission | **TOOLS & SYSTEMS** | Do the tools and systems support the work? | Lean (waste, tool-fit) · RCA (5 Whys, Fishbone) · feedback loops |
| **STORY** — Direction across time | **DIRECTION** | Are we aimed at the right destination? | Output vs. outcome · problem framing · Shingo: *Create Value* |
| **SPIRIT** — Direction beyond time | **VALUES & VISION** | Do our values and vision match what we're building? | Shingo: *Tools → Systems → Culture* · stated vs. enacted values · constancy of purpose |

**Why this is more cohesive than four disconnected "tips":** the four dimensions are genuinely distinct — *who runs it* (People), *what it runs on* (Tools & Systems), *where it's headed* (Direction), *what it's rooted in* (Values & Vision). They move from the visible and concrete (people you can see) to the invisible and deep (the values that silently drive everything). That arc gives the session its build — and lands the most profound idea (culture is the deepest lever) last.

The three named frameworks don't split evenly into four buckets, and forcing them to would feel artificial. Instead the dimensions are the spine, and each framework is deployed where it genuinely fits: **Lean and RCA both live in SPACE** (they diagnose the tools and the systems), while **Shingo spans the human, directional, and cultural dimensions** because it is a whole-system model by design.

## Through-Line Scenario

> A business area launches a new reporting initiative. Resources are allocated. A tool is purchased. The team attends training. Six months later: the reports exist but no one uses them. The initiative is quietly shelved.

This scenario threads through every module. Each of the four dimensions reveals a *different* failure hiding inside it:
- **People**: the team was already at capacity — no one checked
- **Tools & Systems**: the workflow spanned three platforms; the root cause was an upstream procurement rule
- **Direction**: the initiative aimed at *producing reports* (output) when the real destination was *better decisions* (outcome) — wrong target
- **Values & Vision**: the stated value "data-driven decisions" never matched how decisions were actually made

Use it as-is, or substitute a real challenge from your team.

## Section 2 — What Is a System? (10 min)

---

### Slide 5 — The Definition

**Headline**: A system: connected parts, a shared goal, feedback that keeps it on track

**Talking points**:
- Every system has three elements: components (the parts), relationships (how they connect), and purpose (what they produce)
- A team is a system. A process is a system. Your organization is a system of systems.
- Systems produce *emergent behavior* — outputs no single part could produce alone. A great team isn't just good individuals; it's what happens *between* them.
- This is why fixing one part often doesn't fix the problem — the problem lives in the relationship *between* parts
- Systems thinking is learning to see those relationships

**Visual description**: Minimal flow diagram — three components A, B, C connected by arrows forming a loop, not a line. An arrow from output loops back to input (feedback loop visible). Below: the words *parts* and *between*, with "between" slightly emphasized. Clean white background, simple line art.

---

### Slide 6 — Two Types of Feedback Loop

**Headline**: Systems either amplify or stabilize — feedback decides which

**Talking points**:
- A feedback loop is what happens when a system's output flows back as input, letting it self-adjust
- **Reinforcing loops** amplify: small changes grow. Momentum is a reinforcing loop. So is a death spiral.
- **Balancing loops** stabilize: the system measures output against a target and corrects toward it. A thermostat. Quality control. A project review.
- Most dysfunctional work systems have *broken* feedback loops — the output never gets back to the people who could fix the input
- Ask your team: do we actually review whether last quarter's initiatives worked? If not, the loop is open and the system can't correct.

**Visual description**: Two side-by-side diagrams on a clean background. Left: reinforcing loop — circle with a plus sign, "Momentum builds," upward spiral. Right: balancing loop — circle with a minus sign, "System self-corrects," level line toward a target. Labels: "Growth engine" vs. "Quality control." Clean vector style.

---

### Slide 7 — The Key Insight

**Headline**: Every system is perfectly designed to get the results it gets

**Talking points**:
- This is W. Edwards Deming — an architect of modern quality thinking — and it's the most important idea in this room
- Sit with it: if you're getting frustrating results, the system is working *perfectly*. It's just optimized for something you don't want.
- This does two things. It removes blame from individuals — the person isn't failing, the system is producing exactly what it's built to.
- And it clarifies leverage: if you want different results, you need a different *system* — not harder effort on the same one
- For us: our job with client areas isn't to push harder on the same lever. It's to help them see and redesign the system.

**Visual description**: Centered quote card, clean dark background, white text: *"Every system is perfectly designed to get the results it gets."* Attribution: W. Edwards Deming. No other elements. The quote fills the space.

## Section 4 — PEOPLE (8 min)
*Backstage: SELF — Reception. "Are we clear enough to take this in?"*

---

### Slide 10 — The People Question

**Headline**: Most frameworks forget that systems are run by humans

**Talking points**:
- Every system has humans in it — and humans aren't interchangeable parts. Their capacity fluctuates, attention is finite, and willingness to act depends on conditions you may not have designed for.
- "Resourced to perform" means three things: **Clarity** (do they know what to do and why?), **Capacity** (do they have bandwidth, or are they overloaded?), and **Safety** (do they feel safe to flag problems, ask questions, push back?)
- **Cognitive load** is a real constraint — not about time management, but about how many things compete for finite mental bandwidth. Add a system to people already at capacity and you don't get more output; you get drag on everything.
- **Psychological safety** is a system condition, not a personality trait. Safe teams surface problems early, when they're fixable. Unsafe teams hide them until they're catastrophic.
- Ask yourself: when we help a business area launch something, do we check whether the people who run it are resourced to do so?

**Visual description**: Clean gender-neutral human silhouette with three overlapping circles: Clarity (white), Capacity (amber), Safety (green). Where all three overlap: a small flow/arrow symbol = performance. Remove any one circle and the overlap disappears. Flat design.

---

### Slide 11 — Shingo: Respect Every Individual

**Headline**: The Shingo model makes this its foundation — not for ethics, for results

**Talking points**:
- The Shingo Prize goes to organizations with *sustained* operational excellence — durable improvement that compounds, not just good quarters
- Its foundational principle: **Respect Every Individual**
- This sounds like a values statement. It's also a systems-design statement.
- A system that ignores the humans running it produces variable, unpredictable results — even with perfect tools. People work inconsistently when they're unclear, overloaded, or unsafe.
- In practice: design systems *with* the operators, not *for* them. Ask what they need. Build alongside them.
- The diagnostic this gives us: before we design or implement, did we ask the people who have to run it?

**Visual description**: Simplified Shingo model — a three-tier pyramid (base: Tools, middle: Systems, top: Culture) with "Results" labeled to the side. In the Culture tier, "Respect Every Individual" highlighted. Communicates that everything rests on the human and cultural foundation. Clean, professional.

## Section 5 — TOOLS & SYSTEMS (16 min)
*Backstage: SPACE — Transmission. "Is my context amplifying or degrading the signal?"*

This is the largest dimension because it holds **two** toolkits: **Lean** diagnoses the *tools and environment*, **RCA** diagnoses the *systems and their root causes*. Both answer the same question — does the machinery support the work, or fight it?

---

### Slide 13 — The Tools & Systems Question

**Headline**: Do the tools and systems support the work — or fight it?

**Talking points**:
- This dimension is about everything *around* the people: the tools they use, the workflows they follow, the feedback the system gives back
- Two failures live here. The first is **friction** — tools and environments full of waste everyone has adapted to and stopped noticing. The second is **broken structure** — systems whose root design produces the same failure again and again.
- We have a toolkit for each: **Lean** for the friction, **Root Cause Analysis** for the structure
- The shared question: is the machinery amplifying the work, or degrading it?

**Visual description**: Split-panel slide. Left panel labeled "TOOLS" with a wrench/gear icon and the word "Lean." Right panel labeled "SYSTEMS" with a flow-diagram icon and the words "Root Cause Analysis." A connecting bracket above both reads "Does the machinery support the work?" Clean, balanced.

---

### Slide 14 — Lean: Remove What Fights the Work

**Headline**: Lean — subtract friction before you add anything new

**Talking points**:
- Lean removes **waste** from systems: anything that consumes resources without adding value to the result
- It came from the Toyota Production System, where scarcity forced one question: what is strictly necessary? Everything else is waste.
- Its powerful default: before you add, *subtract*. Before you improve, *simplify*. Before you train people, *remove the friction* that makes the work hard.
- This runs against the org instinct, which is to add — more process, more oversight, more tools, more meetings
- The five wastes most relevant to knowledge work:
  - **Waiting** — delays between steps (approvals, handoffs, unclear ownership)
  - **Over-processing** — more work than the output requires (40 slides when 1 page would do)
  - **Defects** — rework from unclear instructions, shifting requirements, undefined scope
  - **Motion** — unnecessary switching between tools, systems, people
  - **Underutilized talent** — skills the system never deploys (the most invisible waste in knowledge work)

**Visual description**: Top third — the simplified Toyota Production System "house" (roof: Goals; two pillars: Just-in-Time, Built-in Quality; foundation: Stability). Bottom two-thirds — five clean icons in a row: hourglass (Waiting), paper stack (Over-processing), document-with-X (Defects), scattered arrows (Motion), faded lightbulb-person (Underutilized Talent), each with a one-word label. Flat, scannable.

---

### Slide 15 — RCA: The 5 Whys

**Headline**: Don't ask what broke. Ask what system produced this — five times.

**Talking points**:
- Most troubleshooting asks "what happened?" Root Cause Analysis asks "*why* did this happen — and why did *that* happen?"
- A **proximate cause** is what broke. A **root cause** is the system condition that made the break inevitable. Fix only the proximate cause and the root produces the same failure again.
- The 5 Whys: ask why, then why that, five times. Watch the scenario shift:
  - Nobody uses the reports → *Why?* Hard to access and read → *Why?* Format doesn't match how the team already works → *Why?* Tool chosen without input from end users → *Why?* Procurement doesn't require end-user validation → *Why?* The org optimizes for speed of deployment, not adoption
  - **Root cause:** the system rewards *launching* initiatives, not *embedding* them
- Rule for the room: if your Why #4 or #5 names a person, start over. Root causes are system-design decisions, not individual failures.
- The leverage point falls out of the chain: change the *procurement process*, not the training agenda

**Visual description**: Vertical 5-step chain, boxes joined by downward arrows. Top (red): "Nobody uses the reports." Then: "Hard to access/read" → "Format doesn't match workflows" → "Chosen without end-user input" → "Procurement doesn't require validation" → bottom (highlighted green, bold outline): "Org optimizes for deployment speed, not adoption" labeled **Root Cause**. Each arrow tagged "Why?"

---

### Slide 16 — RCA: The Fishbone (when causes tangle)

**Headline**: When one chain of "why" isn't enough, map all the causes at once

**Talking points**:
- The **Fishbone** (Ishikawa) diagram is for messier problems with several contributing causes at once
- The symptom sits at the fish's "head." Six categories branch off the spine: People, Process, Environment, Equipment, Measurement, Management
- Each person adds the causes they see in each category. It surfaces disagreement — different people point to different causes — and gets every theory on the table before anyone defends one
- You don't need to master it today. Remember the use case: when a team keeps arguing about what caused a problem, this maps everyone's theory at once
- Where causes concentrate is where you intervene

**Visual description**: Clean Fishbone diagram. Head (right): "Low adoption of reporting initiative." Six diagonal bones off the spine: People, Process, Environment, Equipment, Measurement, Management — each with 2–3 pre-filled scenario causes (People: "no capacity check"; Process: "3-platform workflow"; Management: "no adoption metric set"). Causes spread across categories = a multi-factor problem.

### Slide 17 — Scenario: The Tools & Systems Diagnosis

**Headline**: The reporting initiative — what did the machinery do to the work?

**Talking points**:
- **Tools (Lean lens):** completing one report meant Tool A → spreadsheet → Tool B → Tool C → email. Three platform switches per artifact (Motion). Approvals needed two managers in different time zones (Waiting). Training taught the tool, never simplified the workflow (Over-processing).
- **Systems (RCA lens):** the 5 Whys took us past the tool to the *procurement process that never required end-user validation* — the real root cause
- The system was designed around the tool's capabilities, not around making the work easy for the people doing it
- **Verdict: No.** The tools and systems actively fought the work.

**Visual description**: Process flow START → Tool A → Spreadsheet → Tool B → Tool C → Email → REPORT, each arrow tagged with a waste type (Motion, Motion, Over-processing). A clock icon labeled "Waiting" at the approval step. Below the flow, a small inset arrow drilling down to a box: "Root cause: procurement process." Red highlights on waste steps.

> "The 5 Whys drilled *down* to find the root cause — that's the systems lens. But you can run the same tool *upward*: 'Why are we doing this at all?' Keep asking and you stop finding causes and start finding *purpose*. That's the next dimension — direction."

## Section 6 — DIRECTION (10 min)
*Backstage: STORY — Direction across time. "What are we building toward?"*

---

### Slide 18 — The Direction Question

**Headline**: You can build the thing right — and still build the wrong thing

**Talking points**:
- The first two dimensions ask whether we're doing the thing *well*. This one asks whether we're doing the *right thing*.
- The most expensive failures aren't sloppy execution — they're flawless execution aimed at the wrong target
- The core distinction: **output vs. outcome**. Output is what you *produce* (reports). Outcome is the *change you're trying to create* (better decisions). Teams optimize outputs and quietly miss outcomes.
- A system pointed at the wrong destination gets *more* lost the harder it works — better tools and resourced people just arrive at the wrong place faster
- Before any initiative, three questions define the destination:
  1. What **outcome** are we actually trying to create? (not "what will we produce?")
  2. How will we **know** we got there? (what does success look like, concretely?)
  3. Is this initiative the **highest-leverage** path to it — or just the most obvious one?

**Visual description**: Two arrows from a single starting point. One short arrow, bold, labeled "OUTPUT — what we produce" pointing at a small box ("reports"). One long arrow, lighter at first then bold, labeled "OUTCOME — the change we want" pointing at a distant target ("better decisions"). Visually shows output as the near, easy target and outcome as the true, distant one. Clean, directional.

---

### Slide 19 — Shingo: Create Value (toward the right destination)

**Headline**: Value is defined by the destination — not by what you produce

**Talking points**:
- The Shingo principle here: **Create Value for the Customer**. Value isn't what you make — it's what the person you're serving actually needs.
- This is the antidote to output-thinking. A report nobody uses has zero value, no matter how well-made. A scrappy summary that changes a decision has enormous value.
- For a planning and consulting team, "the customer" is the business area you serve. Their outcome — not your deliverable — is the destination.
- Two more directional principles worth naming:
  - **Create Constancy of Purpose** — a stable, clear "why" so people at every level can make aligned decisions without being told. When purpose shifts each leadership cycle, nothing durable gets built.
  - **Think Systemically** — every local choice ripples. Aiming one team at its own metric, blind to the next team, sends the whole system off-course.
- Diagnostic for the room: for the initiatives you support, can everyone name the *outcome* — or only the *deliverable*?

**Visual description**: Three horizontal bars, same color family. Each: principle name (bold left) + one-line plain-language meaning (right) + a small diagnostic question beneath in lighter text. "Create Value for the Customer" sits on top, largest. Clean, corporate, readable at scale.

---

### Slide 20 — Scenario: The Direction Diagnosis

**Headline**: The reporting initiative — was it even aimed at the right place?

**Talking points**:
- The initiative's goal was stated as "produce a weekly reporting dashboard." That's an *output*.
- The real need underneath was "help leaders make faster, better-informed decisions." That's the *outcome* — and nobody defined it, measured it, or designed toward it.
- Because the target was the output, success was declared at "dashboard launched" — exactly when the real work (changing how decisions get made) should have *started*
- No success criteria were set, so no one could tell the initiative had missed
- **Verdict: No.** The system was aimed at producing an artifact, not at the outcome the artifact was supposed to create.

**Visual description**: Side-by-side comparison. Left card (what was aimed at): "Output: dashboard launched ✓" with a green check but a faded background. Right card (what mattered): "Outcome: better decisions" with a question mark and a gap between the two cards labeled "Nobody measured this." The check on the left looks hollow next to the unanswered right.

## Section 7 — VALUES & VISION (8 min)
*Backstage: SPIRIT — Direction beyond time. "What stays true regardless of conditions?"*

---

### Slide 21 — The Values & Vision Question

**Headline**: Underneath every system are the values that silently drive it

**Talking points**:
- The Shingo model's deepest insight: tools, systems, and culture are three *levels* of change, with very different durability
  - **Tools level** — implement a new tool or practice. Visible fast, decays fast. The old behavior reasserts once attention moves on.
  - **Systems level** — redesign workflows, feedback loops, accountability. More durable: the system now produces different behavior by default.
  - **Culture level** — change the values and mental models driving behavior. Most durable: people make different choices *even when no one is watching*.
- Most initiatives only work at the Tools level — and decay because the culture that produced the original problem is still running underneath
- This is the deepest lever and the slowest to move. It's also where the most stubborn failures actually live.

**Visual description**: Three-tier pyramid, wide base. Base (gray): "TOOLS — what we use." Middle (blue): "SYSTEMS — how we work." Top (deep purple): "CULTURE — what we believe." To the side, "RESULTS" with an arrow pointing back at all three. A small note at the base: "Most initiatives live here →" pointing at Tools. Conveys depth and durability.

---

### Slide 22 — The Gap: Stated vs. Enacted Values

**Headline**: Every organization has two sets of values — the ones it states and the ones it lives

**Talking points**:
- **Stated values** are on the wall and in the deck. **Enacted values** are what the system actually rewards and repeats.
- When the two diverge, the *enacted* values win every time — because that's what the system is actually built to produce (back to Deming)
- You can't tool your way across this gap. A new dashboard doesn't make a culture data-driven if decisions are still made before the data is opened.
- The final Shingo principle for the room: **Lead with Humility** — the people closest to the work know how it really happens. Systems improve when leaders ask more than they direct. Stop asking and the feedback loop closes.
- The honest diagnostic: name one stated value of an organization you support — then name what it actually rewards. Where's the gap?

**Visual description**: Three-column comparison. Col 1 "Stated Value." Col 2 "Enacted Behavior." Col 3 "What the System Rewards." A red diagonal "gap" line cuts from Col 1 to Col 3, labeled "The Gap." Clean, slightly stark.

---

### Slide 23 — Scenario: The Values & Vision Diagnosis

**Headline**: The reporting initiative — what did the culture silently decide?

**Talking points**:
- Stated value: "We make data-driven decisions."
- Enacted behavior: decisions were made in Tuesday leadership meetings, typically before anyone consulted a report
- The tool was built assuming the culture already consumed data. It didn't. The tool tried to create a behavior the culture didn't reward — and lost.
- No new dashboard changes a culture that decides first and justifies later
- **Verdict: No.** The stated values never matched the enacted ones — and the system rewarded the gap.
- This is why the initiative was doomed before training day: it was a Tools-level fix for a Culture-level problem.

**Visual description**: The stated-vs-enacted gap diagram from Slide 22, now filled with the scenario: "Data-driven decisions" vs. "Decisions made before data consulted" vs. "Meetings reward speed over evidence." The red gap line is widest here. A small tag: "Tools-level fix → Culture-level problem."

## Section 8 — Putting It Together (13 min)

---

### Slide 24 — All Four, Diagnosed

**Headline**: One initiative, four dimensions, four different answers

**Talking points**:
- Recap fast — all four verdicts on the same scenario:
  - **People:** No — no capacity check, no clarity, no safety to flag concerns
  - **Tools & Systems:** No — three-platform friction; root cause was the procurement process
  - **Direction:** No — aimed at an output (a dashboard) instead of an outcome (better decisions)
  - **Values & Vision:** No — stated "data-driven" culture never matched how decisions were actually made
- In a real system these failures *compound*. Fix the tool (Tools & Systems) without the culture (Values) and adoption still dies. Resource the people (People) without fixing the target (Direction) and they execute the wrong thing beautifully.
- The systems thinker's job isn't to fix everything — it's to find the **highest-leverage dimension**. Which one, answered "yes," would shift the most?

**Visual description**: The four-quadrant card with each quadrant filled: question + a red "No" + a one-line diagnosis. A bracket beneath: "These failures are connected — fixing one in isolation rarely holds." A faint glow on one quadrant (Direction) hinting at "highest leverage" for discussion.

---

### Slide 25 — Your Turn

**Headline**: Pick a challenge. Run the four questions.

**Talking points / Facilitator guide**:

**Option A — Whole room** *(best for groups < 15)*:
- Name one shared client challenge right now
- Run the four questions together; capture on a whiteboard or shared doc
- ~12 min: 2 min per question + 4 min synthesis

**Option B — Pairs** *(best for 15–30)*:
- Each pair picks their own current challenge
- 8 min: run the four questions; 4 min: 2–3 pairs share their sharpest diagnosis

**Option C — Individual** *(when energy is low or time is tight)*:
- Fill in the four-question card for your most complex current initiative
- 8 min silent; 3–4 min voluntary shares

**Key facilitation prompts** (any option):
- "Which dimension gets the clearest *No*? What would move it to a *maybe*?"

### Facilitation Notes by Section

**Opening (Slide 2)**: Let the silence after the question sit. Don't answer it. The pause is the hook.

**Scenario (Slide 8)**: Collect instincts *before* running the four questions — write them on a whiteboard. It's more powerful when the room's first instincts turn out to be symptoms, and the four dimensions reveal the deeper causes.

**People (Slides 10–12)**: Cognitive load may get pushback ("aren't they just bad at time management?"). Response: cognitive load isn't about time, it's about how many things compete for finite mental bandwidth. It's a system-design variable, not a personal-productivity one.

**Tools & Systems (Slides 13–17)**: This is the longest stretch — keep energy up. The 5 Whys demo is the centerpiece: read each "why" aloud and slowly, so the room *feels* the answer move from the person to the system. Land the transition note out loud before Direction — the "drill down vs. ladder up" idea is the hinge that makes the whole model feel like one tool, not four.

**Direction (Slides 18–20)**: This is the dimension most likely to be *new* to a planning/consulting team — they're trained to deliver outputs. Spend the energy here. The output-vs-outcome distinction is the single most portable idea in the session; if they remember one thing, make it this.

**Values & Vision (Slides 21–23)**: The session's deepest insight. Slow down. The Tools→Systems→Culture pyramid plus "stated vs. enacted values" usually generates the most honest conversation of the day. Let it breathe — this is the climax, not a wrap-up.

**Putting It Together (Slides 24–25)**: Choose Option A if the room has any energy and the layout allows conversation. Option C is the fallback only. The debrief is where learning compounds — protect the full 4 minutes.

**Common Q&A**:
- *"Is this just another framework to learn?"* — No. The four questions are a *lens*, not a methodology. You're not learning Lean today — you're learning the question Lean is built to answer.
- *"Which dimension matters most?"* — Start with People; if they aren't resourced, nothing else holds. Then Direction; flawless work on the wrong target is the costliest failure. Then Values; culture overrides everything eventually.
- *"Can a system fail all four?"* — Yes. Most systemic failures touch all four, with one as primary. That's where you intervene first.

### Substituting a Real Scenario

To use a real example instead of (or alongside) the reporting initiative:

1. Choose something the whole room knows — not a one-person story
2. Far enough in the past to diagnose without defensiveness, close enough to feel real
3. Walk the same structure: timeline → People → Tools & Systems → Direction → Values & Vision
4. The root cause should point to a *system*, not a person. If it points to a person, run the 5 Whys once more.

## Notes on the FourFlow Spine (for the presenter only — never said aloud)

The four dimensions are FourFlow's four pillars, translated into business language:
- **People** = SELF (Reception) — can the person take the signal in?
- **Tools & Systems** = SPACE (Transmission) — is the environment amplifying or degrading the signal?
- **Direction** = STORY (Direction across time) — what are we building toward?
- **Values & Vision** = SPIRIT (Direction beyond time) — what stays true regardless of conditions?

This is why the model holds together without effort: it isn't four tips bolted onto three frameworks — it's one coherent map of a whole system, with the frameworks slotted in where each genuinely fits. The audience experiences the coherence; they never need the architecture behind it.
