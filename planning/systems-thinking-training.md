## The Architecture: Principles on Top, Models Below

The session teaches **six ideology-neutral principles of systems thinking**, drawn from the real canon — Deming, Ackoff, Meadows, Senge, Beer. No single methodology owns the top layer. The named models (Lean, RCA, Shingo) appear *below* the principles, each as the worked example of the principle it embodies best.

| # | Principle | One-line question | Model deployed |
|---|-----------|-------------------|----------------|
| 1 | **PURPOSE** | What is this system actually producing? | Deming / POSIWID · output vs. outcome |
| 2 | **EMERGENCE** | What happens *between* the parts? | Everyday examples (no formal model needed) |
| 3 | **STRUCTURE** | What structure keeps producing this event? | Root Cause Analysis (5 Whys, Fishbone) |
| 4 | **FLOW** | Where is the flow blocked? | Lean (waste, value stream) |
| 5 | **FEEDBACK** | Does the output ever reach someone who can change the input? | The team's own progress-checking work |
| 6 | **ALIGNMENT** | Are the parts, the purpose, and the values pointing the same way? | Shingo (Tools → Systems → Culture) |

**Why this ordering**: it moves from the most familiar idea (judge a system by results) to the most profound (the deepest layer of a system always wins). Alignment lands last — it is the climax, and it is where holistic thinking lives: a system isn't healthy because each part is healthy; it's healthy because everything points the same way.

**Per-principle rhythm** (every block follows the same four beats):
1. **Name it** — the principle, with an everyday illustration
2. **Prove it** — the model as a worked example
3. **Apply it** — diagnose the through-line scenario
4. **Own it** — 60–90 second canvas beat: *"Where do we already do this? Where's the gap?"*

## The Running Canvas (interactive thread)

A six-row grid lives on a whiteboard or flip chart for the whole session:

| Principle | Where we already do this | Where the gap is |
|-----------|--------------------------|------------------|
| Purpose · Emergence · Structure · Flow · Feedback · Alignment | *(filled live)* | *(filled live)* |

After each principle block, the facilitator takes 60–90 seconds of shout-outs (or sticky notes) for that row. Nobody leaves their seat; momentum stays high. By the end of the session the canvas **is** a systems audit of the team's own practice — existing systems named, gaps visible — and the final exercise builds directly on it. Photograph it before everyone leaves; it's the post-session artifact.

## Through-Line Scenario

> A business area launches a new reporting initiative. Resources are allocated. A tool is purchased. The team attends training. Six months later: the reports exist but no one uses them. The initiative is quietly shelved.

Each of the six principles reveals a *different* failure hiding inside it:
- **Purpose**: aimed at producing reports (output) when the destination was better decisions (outcome)
- **Emergence**: every part worked — tool, training, people — and the whole still failed
- **Structure**: the root cause was an upstream procurement rule, not the tool
- **Flow**: the workflow spanned three platforms; friction everywhere
- **Feedback**: no adoption metric — failure discovered at month 7 instead of week 2
- **Alignment**: the stated value "data-driven decisions" never matched how decisions were made

Use it as-is, or substitute a real challenge (see facilitator guide).

### Slide 2 — Opening Question

**Headline**: Why do smart teams with good tools still fail to improve?

**Talking points**:
- Pose the question directly to the room. Give it 10 seconds of silence.
- You'll hear answers like: the wrong people, not enough resources, unclear priorities, leadership didn't commit
- All of those answers are about *parts*. None are about the *whole*.
- The frustrating truth: almost every failure traces not to a bad person or a bad tool — but to a system designed, intentionally or not, to produce exactly the result it produced
- We're going to change how you see this

**Visual description**: Two images side by side. Left: a single gear spinning smoothly in isolation, functional. Right: three gears that don't mesh — gaps between them, unable to turn together. Minimal, editorial illustration style. The contrast is immediate.

---

### Slide 3 — Holism with Rigor

**Headline**: Systems thinking = holistic thinking + rigor

**Talking points**:
- For three centuries, the dominant way to understand anything has been **analysis**: take it apart, study the pieces. It built the modern world — and it has a blind spot.
- **Holistic thinking** is the opposite stance: the whole is more than the sum of its parts, so you can't understand something only by studying its pieces
- On its own, holism stays vague — "everything is connected" doesn't tell you what to do Monday morning
- **Systems thinking is holism with rigor.** It keeps the stance and adds machinery: purpose, structure, flow, feedback, alignment — things you can actually diagnose
- Russell Ackoff's framing: analysis looks *into* things; synthesis asks what larger whole this is part of, and what role it plays there. Today we practice synthesis.

**Visual description**: Split-screen editorial illustration. Left: a watch fully disassembled into neat labeled parts on a white surface — precise, sterile, lifeless. Right: the same watch assembled and ticking on a wrist in motion. Caption-sized words beneath: "analysis" (left), "synthesis" (right). Clean, minimal, two-tone.

---

### Slide 4 — What a System Is

**Headline**: Connected parts, a shared purpose, behaving as one whole

**Talking points**:
- Every system has three elements: **parts** (components), **connections** (how they relate), and **purpose** (what it produces)
- A team is a system. A process is a system. Your organization is a system of systems.
- The key word is *connected*. When connections break, the parts still work — but the system fails.
- This is why fixing one part so often doesn't fix the problem: the problem lives in the relationships *between* parts
- Six principles govern how systems behave. They're not ours — they come from a century of systems science. Learn the six and you can read any system you walk into.

**Visual description**: Minimal flow diagram — three components A, B, C connected by arrows forming a loop, not a line. Below: the words *parts* and *between*, with "between" emphasized. Clean white background, simple line art.

## Section 2 — Principle 1: PURPOSE (8 min)

---

### Slide 7 — The Principle

**Headline**: Every system is perfectly designed to get the results it gets

**Talking points**:
- This is W. Edwards Deming — an architect of modern quality thinking — and it's the most important sentence in this room
- Sit with it: if you're getting frustrating results, the system is working *perfectly*. It's just optimized for something you don't want.
- Stafford Beer compressed it further: **the purpose of a system is what it does** — not what it says, not what the kickoff deck promised. Judge a system by its results, never its intentions.
- This does two things. It removes blame from individuals — the person isn't failing; the system is producing exactly what it's built to produce.
- And it clarifies leverage: different results require a different *system* — not harder effort on the same one

**Visual description**: Centered quote card, clean dark background, white text: *"Every system is perfectly designed to get the results it gets."* Attribution: W. Edwards Deming. No other elements. The quote fills the space.

---

### Slide 8 — The Working Tool: Output vs. Outcome

**Headline**: Output is what you produce. Outcome is the change you're trying to create.

**Talking points**:
- The fastest way to check a system's purpose: ask whether it's aimed at an **output** or an **outcome**
- Output: reports, dashboards, deliverables, launches. Outcome: better decisions, faster cycles, behavior that actually changed.
- Teams optimize outputs because outputs are visible and countable — and quietly miss outcomes
- A system pointed at the wrong destination gets *more* lost the harder it works: better tools and harder-working people just arrive at the wrong place faster
- Three questions define a destination before any initiative: What outcome are we creating? How will we *know* we got there? Is this the highest-leverage path — or just the most obvious one?

**Visual description**: Two arrows from a single starting point. One short arrow, bold, labeled "OUTPUT — what we produce" pointing at a small box ("reports"). One long arrow, lighter at first then bold, labeled "OUTCOME — the change we want" pointing at a distant target ("better decisions"). The near target looks easy; the distant one looks true. Clean, directional.

---

### Slide 9 — Scenario Verdict: Purpose

**Headline**: The initiative was aimed at an artifact, not a change

**Talking points**:
- Stated goal: "produce a weekly reporting dashboard." That's an *output*.
- The real need underneath: "help leaders make faster, better-informed decisions." That's the *outcome* — and nobody defined it, measured it, or designed toward it.
- Success was declared at "dashboard launched" — exactly when the real work (changing how decisions get made) should have *started*
- Judged by what it did, not what it intended: the system's actual purpose was *launching a tool*. It achieved that purpose flawlessly.
- **Verdict: purpose failure.** Right execution, wrong destination.

**CANVAS BEAT (60–90 sec)**: *"Think of the initiatives we support right now. For which ones can we name the outcome — and for which only the deliverable?"* Capture 2–3 in the Purpose row: one "already do this," one gap.

**Visual description**: Side-by-side comparison. Left card: "Output: dashboard launched ✓" with a green check but a faded background. Right card: "Outcome: better decisions" with a question mark, the gap between them labeled "Nobody measured this." The check on the left looks hollow next to the unanswered right.

## Section 3 — Principle 2: EMERGENCE (8 min)

---

### Slide 10 — The Principle

**Headline**: A system's behavior lives between the parts, not in them

**Talking points**:
- **Emergence**: a system produces behavior that no single part could produce alone
- A traffic jam has no driver causing it — it emerges from a thousand small interactions. No single neuron is conscious. No single musician is the orchestra.
- A great team isn't a collection of great individuals — it's what happens *between* them: trust, handoffs, shared context, the speed of an honest question
- The inverse is the trap: you can staff a project with excellent people, excellent tools, an excellent plan — and the *combination* still fails, because excellence in the parts says nothing about the connections
- Ackoff's classic proof: take the best engine from one car, the best transmission from another, the best brakes from a third — assemble them. The car doesn't run. The parts were never designed *together*.

**Visual description**: Murmuration of starlings forming a single sweeping shape in the sky — thousands of small birds, one unmistakable wave. Minimal text. The image alone carries the idea: no bird is the pattern; the pattern is between them.

---

### Slide 11 — Scenario Verdict: Emergence

**Headline**: Every part worked. The whole still failed.

**Talking points**:
- Run the audit: the tool? Solid product, well-reviewed. The training? Professionally delivered. The people? Competent, experienced. The budget? Approved on time.
- Inspect any single part and you find nothing broken. The failure lived in the *combinations*: tool ↔ existing workflow (didn't mesh), training ↔ daily reality (taught features, not the team's actual cases), reports ↔ decision meetings (never connected at all)
- This is why "which part failed?" was the wrong question from the start — and why the room's first instincts on Slide 6 were mostly parts
- The practical shift: when reviewing an initiative, spend as much time on the *interfaces* — handoffs, integrations, meeting-to-artifact connections — as on the components
- **Verdict: emergence failure.** Nothing was broken; nothing was connected.

**CANVAS BEAT (60–90 sec)**: *"Where do we already manage connections, not just parts — handoffs we design deliberately? And where do we review components but never the interfaces?"* Capture in the Emergence row.

**Visual description**: Five puzzle pieces laid in a row, each individually polished and gleaming, each stamped with a small green check — but with visible gaps between them, unassembled. Beneath, faint outline of the completed picture they never formed. Flat design, generous white space.

## Section 4 — Principle 3: STRUCTURE (10 min)

---

### Slide 12 — The Principle

**Headline**: Recurring events are produced by underlying structure

**Talking points**:
- Systems science distinguishes three depths: **events** (what happened today), **patterns** (what keeps happening), **structure** (what makes it keep happening)
- Most organizations live at the event level — firefighting. The same fire, fought heroically, every quarter.
- The systems thinker's reflex: the second time something happens, stop asking "what broke?" and start asking "what *structure* keeps producing this?"
- A **proximate cause** is what broke. A **root cause** is the system condition that made the break inevitable. Fix only the proximate cause and the structure produces the same failure again — with a different face.
- The model built for this is **Root Cause Analysis**. Two tools, next two slides.

**Visual description**: An iceberg. Above the waterline: a single red warning icon labeled "EVENT." Below, progressively deeper and larger: a layer labeled "PATTERNS" and beneath it a wide foundation labeled "STRUCTURE." Blue tones underwater, clean white above. The depth proportions tell the story.

---

### Slide 13 — The 5 Whys (worked on our scenario)

**Headline**: Ask why five times — and watch the answer move from a person to a system

**Talking points**:
- The 5 Whys: take the event, ask why, then why that, about five layers down. Walk it live, slowly:
  - Nobody uses the reports → *Why?* Hard to access and read → *Why?* Format doesn't match how the team already works → *Why?* Tool chosen without input from end users → *Why?* Procurement doesn't require end-user validation → *Why?* The org rewards *launching* initiatives, not *embedding* them
  - **Root cause:** a structural rule, five layers beneath the visible event
- Notice what happened: the first answers sound like user failure ("they don't open it"). By Why #4 the answer is a *process*. By Why #5 it's an *incentive structure*. That migration from person to system is the whole point.
- Rule for the room: if your Why #4 or #5 names a person, start over. Root causes are system-design decisions.
- And the leverage point falls out of the chain: change the *procurement rule*, not the training agenda

**Visual description**: Vertical 5-step chain, boxes joined by downward arrows. Top (red): "Nobody uses the reports." Then: "Hard to access/read" → "Format doesn't match workflows" → "Chosen without end-user input" → "Procurement doesn't require validation" → bottom (highlighted green, bold outline): "Org rewards launching, not embedding" labeled **Root Cause**. Each arrow tagged "Why?"

---

### Slide 14 — The Fishbone (when causes tangle)

**Headline**: When one chain of "why" isn't enough, map all the causes at once

**Talking points**:
- The **Fishbone** (Ishikawa) diagram handles messier problems — several contributing causes at once
- The symptom sits at the fish's head. Six categories branch off the spine: People, Process, Environment, Equipment, Measurement, Management. Everyone adds the causes they see.
- Its real power is social: it surfaces disagreement — different people point to different causes — and gets every theory on the table *before* anyone defends one
- Where causes concentrate is where you intervene
- You don't need to master it today. Remember the use case: when a team keeps arguing about what caused a problem, this maps everyone's theory at once.

**CANVAS BEAT (60–90 sec)**: *"What's our most repeated fire — the problem we've solved more than twice? Has anyone ever run it past two whys?"* Capture in the Structure row.

**Visual description**: Clean Fishbone diagram. Head (right): "Low adoption of reporting initiative." Six diagonal bones: People, Process, Environment, Equipment, Measurement, Management — each with 2–3 pre-filled scenario causes (People: "no capacity check"; Process: "3-platform workflow"; Management: "no adoption metric set"). Causes spread across categories = a multi-factor problem.

## Section 5 — Principle 4: FLOW (10 min)

---

### Slide 15 — The Principle

**Headline**: Every system exists to move something

**Talking points**:
- Value, information, decisions, approvals, materials — every working system is a channel for *something flowing through it*
- Two consequences. First: a system is only as fast as its **narrowest point** — the bottleneck. Improving anything other than the bottleneck improves nothing.
- Second: everything in the channel either moves the flow or resists it. The resistance has a name — **friction** — and most of it is invisible because everyone has adapted to it.
- The diagnostic move: stop looking at the boxes on the process diagram and follow the *thing that flows* — walk one report, one request, one decision through its actual path, end to end
- The model built for this is **Lean**

**Visual description**: A river running left to right across the slide, wide and smooth — except one narrow rocky gorge where the water churns. Small markers along the banks at other points labeled "improvement effort" pointing at the wide parts; the gorge unmarked. Communicates: effort everywhere except the constraint. Editorial illustration, two-tone.

---

### Slide 16 — Lean: Subtract Before You Add

**Headline**: Lean — remove what fights the work before adding anything new

**Talking points**:
- Lean removes **waste**: anything that consumes resources without adding value to the result. Born in the Toyota Production System, where scarcity forced one question — what is strictly necessary?
- Its powerful default runs against the organizational instinct. Orgs *add*: more process, more oversight, more tools, more meetings. Lean *subtracts first*: before you add, remove; before you improve, simplify; before you train people, remove the friction that makes the work hard.
- The five wastes most relevant to knowledge work:
  - **Waiting** — delays between steps (approvals, handoffs, unclear ownership)
  - **Over-processing** — more work than the output requires (40 slides when 1 page would do)
  - **Defects** — rework from unclear instructions, shifting requirements, undefined scope
  - **Motion** — unnecessary switching between tools, systems, people
  - **Underutilized talent** — skills the system never deploys (the most invisible waste in knowledge work)

**Visual description**: Five clean icons in a row: hourglass (Waiting), paper stack (Over-processing), document-with-X (Defects), scattered arrows (Motion), faded lightbulb-person (Underutilized Talent), each with a one-word label. Above them, a single bold line: "Subtract before you add." Flat, scannable.

---

### Slide 17 — Scenario Verdict: Flow

**Headline**: Follow one report through the system — feel the friction

**Talking points**:
- Walk the actual path of one report: Tool A → export to spreadsheet → reformat → Tool B → Tool C → email to two managers in different time zones → wait → publish
- Tag the wastes live: three platform switches per artifact (**Motion**), two-time-zone approval (**Waiting**), reformatting that adds nothing (**Over-processing**)
- The training taught the tool's features. Nobody ever simplified the workflow. The system was designed around the tool's capabilities — not around making the work easy for the people doing it.
- The bottleneck wasn't skill or motivation. It was friction nobody owned.
- **Verdict: flow failure.** The machinery fought the work, and the work lost.

**CANVAS BEAT (60–90 sec)**: *"Pick one thing that flows through our team — a plan, an approval, a status update. Where does it wait? Where does it switch tools?"* Capture in the Flow row.

**Visual description**: Process flow START → Tool A → Spreadsheet → Tool B → Tool C → Email → REPORT, each arrow tagged with a waste type (Motion, Motion, Over-processing). A clock icon labeled "Waiting" at the approval step. Red highlights on the friction points.

## Section 6 — Principle 5: FEEDBACK (8 min)

---

### Slide 18 — The Principle

**Headline**: Without a closed loop, a system is flying blind

**Talking points**:
- A **feedback loop** is output flowing back as input, letting the system see its own results and adjust
- Two types, opposite jobs. **Reinforcing loops** amplify: small changes grow — momentum, word of mouth, or a death spiral. **Balancing loops** stabilize: measure output against a target, correct toward it — a thermostat, quality control, a project review.
- Here's the uncomfortable part: most dysfunctional work systems don't have *bad* feedback — they have **open loops**. The output never reaches anyone who can change the input.
- The test for any system: does it find out it's failing in *days*, or in *quarters*? Loop speed is the difference between a course correction and a post-mortem.

**Visual description**: Two side-by-side circular diagrams. Left: reinforcing loop — circle of arrows with a plus sign, upward spiral, labeled "amplifies." Right: balancing loop — circle of arrows with a target at center, level line, labeled "self-corrects." Beneath both, a third broken circle with a gap in it, gray, labeled "open loop — the system can't see itself." Clean vector style.

---

### Slide 19 — You Are the Feedback Function (+ scenario verdict)

**Headline**: Checking on progress isn't admin — it's the system's nervous system

**Talking points**:
- Look at what this team already does: you check on initiatives, review progress, ask "is this working?" In systems terms, *you are the balancing loop* for the business areas you support. That's not overhead — it's the most systemic work in the building.
- Which makes loop *design* your craft. Three questions sharpen any loop: Does the signal reach someone who can act? (a report nobody reads is an open loop) — How fast does it cycle? (annual reviews can't correct monthly drift) — Does anything actually change when it fires? (a loop that triggers no action is decoration)
- Now the scenario: no adoption metric was ever set. Usage sat at 10% for *three months* and no one was looking. The failure was discovered at month 7 — at shelving time — instead of week 2, when one usage report would have triggered a fix.
- **Verdict: feedback failure.** The loop wasn't broken; it was never built.

**CANVAS BEAT (60–90 sec)**: *"Which of our check-ins genuinely changes what happens next — and which are reporting theater? Where is there no loop at all?"* Capture in the Feedback row.

**Visual description**: The Month 1–7 scenario timeline returns, with a heartbeat/EKG line running above it — flat (no signal) from Month 3 onward, with a ghosted annotation at Month 4: "one usage report here = correctable" versus the red X at Month 7: "discovered here = post-mortem." Muted palette, one red accent.

## Section 7 — Principle 6: ALIGNMENT (10 min) — the climax

---

### Slide 20 — The Principle

**Headline**: Every part can be excellent — and the whole still pulls apart

**Talking points**:
- The final principle is the deepest, and it's where everything today converges
- **Alignment**: the parts, the purpose, and the values all pointing the same way. Not the same thing as quality — a system can be full of excellence and still tear itself apart.
- A rowing eight of strong rowers, out of sync, loses to a weaker crew in perfect time. The boat doesn't average the rowers; it gets their *coherence*.
- Ackoff named the failure mode **suboptimization**: optimize each part against its own metric, and you can make the whole *worse*. One team hits its numbers by pushing cost onto the next team. Every department wins; the organization loses.
- Deming's antidote: **constancy of purpose** — a stable, clear "why" that lets people at every level make aligned decisions without being told. When purpose shifts every leadership cycle, nothing durable gets built.
- This is holistic thinking made practical: stop asking "is each part good?" and ask "is everything pointing the same way?"

**Visual description**: Two rowing eights viewed from above, side by side on dark water. Left boat: eight powerful oars frozen at conflicting angles, wake chaotic, boat slightly turned. Right boat: eight oars in identical position, one clean wake, boat arrow-straight and ahead. No text needed beyond a thin caption: "same strength, different system."

---

### Slide 21 — Shingo: Tools → Systems → Culture

**Headline**: Three levels of change — and the deepest one always wins

**Talking points**:
- The Shingo Prize goes to organizations with *sustained* operational excellence — improvement that compounds over decades, not good quarters. Its central insight is about depth:
  - **Tools level** — adopt a new tool or practice. Visible fast, decays fast. The old behavior reasserts the moment attention moves on.
  - **Systems level** — redesign workflows, loops, accountability. More durable: the system now produces different behavior by default.
  - **Culture level** — the values and beliefs actually driving behavior. Most durable: people make different choices *even when no one is watching*.
- Most initiatives only ever work at the Tools level — and decay, because the culture that produced the original problem is still running underneath
- Alignment in Shingo's terms: all three levels pointing the same way. A tool that contradicts the culture loses to the culture. Every time.

**Visual description**: Three-tier pyramid, wide base. Base (gray): "TOOLS — what we use." Middle (blue): "SYSTEMS — how we work." Top (deep purple): "CULTURE — what we believe." To the side, "RESULTS" with an arrow pointing back at all three. A small note at the base: "Most initiatives live here →" pointing at Tools. Conveys depth and durability.

---

### Slide 22 — Stated vs. Enacted (+ scenario verdict)

**Headline**: Every organization has two sets of values — the ones it states and the ones it lives

**Talking points**:
- **Stated values** are on the wall and in the deck. **Enacted values** are what the system actually rewards and repeats. When they diverge, the enacted values win every time — because that's what the system is built to produce. (Hear the echo of Slide 7: the purpose of a system is what it *does*.)
- The scenario's stated value: "We make data-driven decisions." The enacted behavior: decisions were made in Tuesday leadership meetings — typically *before* anyone consulted a report.
- The tool assumed a culture that consumed data. That culture didn't exist. The tool tried to create a behavior the culture didn't reward — and lost. A Tools-level fix for a Culture-level problem.
- **Verdict: alignment failure** — and notice it was the *fatal* one. Fix everything else and this alone still kills the initiative.
- One more Shingo principle to carry out of the room: **Lead with Humility** — the people closest to the work know how it really happens. Systems improve when leaders ask more than they direct.

**CANVAS BEAT (60–90 sec)**: *"Name one stated value of an organization we support — then name what its system actually rewards. Where's the gap?"* Capture in the Alignment row. This beat usually generates the most honest conversation of the day — let it run slightly long if it's alive.

**Visual description**: Three-column comparison filled with the scenario: "Stated: data-driven decisions" / "Enacted: decisions made before data is consulted" / "Rewarded: speed over evidence." A red diagonal gap line cuts across, labeled "the gap — where initiatives go to die." Slightly stark, the visual climax of the deck.

## Section 8 — Integration (12 min)

---

### Slide 23 — One Initiative, Six Failures

**Headline**: Six principles, six different answers — all hiding in one initiative

**Talking points**:
- Recap fast, pointing at each:
  - **Purpose**: aimed at an output (dashboard) instead of an outcome (better decisions)
  - **Emergence**: every part worked; nothing was connected
  - **Structure**: root cause was a procurement rule five "whys" beneath the surface
  - **Flow**: three-platform friction nobody owned
  - **Feedback**: no adoption loop — failure invisible for months
  - **Alignment**: stated "data-driven" values never matched enacted behavior — the fatal one
- In a real system these failures *compound*: fix the flow without the culture and adoption still dies; close the feedback loop on the wrong purpose and you efficiently correct toward the wrong target
- The systems thinker's job isn't to fix all six — it's to find the **leverage point**: the one principle where a fix unlocks the most. (Here, arguably Purpose: aimed at the real outcome, half the other failures become visible immediately.)
- Compare this list to the room's first instincts from Slide 6, still written on the canvas edge. That distance — instinct to diagnosis — is what you've learned in the last hour.

### Slide 26 — The Vocabulary We Now Share

**Headline**: Eighteen terms. One shared lens.

**Talking points**:
- Walk through fast — don't define each, just name them
- The value isn't memorizing definitions; it's having a *word* for something you've already lived
- When you say "this is an output-vs-outcome problem" or "that loop is open" in a client meeting, everyone on the team knows exactly what you mean
- Shared language is itself a systems upgrade — it cuts the translation cost in every conversation

**Vocabulary list** (on slide):

| Term | One-line definition |
|------|-------------------|
| System | Connected parts working toward a shared purpose |
| Synthesis | Understanding something by its role in a larger whole (vs. analysis) |
| Emergence | Behavior no single part could produce alone |
| Purpose (POSIWID) | The purpose of a system is what it does — judge by results |
| Output | What you produce |
| Outcome | The change you're actually trying to create |
| Event / Pattern / Structure | Three depths — what happened, what recurs, what produces it |
| Proximate cause | What broke — the visible symptom |
| Root cause | The system condition that made the failure inevitable |
| 5 Whys | Drilling through layers of "why" until the answer is a system |
| Fishbone | Multi-factor cause mapping across six categories |
| Waste | Anything consuming resources without adding value |
| Bottleneck | The narrowest point — the only place improvement counts |
| Feedback loop | Output flowing back as input, enabling self-correction |
| Reinforcing / Balancing | Loops that amplify vs. loops that stabilize |
| Open loop | Output that never reaches anyone who can change the input |
| Suboptimization | Optimizing parts against their own metrics, worsening the whole |
| Stated vs. enacted values | What we say vs. what the system rewards |

Canvas beats are *inside* each principle block's time — they are the last 60–90 seconds of each block, not additions.

### Running the Canvas

- Set up the six-row grid before the session (whiteboard or flip chart). Rows pre-labeled with the six principles; columns: "Where we already do this" / "Where the gap is."
- During each beat, *you* scribe — don't pass the marker. Two or three entries per row is plenty; cut the beat at 90 seconds even mid-energy (scarcity keeps it alive).
- If a beat stalls, seed it with one example from their actual work, then ask "what else?"
- The canvas is deliberately imperfect — it's raw material for Slide 24, not a polished artifact. Photograph it at the end and circulate with the card.

### Facilitation Notes by Section

**Opening (Slide 2)**: Let the silence after the question sit. Don't answer it. The pause is the hook.

**Holism slide (Slide 3)**: Don't linger philosophically — one strong beat ("holism with rigor") and move. The session *demonstrates* the idea; it doesn't need to argue it.

**Scenario (Slide 6)**: Collect instincts *before* the principles — write them on the canvas edge. The payoff comes at Slide 23 when the room measures the distance between first instincts and the six diagnoses.

**Purpose (7–9)**: The Deming quote is the anchor of the whole session — read it aloud, slowly, once. POSIWID lands best as a punchline, not a lecture.

**Emergence (10–11)**: The lightest block — keep it visual and quick. The starling murmuration and the Ackoff car are the whole teaching. If pressed for time, this block compresses best.

**Structure (12–14)**: The 5 Whys demo is the centerpiece of the middle hour — read each "why" aloud and slowly, so the room *feels* the answer migrate from the person to the system. The Fishbone is a flyby: use case, not mastery.

**Flow (15–17)**: Walk the Slide 17 report path physically if the room allows — point across the room for each tool switch. Friction is kinesthetic; let them feel the zigzag.

**Feedback (18–19)**: Slide 19 is the team's mirror — "you are the balancing loop" reframes their progress-checking work as the most systemic thing they do. Watch the room here; this is often where the session becomes *theirs*. Don't rush the reframe.

**Alignment (20–22)**: The climax. Slow down. The rowing image, the pyramid, and stated-vs-enacted usually generate the most honest conversation of the day. The canvas beat here can run long if it's alive — borrow from the buffer, not from Integration.

**Integration (23–25)**: Protect this block — it's where learning compounds. If time is short, compress Slide 23's recap (it's a reminder, not a re-teach) and protect the pairs work in Slide 24.

**Common Q&A**:
- *"Is this just another framework to learn?"* — No. The six questions are a *lens*, not a methodology. You're not learning Lean today — you're learning the question Lean was built to answer.
- *"Which principle matters most?"* — Start with Purpose: aimed wrong, everything else optimizes the wrong thing. End with Alignment: whatever the deepest layer rewards eventually wins. The middle four tell you where to look in between.
- *"Can a system fail all six?"* — Yes — our scenario did. Most systemic failures touch several, with one as primary. Finding the primary one is the diagnostic skill.
- *"Isn't this all just common sense?"* — Individually, yes. The discipline is checking all six *every time* — common sense applied uncommonly systematically. That's what the card is for.

### Substituting a Real Scenario

To use a real example instead of (or alongside) the reporting initiative:
1. Choose something the whole room knows — not a one-person story
2. Far enough in the past to diagnose without defensiveness, close enough to feel real
3. Walk the same structure: timeline → six principles in order
4. The root cause should point to a *system*, not a person. If it points to a person, run the 5 Whys once more.

## Notes for the Presenter Only (never said aloud)

The six principles are genuine systems-science canon — every one is honestly attributable (Deming, Beer, Ackoff, Senge, Meadows, Shingo). Nothing here is borrowed from anywhere that needs hiding. That said, the session quietly carries a worldview worth knowing you hold:

- **Alignment as the climax** is the holistic thesis: a system is healthy when everything points the same way — coherence, not component quality, is the mechanism. The ordering of the deck (visible → deep, mechanics → values) embodies it.
- **"The deepest layer always wins"** (Slide 21–22) is the bridge concept: culture beneath systems beneath tools. Anyone who internalizes it is one step from asking what sits beneath culture in a *person* — values, vision, story. That conversation can happen after the session, one-on-one, if someone walks up and asks. Let them ask.
- **The team-as-loop reframe** (Slide 19) is deliberately the emotional hook for *this* audience — it dignifies their existing work in systems language. It's also true.
- If the session lands, the natural sequel is a 60-minute follow-up: "the six questions applied to one live initiative, as a working session." Offer it only if asked.
