Why Spartans, Samurai, and Billionaires All Did This One Thing
Throughout history, the world's top performers have known something most of us don't.
From Spartan warriors to modern billionaires, there's something they do that powers their success.
It's not strength, status, money, or even genius.
It's something you can tap into immediately to free up your time, upgrade your cognition, and, as a bonus, it's one of the only performance hacks that will save you money.
I'm Rian Doris. I've trained thousands of professionals — including teams at Audi, Facebook, and the US Air Force — on how to increase productivity and access flow state on demand.

I. THE PATTERN ACROSS HISTORY

The Spartans owned little, focusing instead on the mastery of their martial skills. With minimal resources, a few hundred Spartans held off thousands of Persian soldiers at Thermopylae.

The Stoics — Seneca and Marcus Aurelius — eschewed the trappings of wealth in favor of a life centered around inner virtue and simplicity. Their philosophy allowed them to maintain composure and effective decision-making even in the face of extreme stress and adversity.

The Samurai of feudal Japan adhered to bushido, a code that celebrated frugality and discipline. Miyamoto Musashi won over 60 duels. Monastic traditions worldwide — Christian monks to Buddhist hermits — demonstrate lives of simplicity dedicated to higher pursuits. Thomas Aquinas' "Summa Theologica" was accomplished in the solitude of a monastic environment.

The Mongols were nomads who owned little more than weapons, food, and portable yurt homes. This allowed them to strike enemies like lightning — eventually forming the largest land empire of the time, four times the size of Rome's.

The unifying pattern across these wildly differing contexts: the intentional prioritization of what's most valued and the removal of everything that distracts from it. Not deprivation — radically excluding everything except the essential. This philosophy is called minimalism.

II. THE COST OF OWNERSHIP

[Morning routine scenario: searching for favorite shirt in cluttered closet, rummaging for charger among unrecognized cables, impulse orders to return, scratched watch consuming 30 minutes of mental attention, surfboards collecting dust as reminders of time poorly spent.]

"Thinking about things" is the real cost of ownership. Possessions get lost, or damaged, or have to be returned or maintained, or become an obstacle between you and some other, more important target of your attention.

III. THE SCIENCE OF COGNITIVE LOAD

John Sweller, psychologist at the University of New South Wales, spent his career understanding how our cognitive system handles information. His research unveiled cognitive load: the amount of information we're holding in working memory at any given time.

Think of your brain like a computer. The higher the cognitive load, the more RAM is used up. Your brain gets slower, your ability to learn plummets, and your attention gets whipsawed. You're left with fewer attentional resources (focus, decision-making, creativity) to devote to your priorities.

The brain contains roughly 86 billion neurons, each capable of forming thousands of synaptic connections. But there's a hard limit to how many can fire simultaneously. Neurotransmitters like glutamate (accelerator) and GABA (brake) move along neural pathways — too much traffic, and everything jams. The prefrontal cortex — your cognitive command center for working memory, focus, and decision-making — has finite capacity. Overload it, and efficiency collapses.

The brain is 2% of your body mass but consumes 20% of its energy. There's a ceiling to how much processing it can sustain before performance degrades.

Every misplaced shirt, every scratched watch, every borrowed item not returned — these unnecessarily spike cognitive load. A constant background of noise, irrelevant to your goals, overloading your attention.

IV. POSSESSIONS AND FLOW STATE

Flow can only arise when all of our attention is focused on the present moment. Flow triggers increase focusing chemicals (dopamine and norepinephrine) or reduce cognitive load. Possessions actively block this — they possess the mind, increasing cognitive load to the extent it suppresses flow state.

For every possession you own, some increment of your attention is captured. Each possession represents a piece of information the brain needs to process, requiring attentional resources for recognition, categorization, and decision-making, thereby contributing to cognitive load.

The Attention Math:
- Pants: 10 seconds/day
- iPad: 2 minutes/day (120 seconds)
- Rolex: 10 minutes/day (600 seconds)
- Mansion/Boat: 1 hour/day (3,600 seconds)
Total: 4,330 seconds = ~1.2 hours/day = 300+ hours/year = approximately 14 days of continuous waking attention for just four items.

The average number of possessions in a typical American household is often cited as 300,000 items.

Csikszentmihalyi: "The problem is that the more the ego becomes identified with symbols outside the self the more vulnerable it becomes... the sudden loss of one's possessions results in a 'shrinkage of our personality, a partial conversion of ourselves to nothingness.' To prevent its annihilation, the ego forces us to be constantly on the watch for anything that might threaten the symbols on which it relies... which distorts reality so as to make it congruent with the needs of the ego."

V. HOW POSSESSIONS KILL CREATIVITY

This increase in cognitive load doesn't just zap away your time and hijack the resources you need for productivity and flow. It also clogs up your creativity, by degrading the quality of your rumination.

Rumination occurs courtesy of the Default Mode Network in the brain, which is active during your idle moments. Think of your Default Mode Network as a garden where you harvest your best ideas. Each possession you own is like a weed in this garden. Gradually, these weeds overtake the garden, leaving little space for the flowers and plants of creative thought, problem-solving, and personal reflection.

Csikszentmihalyi: "...the acquisition and maintenance of objects can easily fill up a person's life, until there is no time to do anything else, not even to use the things that are exhausting all of one's psychic energy. When such a pass is reached, the adaptive value of objects is reversed; instead of liberating psychic activity, the things bind it to useless tasks. The former tool turns its master into its slave."

Minimalism drives flow because you reduce cognitive load and free up mental capacity. Flow drives minimalism — the more flow you get, the more you do the work for its own sake, the less stuff you want. In short, minimalism leads to maximalism in performance.

VIII. HOW TO APPLY MINIMALISM FOR FLOW

Step 1: Determine Your Minimalist Sweet Spot

Tier 1 — Aggressive Minimalism: optimize for performance above all. Own only what's essential for your work and goals. Everything is a tool, a means to an end — never an end in itself. (Example: Doris lived this for three years — entire life in a single backpack, mornings seamless, straight into work or adventure.)

Tier 2 — Tempered Minimalism: balance minimalism with practical living. Possessions chosen for both comfort and flow, but flow wins when they conflict. A small, efficiently organized home. Multi-functional furniture. A few well-loved items that enhance daily life.

Tier 3 — Mild Minimalism: not a minimalist, not a maximalist — but conscious about possessions and their cognitive cost. Each item chosen with care. Winston Churchill landed here: deep appreciation for art, literature, and luxury — but deliberate about what he owned.

No ideal tier — it depends on your values and goals. But the question acknowledges the cost of ownership.

Step 2: Do a Possession Purge

Set aside one full day. Remove absolutely everything from your room except the bed and couch. Keep the 15% you actually need. Have someone get rid of the rest.

Process: gather everything you own into one room so you can see it all. Sort into keep or cut. Cut pile: donate/gift, trash/recycle, or sell.

Filter for Aggressive Minimalists: "Is this a tool that advances my craft and professional pursuit?" Yes = keep. No = cut.

Filter for Less Aggressive Minimalists: "What am I assuming I'll get from this... and is that worth the cost of ownership?" If it's not an obvious yes, it's a cognitive or emotional possession staking claim to your attention. The more considerations that arise, the higher the cost of ownership.

Step 3: Maintaining Minimalism

For performance maximalists: one-in-one-out. Get rid of something for everything you bring in.

For less extreme: filter question for new acquisitions: "Is acquiring this new possession worth the temporary neurochemical reward it brings?" Distinguish pleasure (temporary dopamine hit from acquisition — doesn't lead to increased skills, growth, or a better future) from enjoyment (through flow state — does).

Backup: annual purge. Re-run the possession purge once per year as a recalibration.

Csikszentmihalyi: "The organization of the household can be seen as a pattern of attention and intention made concrete in the artifacts and the ambiance they create; a pattern that, in turn, channels the psychic energy of the inhabitants. The household objectively represents what the self is in terms of what things psychic energy has been invested in — what we consider significant to possess. If examined closely, it can reveal the patterns of attention that help to structure our everyday consciousness."
