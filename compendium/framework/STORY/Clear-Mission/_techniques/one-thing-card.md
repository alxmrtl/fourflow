# One Thing Card

> The constraint of a single card forces genuine prioritization — you can't write two things on it.

## Mechanism
Prioritization lists are cognitively deceptive: a ranked list of five items still holds all five items in the attentional field. The top item is nominally first, but the presence of items two through five generates ongoing competition for processing resources. Research on attentional capacity (Lavie's load theory) demonstrates that high-perceptual-load conditions reduce distractor interference — but a ranked list creates the opposite: all items are visible and therefore all compete.

The single-item constraint forces a qualitatively different cognitive operation. Choosing one thing from a list is additive selection; reducing to a single item requires genuine exclusion — deciding what is NOT the priority. The forced exclusion activates a different prefrontal process (suppression rather than selection) and produces a clearer priority signal.

The physical card — whether actual cardstock or a digital equivalent occupying dedicated screen real estate — externalizes the priority into the perceptual field. Working memory has limited capacity; an externalized priority offloads the "remember what matters most" operation from working memory to the environment, freeing cognitive resources for the work itself. The card functions as a persistent environmental cue that re-anchors attention when drift begins.

## Protocol
1. At the start of each day or session, take one index card (physical preferred) or open a single text file.
2. Write one sentence: "Today, the only thing that matters is ___." The sentence must name a specific output or outcome — not a project name or category.
3. Place the card in your primary visual field — propped against your monitor, taped to the wall at eye level, pinned to the top of your screen.
4. If you find yourself writing a second thing on the card: stop. That means the first item wasn't actually singular — it was two things combined. Decompose and choose one.
5. At session end, the card either gets a checkmark (done) or a note on why it wasn't the right priority (to inform tomorrow's card).
6. Archive the cards weekly — they become a record of what actually mattered.

## When to Use
- Daily, as a standing practice during any sustained project phase
- When "I don't know where to start" paralysis has been recurring — the card forces a choice that resolves the paralysis
- When a day has multiple legitimate priorities and the absence of a forced choice is causing the day to fragment

## Key Insight
> "A list is a menu. A card is a commitment."
