# Outcome Horizon Setting

> Bounds attention to an appropriate timeframe.

## How It Enables Flow

The outcome horizon is how far ahead you're shining your attention. Too short (next five minutes only) and you lose direction — each action is disconnected from purpose. Too long (five years from now) and you lose presence — each action feels insignificant against the scale of the vision. The right horizon bounds attention to a timeframe where action and meaning overlap.

When the horizon is well-set, the aperture finds its natural width: wide enough to see where you're going, narrow enough to act precisely right now. This is the STORY-SELF interface — direction (STORY) calibrated to capacity (SELF). Too much horizon overwhelms the lens; too little horizon leaves the flashlight sweeping aimlessly.

When absent, attention either collapses to the immediate (reactivity, firefighting, no arc) or disperses to the distant (dreaming, planning, no execution). Both states generate time distortion: reactive time drags because each moment is disconnected; fantasy time evaporates because you're not actually acting. The well-set horizon produces the time perception of flow — hours feeling like minutes because direction and action are fused.

## Techniques & Applications

### The Aperture-Horizon Calibration (Source: What Time Actually Is)

If time is the frame rate of your perceptual aperture, then the outcome horizon is the *focal length* — how deep into the structure you're looking with each frame. A short focal length shows high detail, low context (you see the grain of the wood but not the shape of the table). A long focal length shows low detail, high context (you see the whole room but can't read the book on the desk).

**The calibration principle**: Match your horizon to your current aperture width. When the aperture is narrow (stressed, fatigued, emotionally dysregulated), shorten the horizon — "what's the next action?" is enough. When the aperture is wide (rested, clear, emotionally stable), extend the horizon — connect today's work to the quarterly arc. Forcing a long horizon through a narrow aperture produces anxiety. Forcing a short horizon through a wide aperture produces boredom.

**Practice**:
1. Check your current state (SELF keys): Am I emotionally regulated? Physically present? Mentally clear?
2. If yes to most: set the horizon to the session or the week — connect what you're doing now to where it leads.
3. If no to most: collapse the horizon to the next action only — "what is the single next thing?" Trust that direction will re-emerge when the aperture widens.
4. Adjust dynamically. The horizon isn't fixed for the day — it breathes with your state.

**Key insight**: The right outcome horizon isn't a planning decision — it's an aperture decision. How far can you see clearly right now?

### The Presentist-Eternalist Toggle (Source: What Time Actually Is + Generative Story)

The Generative Story overview already describes presentism (only now is real) and eternalism (all moments coexist) as narrative tools. The aperture frame makes this a practical toggle for horizon-setting:

- **Presentist mode** (narrow aperture, high detail): Use when executing. The horizon collapses to this moment, this task, this rep. Time is generated by your action. Each tick matters.
- **Eternalist mode** (wide aperture, high context): Use when orienting. The horizon expands to see the whole arc. Time flattens — past, present, and future coexist. You can see which trajectory you're on and whether it leads where you want.

**The toggle practice**: Begin each session in eternalist mode (2-3 minutes reviewing the arc, connecting today's work to the vision). Then toggle to presentist mode for execution (full absorption in the immediate). End the session with a brief eternalist check (does what I did still fit the arc?). This sandwich structure sets and releases the horizon cleanly.

**Key insight**: Eternalism is for navigation. Presentism is for action. The toggle between them IS the practice of outcome horizon setting.
