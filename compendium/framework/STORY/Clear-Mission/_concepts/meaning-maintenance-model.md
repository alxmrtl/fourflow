# Meaning Maintenance Model

> Heine et al.'s framework: humans have a fundamental need for the sense that their actions matter and connect to something larger. When this need is threatened, the mind compensates by finding meaning elsewhere — draining focus from the primary mission.

## What It Is

The Meaning Maintenance Model (MMM) was developed by Steven Heine and colleagues (Heine, Proulx, and Vohs, 2006) as a theoretical framework for understanding how people respond to threats to meaning. The model's central claim: humans have a fundamental need for meaning — the sense that their experiences, actions, and existence are significant and connected to a coherent, comprehensible framework. This need is as basic as the needs for food and affiliation; its chronic deprivation is psychologically destabilizing.

The model's distinctive contribution is its account of what happens when meaning is threatened — through failure, absurdity, randomness, or the sense that one's actions have no consequence. The system does not simply tolerate the absence; it compensates. People will seek and affirm meaning in adjacent domains to restore the sense that something matters, even when the compensation is unrelated to the original threat. Heine and colleagues demonstrated this in a series of studies showing that meaning threats in one domain (e.g., being told one's personality test results were random) increased investment in meaning in entirely different domains (e.g., affiliation, nationalism, or aesthetic preferences).

This compensation dynamic has a critical cost: the meaning-seeking energy that should be concentrated on the primary mission disperses across whatever meaning sources are available. The mind is busy managing the deficit.

## Why It Enables Flow

Consequential mission directly addresses the meaning-maintenance need by ensuring that the primary objective is legibly significant — connected to something larger, with stakes that matter, and with outcomes that feel important. When this is established, the compensation dynamic is unnecessary: the meaning need is being met by the work itself.

Flow research consistently shows that autotelic experience — intrinsically motivated engagement — is the natural result of activities that feel significant. The MMM explains the mechanism from the opposite direction: when significance is absent or threatened, cognitive resources are consumed by meaning-compensation rather than available for flow. A mission whose consequences are genuinely felt satisfies the meaning need at source, preventing the drain.

## Application

Audit missions regularly for meaning-saturation: does this objective connect to something larger in a way that is felt rather than merely stated? The diagnostic is whether the compensation behaviors have appeared — fragmented focus, unexpected investment in peripheral activities, difficulty prioritizing, increased distraction. These are often signs that the primary mission has lost its felt significance and the system is compensating. The intervention is not discipline; it is re-establishing why this matters, specifically and concretely, so the meaning need is being met at the mission level rather than distributed across substitutes.
