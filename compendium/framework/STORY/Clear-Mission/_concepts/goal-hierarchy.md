# Goal Hierarchy

> The cascade structure that connects vision to daily action — each level (someday → 5-year → 1-year → quarter → month → week → today) serving the one above it, so every task has a legible place in the larger arc.

## What It Is

A goal hierarchy is a nested structure of objectives across multiple timeframes, where each level's goals are defined by their contribution to the level above. The structure runs from the timeless (vision, purpose) through the long-term (5-year milestones) down through medium-term (quarterly, monthly) to the operational (weekly, daily, right now).

The critical requirement: the cascade must be explicit and traceable. If today's task cannot be connected through a chain of reasoning to the long-term vision, it either shouldn't be on today's list, or the hierarchy has a missing link that needs to be defined.

The hierarchy solves two distinct problems: (1) the direction problem — you know where you're going at every timescale, so decision-making at each level is constrained to what serves the level above; and (2) the momentum problem — completing tasks at lower levels generates the dopamine-rewarded sense of progress that sustains engagement through long projects whose ultimate rewards are distant.

## Why It Enables Flow

Flow requires clear goals — one of Csikszentmihalyi's nine defining conditions. But "clear goals" at the wrong timescale doesn't solve the problem: knowing your five-year vision doesn't tell you what to do this afternoon. The hierarchy provides clarity at every level simultaneously. Today's task is clear. Its relationship to this week is clear. This week's relationship to the month is clear. All the way up. This multi-level clarity eliminates the decision overhead that consumes attentional bandwidth and prevents flow entry.

## Application

Build the hierarchy top-down once, then maintain it weekly. The weekly strategy session (one hour, same time, before the week starts): review the goal stack, assess current position, set the three weekly priorities that serve the monthly targets. The daily power-down (five minutes at end of day): review tomorrow's tasks against the weekly priorities, prep for next-day execution. These two rituals keep the hierarchy operational rather than aspirational.
