# Constraint Satisfaction

> The cognitive science framework: many cognitive problems are constraint satisfaction problems — solutions must simultaneously satisfy multiple constraints. When constraints are clear and bounded, the solution space is defined and search becomes efficient. Unbounded problems drain resources.

## What It Is

Constraint satisfaction is a foundational framework in cognitive science, artificial intelligence, and problem-solving research. A constraint satisfaction problem (CSP) is defined by a set of variables, their possible values (domains), and a set of constraints that limit which combinations of values are acceptable. The task is to find an assignment of values to variables that simultaneously satisfies all constraints.

The framework was formalized in AI research by Montanari (1974) and developed extensively by Mackworth, Dechter, and others. Its significance for cognitive science, developed through work by researchers including Rumelhart and McClelland on parallel distributed processing, is that human cognition appears to operate on broadly similar principles: the mind searches for interpretations, decisions, or solutions that are maximally consistent with available constraints. When constraints are dense and well-defined, search is fast and efficient. When constraints are sparse or absent, the search space is vast and the cognitive cost of search rises sharply.

The key insight for applied cognition: an unbounded problem is not a free problem — it is a maximally expensive one. Without constraints, every possible approach must be considered, evaluated, and compared. The decision space is infinite, the evaluation criterion is unclear, and cognitive resources are consumed in search rather than in production. Constraints are not restrictions on creative problem-solving; they are the definition of the problem space that makes directed search possible at all.

## Why It Enables Flow

Bounded mission converts an open problem into a constraint satisfaction problem. By specifying what counts as success (the goal constraint), the timeframe (the temporal constraint), and the resources available (the operational constraints), a session boundary transforms an unbounded creative or productive space into a defined search problem. The brain can search this space efficiently precisely because the constraints are clear.

Flow's neurological signature — effortful engagement without self-conscious monitoring — is the phenomenological result of efficient, constraint-guided processing. When the problem is well-constrained, the processing resources available for the work itself are maximized; monitoring and evaluation overhead is minimized. Bounded mission is the structural condition that enables this efficient processing mode.

## Application

Before a work session, explicitly state three constraints: (1) the output constraint — what specifically counts as done; (2) the time constraint — when this session ends; and (3) the scope constraint — what is explicitly out of scope for this session. The scope constraint is often the most neglected: naming what you are not doing this session is as important as naming what you are, because it eliminates the consideration of adjacent work from the active search space. Three explicit constraints define a bounded problem. Bounded problems are solvable. Unbounded ones are not — they simply exhaust.
