# Bounded
*The Maintain quality of Clear Mission*

## What it is

When this quality is present, action and meaning overlap. The horizon is calibrated — what you're aiming at is close enough to feel real and far enough to feel directional. Sessions have weight because each one connects visibly to a larger arc, without requiring that arc to be resolved before action is possible. When absent, the horizon is either too close or too distant. Too close: actions feel disconnected from purpose, effort feels arbitrary, the session becomes mechanical. Too far: each action feels insignificant against the scale of the vision — why does this one paragraph matter when the book is months away? The right horizon is the one where the day's work feels like it counts.

## Science

Goal specificity research (Locke & Latham, 1990) established that specific, bounded goals outperform vague ones by significant margins on both effort and output quality. The mechanism is attentional: a bounded goal narrows the field to what's actionable now, concentrating cognitive resources rather than diffusing them across undefined scope.

Temporal motivation theory (Steel & König, 2006) demonstrates that motivation intensity increases as the deadline approaches — the "time horizon effect." Calibrating the attention horizon to a timescale where the goal feels genuinely proximate (days or weeks rather than years) activates this urgency without requiring false deadlines. The horizon is not a constraint but a focal length adjustment.

Time perception in flow states (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990) involves a characteristic suspension of ordinary clock-time — hours feel like minutes. This requires simultaneous directionality (knowing what you're doing and why) and presence (absorption in the immediate action). A correctly set horizon provides both: it gives direction without demanding the full long-arc to be resolved now.

## Recall

**Hook**: The right horizon isn't a planning decision — it's an aperture decision. How far can you see clearly right now?
**Mechanism**: Too short a horizon loses direction; too long a horizon loses presence; the correct horizon is where action and meaning overlap — the window in which the day's work feels like it counts toward something real.
**Anchor**: Presentist-Eternalist Toggle — begin sessions in eternalist mode (2–3 min connecting today's work to the arc), then toggle to presentist for execution. When dysregulated, collapse horizon to the single next action.
