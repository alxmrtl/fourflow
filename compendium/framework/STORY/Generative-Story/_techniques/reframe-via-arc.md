# Reframe Via Arc

> Stuckness is a stage, not a verdict — name the stage and the story reopens.

## Mechanism
When forward movement halts, the nervous system registers it as threat: amygdala activation narrows attention and generates the perceptual experience of being trapped or failing. This is neurologically identical to the emotion — the threat response doesn't distinguish between "I am stuck" as a fact and "I am stuck" as a story.

Narrative reframing works by inserting a higher-order frame around the stuck moment. The hero's journey arc provides a stable map of predictable stages — Ordinary World, Call, Refusal, Ordeal, Reward, Return — each with its own experiential signature. When you locate yourself in the map, the felt sense shifts from "this is wrong" to "this is where the arc goes." The amygdala threat response partially down-regulates because the situation has been reclassified from danger to expected difficulty.

The key mechanism is not denial — the difficulty doesn't disappear. It's reclassification: the same events acquire a different meaning structure, and that structure predicts resolution. Predictable difficulty is tolerable; inexplicable difficulty is destabilizing.

## Protocol
1. Name the stuckness explicitly in one sentence: "I am stuck because ___."
2. Map it to an arc stage. Ask: "In a story about someone doing what I'm doing, which stage does this moment belong to?" (Ordeal is the most common; Refusal and Threshold crossings also generate stuckness.)
3. State the reframe aloud or in writing: "This isn't failure — this is the [stage name]. The arc requires this."
4. Name what the arc says comes next — not a plan, just the narrative expectation (e.g., "After the ordeal, the reward. Something will shift.").
5. Return to the work with the arc frame active.

## When to Use
- When stuckness has persisted for more than one session and has started to feel like identity ("I'm someone who can't do this")
- When present difficulty is being interpreted as evidence that the mission was wrong
- When motivation has collapsed and the work itself feels meaningless

## Key Insight
> "The ordeal doesn't mean you're failing the story — it means you're in it."
