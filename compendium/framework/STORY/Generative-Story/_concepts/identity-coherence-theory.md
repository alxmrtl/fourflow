# Identity Coherence Theory

> People maintain narrative coherence about the self even at the cost of accuracy — editing memories, ignoring contradictions, and reinterpreting events to sustain a consistent self-story. True story is the deliberate counter-practice.

## What It Is

Identity coherence theory draws from several converging lines of research — including work by Dan McAdams on narrative identity, Michael Ross and colleagues on autobiographical memory revision, and Hazel Markus on the self-concept. The convergent finding: the human mind treats the coherence of the self-narrative as a priority that often overrides accuracy. People systematically revise their memories of their past attitudes, beliefs, and behaviors to align with their current self-concept, rather than preserving the original record.

This is not pathology — it is the ordinary operation of autobiographical memory. Memory is not a recording; it is a reconstruction that occurs at the moment of recall, shaped by current self-conception, current goals, and current emotional state. Each retrieval is also a re-encoding. The result is that the self-narrative accumulates in the direction of coherence: the story becomes more consistent with itself over time, regardless of whether that consistency tracks reality.

The functional cost is that the self-story gradually diverges from the actual developmental arc. Capabilities that haven't changed are experienced as having always been present. Habits that have degraded are remembered as stronger than they were. The narrative becomes a curated identity rather than a living document — coherent, but not especially informative about what is actually happening.

## Why It Shapes Flow Access

True story requires that the narrative track reality closely enough to be a reliable guide to action. Identity coherence theory establishes the primary mechanism by which stories lose this tracking: not through dramatic self-deception but through the ordinary operation of memory reconstruction. Every recall is a gentle edit in the direction of the preferred self-image.

Flow requires accurate situational assessment — what level of challenge is appropriate right now, what capabilities are actually available, where the genuine edge is. A story distorted by coherence-preserving revision will either overstate capability (setting up challenge-skill mismatch in the anxiety direction) or understate it (setting up mismatch in the boredom direction). True story is the deliberate practice of maintaining external anchors — records, objective measures, honest feedback — that resist the drift.

## Application

The antidote to identity coherence drift is contemporaneous documentation. Write records of your actual state, decisions, and assessments at the time they occur — not later. Later recall is already reconstructed. A simple practice: at the end of each session, write three sentences about what actually happened, separate from any interpretation. Over time, this creates an external archive that resists the inward revision that coherence mechanisms produce. When the narrative starts drifting — when the remembered past and the recorded past diverge — that divergence is precisely where the work of true story is required.
