# Post-Traumatic Growth

> Tedeschi & Calhoun's research showing that people often experience positive psychological change following struggle — expanded perspective, deeper relationships, and new possibilities emerge from the wreckage of the old story.

## What It Is

Post-Traumatic Growth (PTG) is a theoretical framework developed by Richard Tedeschi and Lawrence Calhoun at the University of North Carolina in the 1990s, grounded in extensive research on how people change following major adversity. Their central finding: a significant proportion of people who experience severe loss, illness, or trauma report not just recovery but measurable positive transformation — greater personal strength, new possibilities, deeper relationships, spiritual development, and an enhanced appreciation for life.

Critically, PTG is not resilience (bouncing back to baseline) and it is not denial. It is genuine growth — but the growth is contingent on one specific mechanism: narrative reconstruction. The old story — who you were, what mattered, what was possible — has to break first. The new story that forms in response to that rupture is often structurally larger. The challenge forced an expansion of the framework that could not have happened without the disruption.

Tedeschi and Calhoun's Posttraumatic Growth Inventory measures five domains: personal strength, new possibilities, relating to others, appreciation for life, and spiritual change. People high in PTG don't have fewer problems; they have a larger story in which problems have legible meaning.

## Why It Shapes Flow Access

Expansive story — the quality of holding a narrative large enough to contain difficulty without collapsing — is exactly what PTG research describes as the outcome of successful narrative reconstruction. Flow is blocked by a story too small to accommodate challenge: when difficulty reads as evidence of failure rather than evidence of plot, the system contracts rather than engages.

PTG establishes that the expansion is real and achievable — not a reframe imposed from outside but a genuine structural enlargement that happens when the narrative reconstruction is complete. The expansive quality is not optimistic spin; it is the story that has survived adversity and grown because of it.

## Application

The practical implication: when reviewing a setback or period of struggle, the question is not "how do I get back to where I was?" but "what has this forced me to see that I couldn't see before?" Identify at least one genuine expansion — a capability discovered, a priority clarified, a relationship deepened. This is not forced positivity; it is the active completion of the reconstruction that PTG research shows the brain is already attempting. Completing it consciously accelerates what would otherwise happen slowly or not at all.
