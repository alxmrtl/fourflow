# Narrative Transportation

> Green & Brock's concept: when deeply absorbed in a narrative, people are transported — critical resistance drops, emotional engagement rises, and self-perception shifts. Transportation is not entertainment; it is a mechanism for genuine perspective expansion.

## What It Is

Narrative transportation theory was developed by Melanie Green and Timothy Brock in their 2000 paper "The Role of Transportation in the Persuasiveness of Public Narratives." Their core finding: when people become absorbed in a story — whether fiction, personal narrative, or testimony — they enter a state of mental transportation in which attention is fully allocated to the narrative world, analytical processing decreases, and both beliefs and self-concept become malleable.

Transportation is characterized by three components: cognitive involvement (attention focused on the narrative), emotional involvement (affective responses to the story's events), and mental imagery (vivid simulation of the narrative world). When all three are present, the transported reader or listener is not evaluating the story — they are inside it. This reduced evaluative processing is precisely why transported individuals often emerge from narratives with altered beliefs and attitudes that they would have resisted if presented as arguments.

Subsequent research by Green and colleagues has shown that the transportation effect is not limited to fiction — it applies equally to personal life narratives. The mechanism is the same: when a person enters their own story as a protagonist fully present in the unfolding arc, the same transportation dynamics apply. The narrative becomes generative rather than merely descriptive.

## Why It Shapes Flow Access

Expansive story requires genuine immersion in one's own narrative — not detached observation, but protagonist-level absorption. Transportation theory explains the mechanism: when the self-narrative achieves the density and coherence required to produce transportation, the critical, evaluative mind yields to the story's logic. This is precisely the state in which the story can expand — because expansion requires suspending the habitual framework long enough for a larger one to form.

Flow itself has strong phenomenological parallels with narrative transportation: reduced self-consciousness, time distortion, full attentional absorption. An expansive story that transports the protagonist into their own arc creates the pre-conditions for flow by establishing the same absorption architecture through narrative means.

## Application

The diagnostic question: is your current life narrative vivid enough to produce transportation? Can you enter it — feel its stakes, see its setting, locate yourself as an active protagonist — or does it feel like a summary of facts? Narratives that transport have specific, sensory detail; they have a protagonist with clear desires and obstacles; they have momentum. Enriching the self-narrative toward these qualities is not dramatization — it is making it structurally capable of producing the absorption that enables perspective expansion.
