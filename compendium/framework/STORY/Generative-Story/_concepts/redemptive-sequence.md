# Redemptive Sequence

> Dan McAdams' research finding: generative adults consistently narrate their lives with redemptive sequences — negative events that were transformed into positive outcomes. The pattern predicts generativity, well-being, and the capacity to keep moving.

## What It Is

A redemptive sequence is a narrative pattern identified by Dan McAdams in his research on highly generative adults: a story beat where a bad event (failure, loss, suffering, humiliation) is followed by a transformation into something good (insight, growth, meaning, opportunity). The person doesn't deny the bad thing happened — they integrate it into a story where it *led somewhere*.

The opposite pattern — the contamination sequence — is where good events are followed by bad outcomes, poisoning what came before. Contamination sequences predict depression, stagnation, and withdrawal.

McAdams found that the presence of redemptive sequences in a person's life narrative was one of the strongest predictors of psychological generativity — the capacity to contribute, create, and keep acting on behalf of something larger than oneself.

## Why It Enables Flow

Flow requires forward orientation — the sense that action leads somewhere, that effort is worthwhile, that difficulty is temporary. A narrative built on redemptive sequences provides exactly this: a track record of evidence that hard things have led to good ones. This reduces the threat signal of new difficulty, lowers the cost of entering the struggle phase, and sustains engagement through uncertainty.

Conversely, a narrative built on contamination sequences generates the opposite orientation: effort leads to loss, investment leads to disappointment, therefore protection is safer than action. In this frame, flow's required challenge feels not like opportunity but like exposure.

## Application

The practice is retrospective reframing: for any significant difficult event, identify what it opened, enabled, or taught that wouldn't have been accessible otherwise. Not toxic positivity — the hardship was real. But the next chapter is also real. Writing this explicitly ("that experience led to X") builds the redemptive sequence into the narrative record and makes the pattern available as evidence for the next difficult passage.
