# Job Demands-Control Model

> Karasek's model: strain is highest when demands are high and control is low. But high demands with high control produce active learning and growth — the "active job" quadrant. Defined role enables high control by clarifying the domain of authority. You can only exercise control over what is clearly yours.

## What It Is

The Job Demands-Control (JDC) model was proposed by Robert Karasek in 1979 as a two-dimensional framework for predicting occupational strain and health outcomes. The model crosses job demands (the pace, volume, and difficulty of work required) against decision latitude or control (the degree to which the worker can determine how to do the work and what skills to deploy). The original insight: it is not high demands alone that produce strain, but high demands combined with low control.

This gives rise to four quadrants. The high-strain quadrant (high demands, low control) is the most damaging: it produces the highest rates of cardiovascular disease, burnout, and psychological distress. The passive quadrant (low demands, low control) produces learned helplessness and skill atrophy. The low-strain quadrant (low demands, high control) is comfortable but developmental plateau. The active job quadrant (high demands, high control) is the most generative: it produces active problem-solving, skill development, intrinsic motivation, and engagement — the conditions closest to sustained flow.

Karasek and Theorell extended the model in 1990 to include social support as a third dimension, but the demand-control relationship remains the core finding. Subsequent decades of research have confirmed the model's predictive validity across numerous industries and cultures.

## Why It Enables Flow

The active job quadrant is structurally identical to the flow conditions Csikszentmihalyi identified: high challenge (demands) matched with high capability and agency (control). The JDC model makes the organizational and role-level conditions explicit: control — the ability to determine method, pace, and approach — is not just a preference; it is a structural prerequisite for the active engagement that flow requires.

Defined role enables control by establishing what is clearly within one's domain of authority. Without definition, control is ambiguous — you don't know whether you can make a decision without approval, whether a responsibility is yours to own, or what latitude you have in method. Ambiguous authority is functionally low control, regardless of nominal autonomy. Defined role converts nominal authority into operational control by making the domain explicit.

## Application

Map your significant roles against the demand-control matrix. Where are you in the strain quadrant — high demands but unclear or insufficient authority? The intervention is not to reduce demands but to increase control: negotiate explicit authority over the methods and decisions within your scope, document what you can decide independently, and identify the specific boundaries that need to be defined to convert ambiguous authority into operational control. Movement from strain to active requires control expansion, not demand reduction. The target is the active job quadrant: high demands, high control, high engagement.
