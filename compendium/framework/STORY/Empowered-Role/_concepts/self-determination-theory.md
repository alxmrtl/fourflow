# Self-Determination Theory

> Deci and Ryan's framework: three basic psychological needs — autonomy, competence, and relatedness — must be satisfied for intrinsic motivation and sustained engagement to emerge. Deprive any one, and engagement degrades.

## What It Is

Self-Determination Theory (SDT), developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan over four decades of research, is the most empirically supported theory of human motivation. Its core claim: humans have three basic psychological needs whose satisfaction is necessary (not just useful) for intrinsic motivation, psychological well-being, and optimal functioning:

- **Autonomy**: the experience of being the author of your own behavior — acting from genuine values and interests rather than external pressure or internal compulsion
- **Competence**: the experience of effective functioning and growing mastery — feeling capable of producing desired outcomes
- **Relatedness**: the experience of meaningful connection to others — caring for and being cared about by people who matter

SDT distinguishes itself from other motivation theories by its insistence that these are genuine *needs*, not preferences. Their chronic deprivation produces predictable degradation in motivation, performance, and well-being. Their satisfaction produces intrinsic motivation, vitality, and sustained engagement.

## Why It Enables Flow

The three SDT needs map precisely onto the conditions Csikszentmihalyi identified for flow: autonomy (clear sense of personal agency), competence (challenge matched to skill level), and the relatedness dimension of meaning (the sense that the work matters). An empowered role that satisfies all three creates the structural foundation for flow to occur reliably rather than accidentally.

The theoretical synthesis: intrinsic motivation (which SDT explains) is the dispositional prerequisite for autotelic experience (which Csikszentmihalyi describes). You cannot reliably access flow in contexts that systematically undermine autonomy or competence.

## Application

The diagnostic: for each role or context where flow is desired, evaluate how well it satisfies each need. Which is most depleted? Address that one first. Autonomy: increase latitude over methods. Competence: calibrate challenge to current skill level, add skill development opportunities. Relatedness: ensure the work connects to people and purposes that genuinely matter.
