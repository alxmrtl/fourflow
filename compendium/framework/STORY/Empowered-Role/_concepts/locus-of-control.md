# Locus of Control

> Rotter's concept: people with internal locus of control believe outcomes are primarily determined by their own actions; external locus — by luck, others, or circumstances. Internal locus correlates with better performance, resilience, and wellbeing. Owned role is the behavioral expression of internal locus.

## What It Is

Locus of control was introduced by Julian Rotter in 1954 and formally defined in his 1966 monograph as a generalized expectancy about the source of reinforcement and outcomes in one's life. People with an internal locus of control believe that their outcomes are primarily contingent on their own behavior, effort, and decisions. People with an external locus of control believe that outcomes are primarily determined by factors outside themselves — luck, powerful others, circumstances, or fate.

Rotter's conceptualization is as a relatively stable generalized expectancy, but it is also situation-specific: the same person may hold internal locus in domains where they have experienced consistent control and external locus in domains where they have experienced consistent helplessness (related to Seligman's work on learned helplessness). Internal locus is not a fixed trait but a learned expectancy — and one that can be shifted through experience and deliberate practice.

The empirical record is consistent: internal locus of control correlates with higher academic achievement, better health outcomes, greater career success, stronger resilience in adversity, lower anxiety, and higher subjective wellbeing. The mechanism is not that internal-locus individuals have more control in objective terms; it is that they act differently in the presence of difficulty. Where external-locus individuals attribute difficulty to fixed circumstances (and therefore stop trying), internal-locus individuals attribute it to their own modifiable approach (and therefore adjust).

## Why It Enables Flow

Owned role — the quality of acting from the assumption that your choices are the primary determining variable in your work — is the behavioral expression of internal locus. Flow requires agency: the sense that your actions matter and that effort connects to outcome. Without this sense, the challenge-engagement relationship inverts: challenge becomes evidence of limitation rather than an invitation to effort.

Internal locus also enables the feedback integration that flow requires. When outcomes are attributed to one's own choices, feedback becomes actionable: if the outcome was my doing, a different approach might produce a different result. External-locus attribution blocks this: if the outcome was determined by luck or circumstance, feedback contains no information about what to change.

## Application

Locus of control can be shifted at the attribution level. For any setback or obstacle, practice the explicit attribution exercise: identify one specific decision or action that contributed to the outcome, and one specific adjustment that would change the result. This is not self-blame — it is the deliberate installation of agency-attribution at the level of a single incident. Done consistently, it retrains the expectancy. The target is not to believe you control everything, but to act as though your choices are the most available leverage point — which, for most individual performance situations, they are.
