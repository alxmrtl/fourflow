# Owned
*The Concentrate quality of Empowered Role*

## What it is

When this quality is present, quitting has a cost. Not because of external enforcement but because investment has been made — commitments extended, resources committed, a stake placed. The work is genuinely yours in a way that makes disengagement feel like a loss rather than a neutral option. When absent, the role is held conditionally: the investment is low enough that stepping back always feels viable, and sustained effort depends on continuous motivation rather than structure. The concentrate move is escalating commitment: each act of investment — a public declaration, a financial stake, a defined boundary — raises the cost of not continuing, making the work self-sustaining rather than motivation-dependent.

## Science

Psychological ownership research (Pierce, Kostova & Dirks, 2001) documents that when individuals feel a sense of ownership over an object, outcome, or project, their investment, care, and sustained effort increase substantially — even in the absence of formal authority or material stake. The mechanisms are identity incorporation (the thing becomes part of "mine"), personal investment (effort and attention reinforce the ownership feeling), and accountability to self.

Commitment escalation (Staw, 1976; Arkes & Blumer, 1985) demonstrates that prior investment increases subsequent commitment — the sunk cost effect, applied constructively. Small commitments build the investment that makes larger commitments feel natural. The structure works because each act of commitment raises the cost of withdrawal, making continuation psychologically cheaper than stopping.

Locus of control research (Rotter, 1966; Bandura, 1977) consistently predicts that internal attribution — the belief that outcomes are authored by oneself — sustains engagement where external attribution doesn't. Owned commitment reinforces internal attribution by design: when you chose to commit and bear the investment, the outcome is genuinely yours.

## Recall

**Hook**: The more you invest, the harder it is to quit — and that's the point. Commitment is designed to outrun motivation.
**Mechanism**: Each act of commitment raises the cost of disengagement and makes continuation psychologically cheaper than stopping; the escalating investment generates its own momentum, independent of felt motivation in any given moment.
**Anchor**: Before beginning, ask: "What commitment can I make now that makes stopping more expensive than continuing?"
