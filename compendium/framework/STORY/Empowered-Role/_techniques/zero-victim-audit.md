# Zero Victim Audit

> Victim narratives don't require dramatic injustice — even subtle ones drain the sense that your actions matter.

## Mechanism
Locus of control research (Rotter) distinguishes between internal locus (belief that outcomes are primarily determined by one's own actions) and external locus (belief that outcomes are primarily determined by external forces). Internal locus correlates consistently with higher performance, greater resilience, and more effective behavior change. The direction of causation runs both ways: people with internal locus take more effective actions, and taking more effective actions strengthens internal locus.

Victim narratives — narratives in which the self is acted upon rather than acting — are not necessarily false. External forces genuinely constrain and affect outcomes. The problem is not the content of victim narratives but their cognitive effect: they train the brain to perceive itself as a passive object of forces, and this perception becomes self-fulfilling. Once the brain models itself as acted-upon, it stops generating and evaluating action options, because actions are the tool of agents, not victims.

The audit doesn't require denial of external reality — it requires identifying where the narrative has assigned causation to external forces in cases where the self also had causal power. The rewrite doesn't eliminate the external factor; it reinstates the self as a causal agent in the same events. "The market shifted and destroyed my business" becomes "The market shifted and I didn't adapt fast enough" — the market is still in the story, but so is the self, and so is the self's power to have acted differently.

## Protocol
1. Write a brief narrative of a current difficulty or a recent painful outcome — 5–10 sentences, no filter.
2. Scan for passive constructions and external attributions: anywhere you are "acted upon," "affected by," "blocked by," "unable to because of," or where causation is assigned to things outside you.
3. For each: ask "What was my role in this? What did I do or not do that contributed?" This is not about blame — it is about recovering the part of the causal picture that involves your choices.
4. Rewrite the narrative in active voice with yourself as a present causal agent. The external factors can remain — they just can't be the only causes.
5. For any item where you genuinely had no causal role: that's a legitimate external constraint, not a victim narrative. Leave it as-is. The audit distinguishes between the two.
6. Run this audit monthly on any narrative that has felt stuck or hopeless.

## When to Use
- When a narrative about a difficulty has been held for more than two weeks without producing any new action
- When you notice that thinking about a problem produces helplessness rather than options
- Before any significant decision-making about whether to continue, change, or exit a situation — the audit ensures the decision is made from full agency rather than from passive constraint

## Key Insight
> "You don't have to deny what happened to you. You just have to find where you were also a cause."
