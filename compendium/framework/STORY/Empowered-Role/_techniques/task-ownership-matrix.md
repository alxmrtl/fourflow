# Task Ownership Matrix

> Accountability fog — nobody knows who owns what — doesn't just waste time. It drains the sense of being someone who matters in the system.

## Mechanism
When ownership of recurring tasks is undefined or ambiguous, two failure modes emerge simultaneously: tasks fall through gaps (no one acts because everyone assumes someone else is responsible) and tasks get duplicated (multiple people act because no one knows the other is). Both produce wasted effort, but the psychological cost is less often examined: undefined ownership severs the connection between individual action and outcome.

Self-Determination Theory identifies autonomy and competence as the two psychological needs most directly linked to intrinsic motivation. Both require role definition: you cannot experience autonomy in tasks you're not sure are yours, and you cannot build genuine competence in responsibilities that are shared without explicit allocation. The matrix makes ownership explicit at the task level — not at the abstract role level, which is often clear, but at the specific recurring-task level, which often isn't.

The matrix format forces a binary choice for each task: owner (you are responsible for the outcome) or contributor (you participate, but someone else is accountable). This binary is clarifying because it prevents the middle ground where everyone feels 40% responsible — a condition that produces 0% accountability.

## Protocol
1. List every recurring responsibility in your current role — tasks that happen weekly, monthly, quarterly.
2. For each, answer the ownership question: Am I the owner (accountable for outcome) or a contributor (involved but not accountable)?
3. For tasks where you are the owner: confirm no one else believes they are also the owner. If they do, resolve it.
4. For tasks where you are a contributor: name the owner explicitly. If no one is the owner, someone needs to become one — determine who.
5. For tasks with no owner: decide now. Either claim ownership, delegate it, or eliminate the task if it lacks a willing owner.
6. Review the matrix quarterly. Recurring tasks drift in and out of relevance; ownership changes as roles evolve.

## When to Use
- When joining a team or taking on a new set of responsibilities where task ownership isn't yet formalized
- When accountability fog has been causing tasks to fall through gaps repeatedly
- Before any high-stakes period (launch, delivery, deadline) where missed ownership will have visible consequences

## Key Insight
> "Partial ownership is no ownership. Every task needs a single person who loses sleep if it doesn't happen."
