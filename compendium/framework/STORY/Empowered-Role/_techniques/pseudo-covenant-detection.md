# Pseudo-Covenant Detection

> Identifies commitments that appear fixed but collapse under pressure — resolution without structure. If it must be re-decided under load, it is not covenant.

## Mechanism
A true covenant is an identity-level commitment that binds across time — it doesn't need to be re-decided when pressure rises. A pseudo-covenant has the form of commitment but not the structure: it is declared in a moment of clarity but has no mechanism to survive load. The markers are recognizable: "I've decided," "never again," "this time is different." These statements feel binding when spoken but dissolve when tested. The gap between the two is not willpower — it's structural. True commitments have been built upward from stable lower layers (behaviour, felt sense, rules, identity). Pseudo-covenants attempt to install at the identity layer without the supporting structure, so they collapse the moment load arrives. Detecting the difference before investing further escalation prevents the oscillation cycle of commit-break-shame-recommit.

## Protocol

1. **Name the commitment.** What have you declared, decided, or committed to? State it clearly.
2. **Check the markers.** Does the commitment use language like:
   - "I've decided" (but have you *structured* or just *declared*?)
   - "Never again" (has the load-bearing function been replaced, or just suppressed?)
   - "This time is different" (what's structurally different, not just emotionally different?)
3. **Run the pressure test.** Imagine the specific scenario where this commitment will be tested. Not abstract pressure — the actual situation (the 11pm craving, the argument, the deadline panic, the boredom). Can the commitment survive *that*, or does it need the question to not arise?
4. **Check structural support.** Does this commitment have:
   - Stable behaviour underneath it (L0 — what do you actually do when tested?)
   - Felt-sense alignment (L1 — does it feel right in the body, or only in the head?)
   - Rules/structures supporting it (L2 — is the environment designed to hold this?)
   - Identity integration (L3 — is this who you are, or who you wish you were?)
5. **Diagnose.** If it lacks structural support at any layer below, it is a pseudo-covenant. Not bad — just not yet ready to bear weight.
6. **Build downward.** Don't abandon the commitment — build the structure underneath it. Start at the lowest unsupported layer and work up. The commitment becomes real when it no longer needs to be re-decided.

## When to Use
Before escalating commitment (public declarations, financial investments, burned bridges). Before trusting a "this time is different" feeling. When reviewing past commitments that repeatedly failed. When someone else makes a commitment to you and you need to assess its structural reality.
