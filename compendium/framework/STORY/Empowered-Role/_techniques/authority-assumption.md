# Authority Assumption

> Most people are waiting for permission that was never required and will never arrive.

## Mechanism
Learned helplessness research (Seligman) demonstrates that when people experience repeated situations where their actions don't produce outcomes, they stop acting — even in new situations where action would work. A subtler version operates in role contexts: people raised in hierarchical environments internalize the pattern that action requires prior authorization. This pattern persists even when the authorization structure has been removed, when the person has genuine authority, or when no one is waiting to grant permission.

The cognitive tell is the experience of wanting to act but feeling a vague sense that something needs to happen first — someone needs to approve, circumstances need to align, the right moment needs to arrive. This waiting-for-permission state is often unconscious; it presents as caution, reasonableness, or strategic patience. Functionally, it is deferred agency.

The authority assumption technique bypasses the authorization-seeking loop by treating permission as already granted and observing what remains. If you would act if you had permission, and you assume you have permission, and you still don't act — something else is stopping you. The technique makes the real obstacle visible: fear of being wrong, uncertainty about the right move, habit of inaction. Once visible, the obstacle can be addressed directly rather than through a proxy search for permission.

## Protocol
1. Identify a domain where you have been waiting to act — something you've been planning to do "when the time is right" or "once you have the green light."
2. Assume full permission, right now. Not as a declaration to others — as an internal cognitive shift: "I am authorized to act on this. There is no permission to obtain."
3. From that assumption: what would you do first, concretely? Write it down. What would you do today?
4. Now examine: with permission assumed, what is the actual resistance to acting? List everything that still stops you.
5. Categorize each resistance item: fear (of being wrong, of failure, of judgment), uncertainty (genuinely don't know the right move), habit (just haven't started), external constraint (something actually is blocked by an outside factor — not permission, but logistics or resources).
6. Address each category directly. Most items will be fear or habit, not genuine external constraints. That changes the intervention.

## When to Use
- When a project or initiative has been "almost ready to start" for more than two weeks
- When you find yourself constructing elaborate plans and rationales but not executing
- When someone external has told you that you have authority in a domain but you don't feel it — this surfaces what's actually in the way

## Key Insight
> "Assume permission and the real obstacle appears. It's almost never the missing permission."
