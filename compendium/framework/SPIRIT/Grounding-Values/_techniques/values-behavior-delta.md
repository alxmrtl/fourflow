# Values-Behavior Delta

> A weekly tally of the gap — because incongruence accumulates silently until it becomes who you are.

## Mechanism
Identity drift doesn't announce itself. Each small action that contradicts a stated value feels like a minor exception — too small to register, easy to rationalize. But the nervous system tracks these violations even when the conscious mind dismisses them. The cumulative signal is a low-grade dissonance: nothing feels wrong enough to name, but nothing feels fully aligned either. This is the state in which flow becomes chronically expensive to enter.

The weekly cadence is critical. At 7 days, the violations are recent enough to remember accurately but numerous enough to reveal a pattern. Monthly audits find the aggregate but miss the specific incidents — the raw material needed to identify recurring triggers. Daily tracking is too granular to see the pattern and becomes self-punishing rather than diagnostic.

The act of naming each delta — writing it down, not just sensing it — activates the prefrontal cortex's self-evaluation circuits. This shifts the violation from an unprocessed emotional residue to a specific, bounded problem. Named problems can be solved. Unnamed residue just accumulates.

## Protocol
1. At week's end (Friday afternoon or Sunday evening), open a simple log — paper, note, or recurring entry.
2. Recall the past 7 days. Write down every action, decision, or avoidance that contradicted a value you claim to hold. Be specific: "Agreed to a project I knew conflicted with my boundary around X." "Stayed silent when honesty was called for."
3. Tally the count per stated value. Note which values were violated most frequently — this is the signal.
4. For the top violation: ask one question — "Is this value actually mine, or inherited?" If it's yours, identify the specific trigger that produces the gap (stress, social pressure, fatigue) and choose one countermeasure for next week.
5. Keep the list small. The goal is not comprehensive guilt — it's early detection of a pattern before it becomes identity drift.

## When to Use
- As a fixed weekly ritual, not triggered by feeling bad (waiting for distress means the gap has already compounded)
- When entering a demanding stretch of work — a pre-flight check that clears background dissonance before it taxes performance
- After a period of external pressure (deadline crunch, conflict, high-stakes negotiations) that tends to generate situational exceptions that quietly become habits

## Key Insight
> "Every exception you don't examine becomes a precedent — for the next one."
