# Operative Worship Test

> Reveals what you actually treat as non-negotiable by testing what doesn't move under pressure — distinguishing operative values from simulated ones.

## Mechanism
What we profess and what we worship are often different things. "Worship" in the operational sense is not religious — it's whatever the system treats as not subject to revision. What we say we value (health, integrity, creativity) may be revisable under load; what we actually worship is whatever survives the test of pressure. This creates three states: absent (everything is negotiable, self remains sovereign), simulated (declared as fixed, but collapses under pressure), and operative (not revisable, resists descent under load). The gap between simulated and operative is where most value-based friction lives. When you think your values are operative but they're actually simulated, you experience internal contradiction without understanding its source — the "why can't I live my values?" question. The answer: they aren't your operative values yet. Something else is.

## Protocol

1. **Name your declared values.** What do you say matters most? What would you tell someone defines you? List 3–5.
2. **For each, run the pressure test.** Not "would I sacrifice this?" in the abstract — that's easy. Instead: recall or vividly imagine the specific scenario where this value was or would be tested under real load.
   - Under financial pressure, did this hold?
   - Under social pressure, did this hold?
   - Under emotional overwhelm, did this hold?
   - Under time pressure, did this hold?
3. **Identify what actually didn't move.** Look at your behaviour under the worst pressure you've faced. Not what you wished you'd done — what you actually did. What was protected even when everything else gave way? That is what you worship.
4. **Classify each value:**
   - **Absent**: never tested, or negotiated away without distress → not yet a value, just a preference
   - **Simulated**: declared, felt binding in calm conditions, but collapsed under specific load → a value-in-progress, needs structural support
   - **Operative**: held under real pressure, even at cost → this is what actually organises your decisions
5. **Name the gap.** The distance between your declared values and your operative values is the source of internal friction. This is not a moral failure — it's a diagnostic. The work is either building the simulated values into operative ones (through repeated small-load testing), or updating your declared values to match what's actually operative (radical honesty about who you actually are).
6. **Check for hidden worship.** Sometimes what's operative is not a value at all — it's comfort, approval, safety, or control. If the question of authority can be revisited, authority has not left the system. Name what's actually sovereign.

## When to Use
During internal-alignment check-ins. When experiencing persistent value-behaviour gaps. When "I know what I should do but I can't seem to do it" becomes a recurring pattern. After a period of pressure where behaviour diverged from stated values — not as self-punishment, but as data collection.

## Key Insight
> "Worship is not what is professed. It is what is not subject to revision. What doesn't move when pressure rises — that is what you worship."
