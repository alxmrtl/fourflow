# Values-Behavior Gap

> The measurable divergence between what you say you value and what your actual choices reveal — the primary source of values-layer friction that drains energy and blocks sustained flow.

## What It Is

The values-behavior gap is the distance between stated values and enacted behavior. It is not a moral failing — it's a signal. Every gap represents cognitive resources being spent on the dissonance between what you believe you should be doing and what you're actually doing.

The gap is often invisible because humans are highly skilled at post-hoc rationalization: constructing explanations for why the choice made was actually the values-consistent one. This makes the gap hard to detect through introspection but visible through behavioral audit: track what you actually spend time on, and compare it to what you say matters most.

The gap has compounding effects. Small divergences — skipping the workout you said mattered, choosing reactive communication over deep work, spending money incongruent with stated priorities — accumulate into a background sense of inauthenticity and lost agency. This is the "I'm busy but not fulfilled" state: technically productive, values-misaligned.

## Why It Blocks Flow

Flow requires full commitment to the task. The values-behavior gap prevents this by keeping part of the system in resistance. Even when you're working, a values-misaligned task generates subtle but constant friction — a signal that something is wrong at a deeper level. This friction is not strong enough to stop work, but it is strong enough to prevent complete absorption.

The closing of this gap — through small integrity loops that bring daily behavior into alignment with stated values — releases the energy that was being spent on the dissonance. The freed energy becomes available for the work itself.

## Application

The integrity audit: for one week, track your actual time allocation and compare it to your stated values. Where are the largest gaps? Choose one gap to close this week — one small behavioral change that brings action into alignment with a stated value. The change doesn't need to be large; the act of closing a gap releases more energy than the effort requires.
