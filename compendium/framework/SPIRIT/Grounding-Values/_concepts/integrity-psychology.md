# Integrity Psychology

> Integrity is not a virtue — it is a structural property of the self: the alignment between stated values, internal experience, and external behavior. Misalignment generates chronic stress regardless of intent.

## What It Is

Integrity, as a psychological construct rather than a moral category, has been studied most rigorously in the tradition of self-determination theory and authentic functioning research (Ryan & Deci, Kernis & Goldman). In this framework, integrity is defined as concordance across three levels: the values you espouse, the internal experience you actually have, and the behavior you enact. All three must point in the same direction for integrity to be structurally present.

Michael Kernis and Brian Goldman's work on authenticity operationalizes this as four components: awareness (knowing your values, emotions, and strengths accurately), unbiased processing (not denying or distorting self-relevant information), behavioral action (acting in accordance with your values rather than social pressure), and relational orientation (valuing and achieving openness and truthfulness in close relationships). Their research shows that all four components are independently associated with wellbeing — integrity is not a monolithic trait but a system of aligned processes.

The stress dimension is particularly important. Research on cognitive dissonance (Festinger) and its health extensions consistently shows that values-behavior misalignment generates a chronic low-level physiological stress response — elevated cortisol, increased cognitive load from managing the gap, and erosion of intrinsic motivation. Integrity gaps are energetically costly even when they remain below the threshold of conscious guilt.

## Why It Matters for Grounding Values

The congruent quality is the active practice of closing integrity gaps — not because alignment is morally required but because it is neurobiologically less costly than misalignment. Each integrity gap maintains a background stress load that consumes resources otherwise available for flow. Congruent practice does not demand perfection; it demands awareness of and movement toward alignment, which is sufficient to reduce the chronic stress burden.

This reframes values work entirely: it is not about discipline or moral worthiness. It is about removing a metabolic tax on the system. When behavior and values are aligned, the self-monitoring overhead disappears, cognitive resources return to primary tasks, and authentic motivation activates — the kind that doesn't require willpower to sustain.

## Application

- Map the three-level integrity stack (espoused values → internal experience → behavior) and identify which level is out of alignment — the mismatch location changes the intervention
- Treat recurring rationalization of a behavior as a diagnostic signal that an integrity gap exists and is generating hidden load
- Notice energy drain as an integrity symptom — chronic low motivation is often downstream of a values-behavior gap, not upstream of it
- Close small gaps before they compound — Kernis and Goldman's research shows that integrity is cumulative: each aligned act strengthens the structural coherence of the system
- Use the unbiased processing component deliberately: resist the pressure to reframe values to match behavior; instead surface the gap and address it at the behavior level
