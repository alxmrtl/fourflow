# Curiosity as Compass

> Elizabeth Gilbert's reframe: instead of "find your passion" (which is high-pressure and often paralyzing), follow curiosity as a navigation system — each small step of genuine interest reveals the next.

## What It Is

"Curiosity as compass" is the navigational principle articulated by author Elizabeth Gilbert: curiosity is always available, always pointing toward something, and always trustworthy as a guide. Passion, by contrast, is intermittent, often elusive, and puts enormous pressure on people to already know what they love most — which they frequently don't.

Gilbert's "breadcrumb method": notice what genuinely interests you right now, however small or seemingly irrelevant. Follow that. The next breadcrumb will reveal itself at the end of the first. The path is not visible in advance — it becomes visible by walking it.

The reframe is significant: "follow your passion" requires prior certainty. "Follow your curiosity" requires only present attention. This lowers the activation energy for beginning. You don't need to know where it leads. You need to notice what's genuinely pulling you today.

## Why It Enables Flow

The conventional prescription of "find your passion and pursue it" creates a meta-problem: what if I don't know what my passion is? The search for passion becomes its own kind of paralysis. Curiosity-as-compass dissolves this by making the navigational question immediate and answerable: what is interesting right now? That question always has an answer.

For flow specifically: curiosity provides the intrinsic pull that makes the struggle phase feel interesting rather than threatening. Following curiosity means you're always working at the edge of your genuine interest — which is exactly where the information gap theory predicts the highest engagement.

## Application

The practice is noticing, not deciding. What are you actually drawn to reading, doing, exploring when no one is watching? What questions keep recurring? What problems feel like puzzles rather than burdens? These signals, however modest, are breadcrumbs. The practice is following them seriously rather than dismissing them as irrelevant to "real work."
