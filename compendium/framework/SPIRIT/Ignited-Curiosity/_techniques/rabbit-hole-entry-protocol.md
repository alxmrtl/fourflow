# Rabbit Hole Entry Protocol

> Curiosity that can't be followed becomes anxiety — this protocol creates a bounded space to follow it fully.

## Protocol
1. When genuine curiosity arises — a question you actually want to follow, not an obligation — stop and name it explicitly: "I'm curious about X." Write it down.
2. Assess: is this worth a dedicated block? If yes, schedule a Rabbit Hole Session (30–90 min) rather than following it spontaneously in whatever you're doing.
3. At session start: write the entry question at the top of a blank page. This is your thread. Everything you find is in relation to this question.
4. Set a timer. When the timer is running, follow freely — every link, tangent, and sub-question is fair game. The container makes the freedom safe.
5. At the 10-minute warning: begin consolidating. Stop acquiring new material and start writing.
6. At session end: capture three things in your log — (a) the key finding or shift in understanding, (b) the best new question generated, (c) one unexpected connection to another domain.
7. Close the session by acknowledging the exit explicitly. The question is either answered, parked with a next-step, or escalated to a longer investigation. Do not leave it unresolved in your working memory.

## When to Use
- When a genuine question arises that keeps returning — this is the signal it warrants a dedicated block
- Before starting a creative project — a rabbit hole session on the core question builds the associative material that makes the work richer
- When energy is high but scattered — curiosity-directed exploration is more productive than forcing linear output

## Key Insight
> "Following curiosity isn't a detour from the work — it's often the fastest route through the part where you're stuck."
