# Novelty Injection

> Familiarity kills the signal — one unfamiliar element per session keeps the seeking circuit alive.

## Protocol
1. Before each work session, identify the session's primary domain and task.
2. Select one novelty element to introduce — choose from a different category each time to avoid habituating to the injection itself:
   - New source: an article, thinker, or reference outside your normal reading
   - Different format: if you normally write, sketch; if you normally outline, free-write
   - Unfamiliar constraint: a rule you've never applied (time limit, structural restriction, forbidden word or approach)
   - Cross-domain input: a question, image, or concept from a completely different field as a starting prompt
   - Different physical context: new location, new physical position, new background sound or silence
3. Introduce the novelty element at the session start — not as a distraction but as the session's opening stimulus.
4. Allow 5–10 minutes of contact with the novel element before transitioning to the primary work. Don't force a connection; let the contact happen.
5. Proceed with normal work. The novelty has primed the seeking circuit — its effects persist even after you've moved to familiar material.

## When to Use
- For any session that has become routine to the point of low engagement — the signal is going through the motions without actually noticing
- Before creative work that requires freshness — a novelty injection is a reliable way to arrive at the work already stimulated rather than waiting for stimulation to arise
- During long stretches of repetitive work — inject novelty at the start of each session to counter accumulating habituation

## Key Insight
> "You don't need a different life to feel alive — you need one unfamiliar thing per day."
