# Curiosity Ledger

> Casual curiosity fades overnight. Questions that return are signal — the ledger finds them.

## Protocol
1. Start a simple log — a dedicated notebook page, a recurring note, or a digital log. Label it "Questions." Do not label it "To Research" — the framing matters. You are tracking genuine questions, not tasks.
2. When a question arises that feels real — not rhetorical, not assigned — write it down with the date. One sentence is enough: "Why does X happen even when Y is true?"
3. Do not answer the question immediately. Log it and return to what you were doing. The ledger is not a research queue — it's a recurrence tracker.
4. Weekly, scan the ledger. Mark any questions that returned (you thought of them again without prompting) or that you've now seen from multiple angles.
5. Questions that appear three or more times across separate days are your signal tier. These are candidates for a dedicated Rabbit Hole Session (see `rabbit-hole-entry-protocol.md`) or longer investigation.
6. Periodically review your signal-tier questions as a set. Often the real underlying question is not any individual entry but a pattern across several of them — a theme your mind keeps circling. Name that theme explicitly.

## When to Use
- As an ongoing background practice — the ledger is always open, not episodic
- Before annual planning: review the ledger for the year's signal-tier questions — they often reveal which domains are genuinely calling for deeper engagement
- When curiosity feels low or mechanical — reviewing the ledger often reactivates something you've been ignoring

## Key Insight
> "Your most important questions are the ones you keep thinking about without deciding to."
