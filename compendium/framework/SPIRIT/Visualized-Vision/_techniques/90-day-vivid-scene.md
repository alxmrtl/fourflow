# 90-Day Vivid Scene

> Long-horizon vision motivates in theory. 90 days motivates in practice — it's close enough to feel like you.

## Protocol
1. Set aside 20–30 minutes of uninterrupted time. No notes from previous planning sessions — start fresh.
2. Write a scene, not a plan. Specifically: it is 90 days from today. Describe what you see, hear, and are doing. What has happened? What does the environment look like? Who are you with or not with? What decision have you already made that you haven't made yet today?
3. Write in present tense, first person, full sensory detail. "I'm sitting at my desk finishing the second draft. The document is longer than I expected — I surprised myself. The morning is quiet. I feel..." Do not write "I will have..." or "by then I hope to..."
4. Write for at least 200 words without editing. Let the scene develop — details that surprise you are the most useful ones.
5. After writing: identify the two or three specific changes that most distinguish the 90-day scene from today. These are your proximate vision anchors — the concrete delta that makes the 90-day version real.
6. Review the scene weekly for the duration of the 90 days. Do not revise it unless the direction fundamentally changes — the stability of the scene is part of its function as an attractor.

## When to Use
- At the start of a new 90-day cycle, quarter, or project — creates the proximate vision before planning begins
- When long-horizon vision is present but feels abstract and motivationally inert — the 90-day scene brings it into actionable proximity
- When daily work feels disconnected from direction — reading the scene re-anchors actions to the proximate state being created

## Key Insight
> "The scene you can feel is worth more than the vision you merely believe in."
