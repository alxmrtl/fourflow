# Resolution Test

> If you can't describe what you see, hear, and feel on the day the vision is realized — you don't have a vision yet.

## Protocol
1. Name your vision in one sentence (use the output of `vision-compression.md` if available).
2. Identify the day of realization — a specific moment when you would know the vision had become real. Not the ongoing state, but the concrete moment: "The day I [specific thing]."
3. Close your eyes for 2 minutes and enter that day. Then open your eyes and answer these questions in writing:
   - **See**: What is in your visual field on this day? Where are you? What are the physical details of the environment? Who, if anyone, is present?
   - **Hear**: What sounds are in the background? What are you saying or thinking? What words, if any, are being exchanged?
   - **Feel**: What physical sensation is present in your body? What emotional tone? What has been released — what burden is lighter on this day than today?
4. Score the resolution on each dimension — High, Medium, or Low:
   - High: specific, immediate, sensory — you could describe it to a stranger and they'd see what you see
   - Medium: general but directionally correct
   - Low: abstract, hedging, or "I don't know"
5. For each Low or Medium dimension: ask "Why can't I see this?" The answer is usually diagnostic — either the vision needs refinement, or there's a specific fear or ambiguity blocking the render.
6. The test passes when all three dimensions score High. That's a real vision.

## When to Use
- As a quality check after any vision-setting session — to confirm you've moved from aspiration to actual vision
- When a vision isn't producing motivational force despite appearing clear: usually the resolution test reveals it's actually low-resolution in one or more dimensions
- When entering a high-stakes execution phase: run the resolution test to sharpen the mental image before the work begins — the clearer the target, the more reliable the navigation

## Key Insight
> "Aspiration has direction. Vision has a destination you can describe in detail — because you've already been there in your mind."
