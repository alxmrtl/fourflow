# Vision Compression

> If you need more than one sentence without hedging, you don't yet know what you want.

## Protocol
1. Write your current vision statement — however long it is, with all qualifiers intact. This is the starting state.
2. Read it once. Count the qualifiers: words like "maybe," "kind of," "ideally," "somewhat," "a bit," "hopefully," "sort of," "in some way." Also count vague nouns ("something," "things," "aspects," "presence").
3. Remove every qualifier, one at a time. After each removal, read the sentence: Is it still true? If yes — the qualifier was protective, not descriptive. Continue removing.
4. Replace every vague noun with the specific noun it's standing in for. "Something meaningful" → what specifically? "Presence" → presence where, with whom, in what form?
5. When no further compression is possible without making the statement false, you have the irreducible core.
6. Test it: Can you say this sentence aloud to someone without flinching? If there's flinching, identify what's causing it — that's where the real specificity still needs to be stated.
7. Write the final compressed statement at the top of your vision document. One sentence. No qualifiers.

## When to Use
- At the start of any planning or strategy phase — compress the vision before building plans so the plans aren't built on ambiguity
- When plans feel scattered or contradictory — often the root cause is that vision is unclear enough to support multiple incompatible plans simultaneously
- After a significant life change or transition: old visions often survive past their expiry date, accumulating qualifiers that signal they no longer fully apply — compression forces honest recalibration

## Key Insight
> "Hedging keeps your options open — and keeps you from actually choosing anything."
