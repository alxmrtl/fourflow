# Evidence Audit

> Distinguishes between evaluating your direction based on the mirror's current state (external evidence) vs. internal clarity — preventing noise-triggered abandonment of a valid trajectory.

## Mechanism
The mirror delay varies: small shifts may reflect in days; large reorientations may take months or years. People who maintain long-horizon coherence don't do so because of external evidence — they do so because they can distinguish the clarity of their internal direction from the lagging current state of the mirror. Evaluating based on the mirror's present state alone causes valid trajectories to be abandoned before the reflection catches up.

## Protocol

1. Identify your primary current direction (the vision or trajectory you're holding).
2. List evidence that it's working.
3. List evidence that it's not.
4. Ask: **"Am I evaluating based on the mirror's current state, or based on my internal clarity about the direction?"**
5. If internal direction hasn't changed:
   - The mirror's current state is relevant data, not the verdict. Continue.
   - Ask: "Is this doubt coming from new information (signal) or from impatience/comparison/fear (noise)?"
6. If internal direction has genuinely shifted:
   - Update the trajectory cleanly — not from stubbornness, but not from fear either.
   - A real update is: "I've learned something that changes the direction." A noise-driven update is: "It hasn't worked yet and I'm scared it won't."

**The key distinction**: Long-horizon coherence is not rigidity. It's holding direction with open hands — fully committed AND responsive to genuine signal. The question is always about the *source* of the update impulse.

## When to Use
When results aren't visible yet but doubt is rising. When comparing your progress to others seems to suggest you should change course. When you're unsure whether staying the course is faith or stubbornness. Monthly, alongside the trajectory review.

## Key Insight
> "Faith is the willingness to outpace the mirror. Long-horizon coherence is faith compounded across time."
