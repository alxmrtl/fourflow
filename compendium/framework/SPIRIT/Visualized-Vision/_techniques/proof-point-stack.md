# Proof Point Stack

> The brain discounts futures it can't see evidence for — three proof points change the probability estimate.

## Protocol
1. Take your proximate vision (the 90-day vivid scene, or your current directional intent).
2. Search for evidence that this is already in motion. Evidence can include: early results (however small), conversations that pointed in this direction, decisions you've already made that you wouldn't have made a year ago, external signals (market shifts, opportunities, people appearing), internal changes (shifts in skill, clarity, or identity).
3. Select the three strongest proof points — the ones that feel most real and specific, not the most dramatic.
4. Write each one as a one-sentence statement: "Evidence that this is already beginning: [specific thing that happened/exists]."
5. Review this stack at the start of each week alongside your proximate vision. The stack's function is to keep the brain's probability estimate elevated — to maintain the experience of the vision as real and in motion, not distant and theoretical.
6. Update the stack as new evidence emerges. Replace the weakest point when a stronger one appears.

## When to Use
- When a vision is clear but feels unreal or distant — the proof-point stack grounds it in existing evidence
- When motivation for a long-term direction fluctuates widely — the stack provides a stabilizing anchor of evidence during low-motivation periods
- After a setback or plateau: deliberately finding proof that the direction is still in motion counteracts the brain's tendency to over-weight recent negative evidence

## Key Insight
> "Vision without evidence is hope. Vision with evidence is a probability you're already acting on."
