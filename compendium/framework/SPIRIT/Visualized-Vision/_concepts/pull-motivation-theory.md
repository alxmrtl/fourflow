# Pull Motivation Theory

> The empirically-established distinction between push motivation (away from threat) and pull motivation (toward desired future). Pull is more durable, less cortisol-costly, and generates approach orientation — the attentional signature of flow.

## What It Is

The distinction between approach and avoidance motivation has a long history in experimental psychology, from Lewin's field theory to Elliot and Covington's hierarchical model of achievement motivation. The core finding, replicated across hundreds of studies, is that approach motivation (movement toward a positive goal) and avoidance motivation (movement away from a negative outcome) differ not just in direction but in neurological substrate, cognitive style, phenomenology, and long-term sustainability.

Andrew Elliot and colleagues' research established that approach goals and avoidance goals are processed by distinct neural systems. Approach motivation is primarily mediated by the behavioral activation system (BAS) — dopaminergic, forward-oriented, associated with positive affect and expanded attention. Avoidance motivation is mediated by the behavioral inhibition system (BIS) — associated with elevated cortisol, threat-scanning, narrowed attention, and negative affect. Critically, avoidance motivation is self-undermining over time: the cortisol response it generates erodes memory, reduces cognitive flexibility, and depletes the sustained motivation required for long-horizon work.

Daniel Pink's synthesis in *Drive* (2009) and Deci and Ryan's self-determination theory converge on the practical implication: the most durable motivation is intrinsic approach motivation — moving toward something you genuinely want, from autonomous choice, rather than being pushed by fear, obligation, or social pressure. Seligman's PERMA model similarly places engagement and meaning (approach-oriented) above the absence of negative experience in the hierarchy of wellbeing predictors.

## Why It Enables Flow

A compelling vision is, by definition, pull motivation made concrete. It pulls rather than drives. This is not a motivational preference — it is a neurological condition for flow. Flow requires the attentional expansion, positive affect, and sustained engagement that only approach motivation generates. The BIS threat response that characterizes push motivation is directly incompatible with flow onset: it narrows attention, monitors for danger, and maintains self-referential vigilance — all of which interrupt the absorption that flow requires.

When vision work produces only "I should be doing this" or "I need to avoid failure," the motivational substrate is avoidance, and flow access is compromised regardless of skill or clarity. The compelling quality specifically cultivates approach motivation: the vision must generate genuine forward pull, not just clarify what is at stake. This distinction matters practically because vision artifacts can be constructed in either register — only the approach-register version produces the attentional conditions for flow.

## Application

- Audit your current motivational register: identify how much of your daily work energy comes from approach vs. avoidance — the ratio has direct implications for flow access and long-term sustainability
- Reconstruct vision statements in approach language: "moving toward X" rather than "avoiding Y" — the linguistic frame shapes the motivational system that activates
- When resistance or procrastination appears, diagnose whether it is approach-goal ambivalence or avoidance-goal activation — they require different interventions
- Use the felt phenomenology to distinguish: approach motivation generates an energized forward lean; avoidance motivation generates a contracted, threat-monitoring state — even when both produce action
- Protect against chronic avoidance motivation by ensuring vision contact happens before performance review or threat-activation — establish pull before pressure adds push
