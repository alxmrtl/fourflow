# Attention Residue

> Partial focus that persists on a previous task after switching — the cognitive bleed that silently depletes every new task you start.

## What It Is

Attention residue is the phenomenon where, after leaving a task (even voluntarily), part of your cognitive resources remain focused on what you just left. Named and studied by organizational psychologist Sophie Leroy, it explains why multitasking and frequent context-switching degrade performance on each individual task.

The effect is proportional to how complete the switch felt. Switching away from an unresolved task produces more residue than leaving a cleanly closed one. This means the common modern habit of leaving dozens of open loops — in work, communication, and planning — silently consumes attentional capacity at all times.

## Why It Blocks Flow

Flow requires full absorption. Attention residue means you never have 100% of your attentional resources available for the current task — you're always working with whatever's left after servicing the residual. Residue from three previous tasks means you arrive at the fourth with significantly reduced focus capacity before you've even started.

The practical ceiling this creates: deep work becomes structurally harder because every interruption doesn't just cost the time of the interruption — it costs the recovery time required to clear the residue and re-enter absorption.

## Application

The primary lever is **completion before switching**: close tasks consciously and fully before moving on. Where full completion isn't possible, a "parking lot" note — writing down exactly where you left off and what the next action is — dramatically reduces residue by giving the mind permission to release.
