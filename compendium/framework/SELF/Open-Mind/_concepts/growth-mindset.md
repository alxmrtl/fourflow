# Growth Mindset

> Carol Dweck's framework: abilities are developable, challenges are information, failure is feedback — the cognitive stance that keeps the mind receptive and adaptive rather than defended.

## What It Is

Growth mindset, defined by Stanford psychologist Carol Dweck after decades of research, is the belief that intelligence, ability, and skill are cultivable through effort and learning — as opposed to fixed mindset, where these traits are seen as innate and static.

The practical distinction is not philosophical but neurological: growth mindset activates the brain's learning circuits in response to difficulty. Fixed mindset activates threat circuits. Same objective event — a failure, a hard task, critical feedback — radically different internal consequences for focus, absorption, and resilience.

## Why It Enables Flow

Open Mind requires that new information be received as information rather than threat. A fixed mindset turns every challenging task into a performance judgment — triggering self-monitoring, ego protection, and defensive processing. All of these compete directly with the absorption flow requires.

Growth mindset removes the threat signal from difficulty. When errors are data and challenge means "growing," the mind can stay curious and engaged through friction. This is the cognitive foundation of what flow literature calls "clear goals with immediate feedback" — you can only use feedback if you're oriented toward learning from it, not surviving it.

## Application

The primary lever is reframing failure at the moment of occurrence: "What does this tell me?" rather than "What does this say about me?" The question shifts processing from threat-mode to learning-mode neurologically. Over time, this builds a default orientation where difficulty triggers curiosity rather than avoidance.
