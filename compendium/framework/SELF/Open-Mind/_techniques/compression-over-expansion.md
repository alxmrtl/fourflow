# Compression Over Expansion

> Condenses concepts to reduce mental effort.

## Why It Enables Flow

Working memory holds a fixed number of chunks — not a fixed amount of information, but a fixed number of units. Compression is the practice of making each chunk larger by encoding more meaning into it: a single phrase that contains a whole framework, a metaphor that instantiates a complex relationship, a symbol that carries an entire context. The person who has compressed their domain knowledge can hold more of it active simultaneously than the person whose knowledge is still in expanded, uncompressed form. This frees working memory for the active problem rather than for the overhead of retrieving and holding basic knowledge. Expansion — adding detail, hedging, elaborating — consumes bandwidth. Compression returns it.

## Techniques

- [[knowledge-boundary-audit]] — identifies the point where knowledge acquisition stops adding capability and starts adding load; the signal to compress and act
- *needs content* — concept compression practice: distilling complex ideas into minimal, memorable representations (single phrases, metaphors, symbols) that can be held and manipulated in working memory without effort
- *needs content* — writing compression: the discipline of reducing drafts to their minimum viable form — removing every word that doesn't add meaning, every sentence that restates the previous one
- *needs content* — commitment compression: applying the same principle to goals, projects, and priorities — reducing to the single essential thing rather than maintaining a list

## Recall

**Hook:** Working memory holds a fixed number of chunks, not a fixed amount of information — compression makes each chunk carry more.
**Mechanism:** Encoding more meaning into fewer units (phrases, metaphors, symbols) frees the working memory slots that would otherwise be consumed by expanded knowledge, making more bandwidth available for active problem-solving.
**Anchor:** After writing or thinking through any complex idea, ask: "What is the one-sentence version?" Compress until losing that sentence would lose something essential. Stop there.
