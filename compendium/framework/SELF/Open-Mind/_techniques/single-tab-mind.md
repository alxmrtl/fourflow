# Single-Tab Mind

> Mental multitasking doesn't split your attention — it multiplies switching costs until there's almost no attention left.

## Protocol

1. **Name the one thing** — before the session begins, complete this sentence out loud or in writing: "For the next [time block], I am only working on [specific task or project]." Be precise. "Working on the report" is a context. "Working on the introduction section of the report" is better. Precision reduces boundary ambiguity during the session.
2. **Close all other contexts physically** — close browser tabs related to other projects, move other notes out of view, put the phone face down. Each visible other-context is a tab held open in the environment that mirrors a tab held open in the mind. Environmental single-tab-ness supports mental single-tab-ness.
3. **Handle any "urgent other things" before starting** — if you know another task is about to demand attention (a message you're expecting, a decision someone needs), resolve it before starting or explicitly defer it: "I will address that after this block." Write it in your capture system (see open-loop-closure). Then start.
4. **When a competing thought or task surfaces mid-session**: write it down on a capture surface, categorize it as "later," and return to the focal task. Do not evaluate whether it's important right now — that evaluation is itself a context switch. Defer all evaluation to the end of the block.
5. **End the session with a deliberate close** — note where you stopped, what the next action is, and what you'll pick up in the next session. This prevents the current task from becoming an open loop that bleeds into your next context.

## When to Use

- Before any session where you're aware of multiple active work streams competing for attention — especially during periods of high project volume.
- When you notice yourself opening multiple documents or browser contexts "just in case" before starting a session — this is a pre-commitment failure that this technique corrects.
- When productivity feels high but output is low — the subjective sense of busyness without output is a signature of high-context-switching overhead.

## Key Insight

> "Parallel contexts don't share your attention — they each take all of it, sequentially, leaving almost nothing for actual work."
