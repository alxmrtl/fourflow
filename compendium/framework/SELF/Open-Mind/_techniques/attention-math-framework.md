# Attention Math Framework

> Estimate the daily attention cost of your highest-attention possessions — converting vague awareness of "too much stuff" into a concrete cognitive tax you can measure and then reduce.

## Mechanism
Every possession generates ongoing cognitive overhead — tracking its location, maintaining it, worrying about it, making decisions about it. This overhead is not incurred once at purchase; it's a daily attention subscription renewed whether or not you consciously think about the object. Calibration benchmarks (Rian Doris): a pair of pants ≈ 10 seconds/day; an iPad ≈ 2 minutes; a Rolex ≈ 10 minutes; a house ≈ 1 hour. Just those four items = ~1.2 hours/day, over 300 hours/year — approximately 14 days of continuous waking attention annually. Making this visible converts abstract minimalism advice into a decision with a measurable return.

## Protocol

1. List your highest-attention possession categories (not everything you own — focus on the highest-consumption items: devices, vehicles, luxury goods, high-maintenance gear, collections).
2. For each, estimate daily attention in seconds or minutes: time spent thinking about using it, cleaning it, maintaining it, finding it, worrying about it, or noticing it.
3. Sum the total. Convert to hours per year (daily minutes × 365 / 60).
4. Identify the top 3 highest-cost items. Ask for each: "Does this possession contribute enough value to justify this attention subscription?"
5. For items where the answer is no or uncertain: release, sell, or simplify. The reduction is permanent and passive — unlike behavioral changes, fewer possessions cost nothing to maintain.

## When to Use
As a one-time audit to identify the highest-leverage items, then periodically (quarterly) to catch accumulation. Run before any major purchase: estimate the attention subscription before acquiring.

## Key Insight
> "Every possession is a subscription you pay in attention, not money."
