# Task-Positive Network

> The brain network that activates during focused external engagement — the neurological substrate of embodied, absorbed action.

## What It Is

The Task-Positive Network (TPN) — also called the frontoparietal network or central executive network — activates when the brain is directing attention toward an external demand: solving a problem, tracking a moving target, executing a physical skill, or engaging deeply with a task. It prioritizes sensory input over internal narrative, making every sight, sound, and tactile sensation feel more vivid and immediate.

The TPN is the structural opposite of the Default Mode Network. When one is active, the other is suppressed. Most people oscillate between the two: the TPN engages during work, then the DMN intrudes when the task feels uncertain or the challenge drops below the threshold of full engagement.

## Why It Enables Flow

Flow's absorption quality — the sense that time disappears, that action unfolds without conscious effort — is the phenomenological experience of sustained TPN dominance. Dopamine and norepinephrine support this state: dopamine sharpens the reward signal of progress, norepinephrine increases sensory acuity. Together they produce "effortless attention" — where the task itself becomes the most interesting thing in the room.

The trigger for TPN activation is appropriate challenge. Too easy, and the TPN doesn't need to fully engage; the DMN wanders in. Too difficult, and stress circuits override. The flow channel is the zone where TPN activation is sustained without alarm.

## Application

TPN activation is accelerated by strong sensory engagement — especially physical movement, precise tactile feedback, or rich auditory input directly tied to the task. Starting with physical engagement (adjusting posture, handling materials, a brief physical warm-up) primes the TPN and makes cognitive absorption follow faster. Feel your way in before thinking your way in.
