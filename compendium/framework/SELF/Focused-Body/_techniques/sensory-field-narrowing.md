# Sensory Field Narrowing

> The nervous system cannot attune to the task when it is simultaneously tracking twelve other signals.

## Protocol

1. **Audit all active sensory channels** before sitting down — visual, auditory, tactile, olfactory. Run them in sequence: What am I seeing? What am I hearing? What am I feeling on my skin? Spend 30 seconds, no more.
2. **Eliminate each non-essential channel** from most disruptive to least. Visual first: clear the desk surface of everything not needed for this session; close browser tabs, turn the screen away from windows if glare is present. Auditory second: silence notifications, choose a single consistent sound environment (silence, white noise, or a single music track — no podcast, no variable conversation). Tactile third: adjust temperature or remove uncomfortable clothing if it's registering.
3. **Anchor on a single sensory primary** — the one channel most connected to the work. For writing: visual field of screen + keystroke sound. For reading: page texture + visual field. For creative work: ambient sound + visual surface. Name it explicitly before starting: "I'm tracking [X]."
4. **Begin work within 60 seconds** of completing the audit. The sensory field narrows fastest when the work starts immediately — delay allows re-expansion.

## When to Use

- Before any session requiring sustained concentration where the environment hasn't been pre-set.
- When you notice your eyes or attention keep pulling away from the task in the first few minutes — this is a diagnostic signal that competing channels are still open.
- After moving from a high-stimulation context (commute, meeting, social) directly into deep work — the sensory field from the previous context remains partially active.

## Key Insight

> "Attunement isn't built by concentrating harder — it's built by removing what the body is tracking instead."
