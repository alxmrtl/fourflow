# Rhythm and Pacing

Align your focused work to the brain's natural 90-minute high-activity windows and protect the low phases as genuine recovery — not gap time to fill.

## Protocol

1. Track the natural fade in concentration that arrives 75–90 minutes into a session. This is the ultradian boundary — not a motivation failure.
2. When the fade arrives, stop. Take a genuine rest interval: no screens, low cognitive load. Walk, lie down, or do something your mind can release completely.
3. Don't re-enter deep work until the next high-activity phase begins — usually 20–30 minutes into recovery.
4. To find your personal high-activity windows: note your energy and clarity at 90-minute intervals across three days. Patterns will emerge. Schedule deep work inside those windows.

*Practice for two weeks to calibrate your personal rhythm. The windows are real but individual.*

## When to Use

- When sessions feel productive early then progressively worse — this is the ultradian pattern
- When planning a day with multiple deep work blocks
- When fatigue is accumulating session to session despite adequate sleep (usually a pacing problem, not a sleep problem)

## Key Insight

> "The brain cycles through 90-minute windows of high and low activity. Flow is accessible in the high phase. Forced in the low phase."

## Recall

**Hook:** The brain cycles through 90-minute windows of high and low activity — flow is accessible in the high phase, forced in the low phase.
**Mechanism:** Aligning focused work to ultradian high-activity windows and genuine rest to the low phases creates sustainable energy output rather than cumulative depletion.
**Anchor:** Notice the natural fade that occurs 75–90 minutes into a focused session — that's the ultradian signal, not a motivation failure. Rest there. Don't push into the next cycle without a genuine recovery interval.
