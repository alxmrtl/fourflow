# Tension Scan and Release

> Held tension is the body arguing with itself — it cannot act with full commitment when part of it is braced against something.

## Protocol

1. **Set a 3-minute timer** before the session begins. This is not optional time — it is preparation, like warming up before a physical performance.
2. **Sit upright with feet flat on the floor.** Close the eyes if the environment permits.
3. **Scan from the top down** — move attention through each region in sequence, spending 10–15 seconds per area. Notice tension without judgment; you are reading the body's current state, not criticizing it. Sequence: scalp and forehead → jaw and face → neck and throat → shoulders → upper back → arms and hands → chest → abdomen → lower back → legs → feet.
4. **At each area where tension is detected**: inhale and actively contract the muscle group for 4–5 seconds (squeeze it harder than it already is), then exhale fully and release completely. Let the released area go slack. Pause for 2 seconds before moving on.
5. **Complete the scan with three full breaths** — slow exhale-extended breathing (in 4, out 6) to consolidate the autonomic shift.
6. **Begin work immediately.** The window of post-release clarity is short; starting within 60 seconds captures it.

## When to Use

- Before any session where you've come from a high-stress or physically demanding prior context (difficult meeting, commute, physical exertion, emotional conversation).
- When you notice you are "gripping" work — typing hard, jaw tight, shoulders raised — these are mid-session signals that the body never fully released.
- Before creative work specifically, where somatic openness correlates with generative thinking more than in analytical work.

## Key Insight

> "You cannot act with full commitment from a body that is bracing — the tension is a standing argument against action."
