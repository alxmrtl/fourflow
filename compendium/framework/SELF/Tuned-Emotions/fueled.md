# Fueled
*The Maintain quality of Tuned Emotions*

## What it is

When this quality is present, the effort-to-progress loop is running. You finish a unit of work and feel something shift — not just exhaustion but the particular satisfaction of having moved something forward. That feeling replenishes the willingness to continue. When absent, work becomes a series of outputs with no felt sense of landing. You can be busy without ever experiencing momentum. The loop that makes effort feel worth continuing goes quiet, and motivation starts to thin — not dramatically, but steadily, the way a fire does when it stops receiving fuel.

## Science

Teresa Amabile's progress principle, drawn from diary studies across hundreds of workers, found that small wins on meaningful work were the single strongest predictor of positive inner work life — more than recognition, more than incentives. The mechanism is neurochemical: dopamine neurons fire not on receipt of reward but in anticipation of it (Salamone & Correa, 2002). Perceived progress is neurochemically equivalent to partial reward — it replenishes the willingness to continue.

When exertion and reward decouple — when effort feels unlinked from discernible progress — the dopamine loop breaks. The brain stops anticipating reward from the next unit of effort, motivation collapses, and the precursor state to burnout begins. Capa et al. (2022) showed that effort-contingent rewards transfer intrinsic value to the effort itself: the structure that links doing to moving generates its own motivational charge over time, independent of whether any external reward arrives.

The maintain path is structural: design the work so completion happens constantly. Discrete sub-goals with clear before/after states, tracked in real time. Progress visibility is the antidote; effort-reward coupling is the mechanism that makes it motivationally active.

## Recall

**Hook:** The brain doesn't reward effort — it rewards completion. Structure work so completion happens constantly.
**Mechanism:** Dopamine fires in anticipation of reward (not just upon receipt), so perceived progress is neurochemically equivalent to partial reward — it replenishes the willingness to continue.
**Anchor:** Progress Ledger — at session end, write 1–3 sentences on what *moved* today, not just what you did. "The argument in section 2 is now clear" couples effort to meaningful progress (not just output volume).
