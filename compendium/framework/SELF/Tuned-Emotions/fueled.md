# Effort-Reward Coupling

> Links exertion directly to perceived progress.

## Why It Enables Flow

Flow is intrinsically rewarding — but not automatically. It requires a felt connection between exertion and progress: when that connection is intact, the brain's dopamine system fires in a sustained loop, making each unit of effort feel like it's going somewhere. Dopaminergic neurons fire *in anticipation* of reward (Salamone & Correa, 2002) — meaning perceived progress is neurochemically equivalent to partial reward, replenishing the willingness to continue. When effort and reward decouple — when exertion feels unlinked from discernible progress — the dopamine loop breaks, motivation collapses, and the precursor state to burnout begins. Progress visibility is the antidote; effort-reward coupling is the mechanism that makes it motivationally active.

## Techniques

- [[progress-ledger]] — end-of-session log of what *moved* today (not what was done); closes the dopamine loop after each session
- [[micro-completion-architecture]] — decomposes work into "wins" with clear before/after states; closes the dopamine loop constantly rather than once at session end
- [[detachment-protocol]] — three-step sequence: accept present reality, surrender control of the uncontrollable while keeping full effort, release the specific delivery pathway — dissolves the monitoring layer that splits focus

## Recall

**Hook:** The brain doesn't reward effort — it rewards completion. Structure work so completion happens constantly.
**Mechanism:** Dopamine fires in anticipation of reward (not just upon receipt), so perceived progress is neurochemically equivalent to partial reward — it replenishes the willingness to continue.
**Anchor:** Progress Ledger — at session end, write 1–3 sentences on what *moved* today, not just what you did. "The argument in section 2 is now clear" couples effort to meaningful progress (not just output volume).
