# Aperture Widening

> Use sensory expansion to physically widen the perceptual aperture that anxiety narrows — letting more of reality in per moment.

## Mechanism
Anxiety narrows the perceptual aperture: each tick of consciousness delivers almost nothing — the smallest possible slice of reality. This is why every second drags when you're afraid. It's not that time slows; it's that you're perceiving so little per moment that each moment feels impoverished. The protective narrowing that patterns produce isn't just emotional constriction — it's perceptual constriction. You're literally seeing less of what exists. Sensory inputs that span peripheral vision and proprioception force the aperture to process more per tick, which mechanically relaxes the constriction enough to restore function.

Re-patterning addresses this at the root: every pattern transformed is an aperture dilation. The heart-opening step in Consciousness Re-Patterning isn't metaphorical — it's the system relaxing a constriction that was reducing perceptual bandwidth. People often report the world looks more vivid and spacious after successful re-patterning. The aperture is wider. They're literally seeing more per moment.

## Protocol

1. Notice the subjective time distortion: seconds dragging, hyper-focus on threat, tunnel vision, the sense that the world is closing in.
2. Name it explicitly: "My aperture has narrowed. I'm perceiving the smallest possible slice right now." Naming it creates the observer position.
3. Widen physically — do all three in sequence:
   - **Look**: Scan the room slowly. Take in corners, objects, peripheral edges.
   - **Feel**: Press feet into the floor. Feel the chair, clothing, temperature.
   - **See wide**: Deliberately soften focus and take in peripheral vision as wide as possible.
4. These sensory inputs force the aperture to process more per tick. The anxiety doesn't disappear, but the narrowing relaxes enough to take useful action.
5. If possible, move into DMN Goal Engagement or Consciousness Re-Patterning from this slightly-widened state.

## When to Use
In acute anxiety moments — when the world feels like it's closing in, when time feels distorted, when attention is locked onto the threat. This is a 30–60 second intervention. It doesn't transform the underlying pattern but creates enough perceptual space to interrupt the full constriction spiral and regain access to conscious action.

## Key Insight
> "Anxiety doesn't just feel like the world is closing in. Perceptually, it is. Each moment delivers less. Re-patterning reopens the aperture."
