# Positive Spike Management

> When excitement or positive emotion spikes mid-session, acknowledge it, make a capture note outside the working frame, then return to work — maintaining session continuity without suppressing the feeling.

## Mechanism
Most practitioners focus on managing negative affective spikes (anxiety, frustration). But positive affective spikes — excitement at a breakthrough, pleasure at a good result, enthusiasm about a new idea — can equally fragment attention. They are high-salience signals: when excitement spikes, the brain wants to dwell, to share, to extend the pleasure. The work pauses while the reward is metabolized. A capture note tells the brain the spike has been acknowledged and doesn't need to keep generating attention to preserve it. The excitement is captured; the session continues.

## Protocol

1. When a positive spike arises during a session — a good result, an elegant solution, an exciting new direction — apply the observer stance immediately: "There is excitement here."
2. Make an explicit record of it *outside* your working context — a brief note in a capture list: "Had an idea about X — revisit after session." One sentence maximum.
3. Acknowledge it: "That's noted. It will be there when the session ends."
4. Return to work. The session continues.

**After the session**: Return to the capture list and give the spike its full attention. Metabolize the excitement, explore the idea, share the result. The technique doesn't suppress the feeling — it defers the metabolization to a non-session context where it can be fully honored.

## When to Use
Any time a positive emotion spike is significant enough to pull attention away from the current work. Common moments: discovering an unexpected solution, completing a section that comes together well, seeing a new direction that excites you more than the current task. The positive spike management protocol is also useful for managing "shiny object" distraction — new ideas that feel more compelling than the current session's work.

## Key Insight
> "Flow isn't the absence of feeling. It's the absence of being controlled by feeling."
