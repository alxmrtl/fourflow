# External Focus of Attention

> Direct attention to the *effect* your work is producing rather than to how you're doing the work — bypassing the self-monitoring circuits that fragment performance.

## Mechanism
Gabriele Wulf's research across decades consistently demonstrates that directing attention externally (to the effect of your movement or the environmental result) produces better performance than directing it internally (to your own body mechanics or process). Internal focus activates exactly the self-monitoring circuits that fragment performance. External focus bypasses them. The mechanism: internal focus causes the prefrontal cortex to supervise the motor/cognitive system — adding a monitoring overhead that degrades output. External focus allows the skill system to run without supervision, accessing automaticity and accumulated expertise directly.

## Protocol

1. Identify the external output of your work — not what you're doing, but what the work is *doing* in the world or to a reader/user/listener.
2. Redirect attention there:
   - Writing: attend to the *reader's experience* ("will this sentence land clearly for them?") rather than your own performance ("is this good writing?")
   - Code: attend to the *system's behavior* ("what does the test tell me?") rather than your code quality ("is this elegant?")
   - Music: attend to the *sound in the room* rather than the execution in your fingers
   - Design: attend to the *user's path through the interface* rather than your design choices
3. When attention drifts back to self-evaluation ("how am I doing?"), ask: "What is the work doing right now?" Return to external observation.

## When to Use
During any performance-oriented work where self-consciousness is active. Particularly powerful in skill-based or embodied work (music, athletics, speaking, physical craft) but equally applicable to knowledge work where the evaluative voice is the main obstacle.

## Key Insight
> "Your performance is almost always better when you're paying attention to what the work is doing, not to how you're doing the work."
