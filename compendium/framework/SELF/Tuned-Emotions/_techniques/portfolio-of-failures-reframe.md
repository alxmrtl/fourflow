# Portfolio of Failures Reframe

> Treat every mistake as a deposit into the portfolio of experience that eventually yields capability — not evidence of inadequacy, but investment in future competence.

## Mechanism
Errors carry information about the gap between current skill and required skill. When treated as threats (identity evidence), the ego protects itself from the verdict and the information gets lost — the feedback loop between mistake and correction gets derailed through self-evaluation. When treated as deposits, the loop runs clean: mistake → information → adjustment → next attempt. The person who accumulates failures at speed becomes capable faster than the person who avoids them.

## Protocol

1. When a mistake occurs, pause before the automatic "what does this say about me?" question fires.
2. Ask instead: "What does this add to the portfolio?" Specifically:
   - What information did this error reveal about the gap between current skill and required skill?
   - What does this tell me about what to do differently on the next attempt?
3. Extract the information, adjust the approach, and continue. The mistake has served its function.
4. Optionally: keep a running note of "portfolio deposits" — a running record of what each failure contributed. Over time, this builds visible evidence that failures are indeed productive.

## When to Use
Any time a mistake triggers the identity-threat loop — the familiar feeling of "this means something bad about me." The reframe is most important in the early stages of skill acquisition, when failure rate is highest and the natural reward signal is weakest.

## Key Insight
> "People are so afraid of making mistakes that they make the biggest mistake of all — not making mistakes."
