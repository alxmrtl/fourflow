# Detachment Protocol

> A three-step sequence for loosening the grip on outcomes — accept what is, release control of what isn't yours, and drop your attachment to the specific delivery route — while maintaining full effort.

## Protocol

**Step 1 — Accept what is (60 seconds)**
When resistance to a situation arises ("this shouldn't be happening," "this isn't what I wanted"):
1. Pause before acting from the resistance.
2. Ask: "Can I accept that this is what's happening right now?" Not "am I happy about it?" Just: can I acknowledge it as currently real?
3. Sit with it for a breath: "This is what is."
4. From that grounded place, choose your response: "Given that this is real, what action makes sense?"

*This is acceptance, not resignation. You can fully accept present reality AND take action to change it. Resistance first → action from reactivity. Acceptance first → action from clarity.*

**Step 2 — Surrender control, not effort**
Distinguish what is yours to control from what isn't:
- Yours: the quality of preparation, the attention you bring, the execution in this session
- Not yours: how it will be received, whether it succeeds, what it leads to

Consciously release grip on the uncontrollable column: "I'm doing my best work. What happens next is not in my hands — and I'm OK with that." Return full attention to the work in front of you.

*If grip on outcomes returns mid-session (checking metrics, imagining reactions, worrying about reception): "That's not mine to control. What's in front of me is."*

**Step 3 — Release the how**
Most attachment isn't to the outcome but to the specific delivery pathway. Name and drop it:
1. State the want clearly — be specific about what you actually want, without hedging.
2. Feel the having — spend 30–60 seconds in the state of already receiving it.
3. Name and explicitly release the specific how: "The how is not my job."
4. Follow what feels most alive right now, even if it seems unrelated.
5. Receive movement toward your want from any direction, including unexpected ones.

*This isn't passivity. Full effort + released grip = surrender. Half effort + released grip = abandonment.*

## Variant — In-Moment Surrender Cue (Dodson)

For situations that are moving too fast for the full three-step protocol — a presentation already underway, a conversation escalating, a performance where you can't pause — use the single-word cue:

1. In the moment when you notice resistance, forcing, or grip: silently say **"Surrender."**
2. Let that word land in the body. Drop muscle tension in shoulders, jaw, hands.
3. Allow what you've prepared to run — trust the preparation rather than managing the execution.
4. Return full attention to the person or task in front of you.

This is not passive: full effort continues. Surrender means releasing management of the outcome, not releasing engagement with the work.

## Variant — Stakes Reduction (Dodson)

When performance anxiety is high before an important situation, deliberately reduce perceived stakes to match the relaxed state you bring to unimportant situations:

1. Remember a situation you treated as critically important (a first date, a client pitch, a significant presentation). Notice how you felt — monitored, guarded, evaluating each move.
2. Remember a situation you treated as unimportant (catching up with an old friend, a low-stakes meeting, casual conversation). Notice the quality of presence — relaxed, spontaneous, natural.
3. Identify: the quality of performance was likely better in the unimportant situation, even though less was "on the line."
4. Before the important situation: write down 3–5 qualities that make it *less* important than it feels. Not denial — genuine reasons why the world doesn't end if it goes imperfectly.
5. Enter the situation with the same quality of presence you bring to unimportant ones. Bring the craft without the audition.

## When to Use

- Before high-stakes sessions where the outcome matters significantly (publishing, presenting, pitching)
- When you notice straining or forcing toward a specific route
- When bad feedback, failed output, or changed plans triggers resistance
- When you've been working something for a long time with diminishing returns — attachment to the how may be the block
- In creative work where the gap between output and intention triggers the avoidance loop

## Key Insight

> "You can want something fully and be OK at the deepest level if it doesn't happen. This paradox usually improves the outcome — you act without the constraint of fear."
