# Interpersonal Safety Audit

> A diagnostic for identifying where interpersonal fear is constraining execution — distinguishing real threats (historically confirmed) from anticipated threats (never tested), then designing experiments to verify or dissolve the anticipated ones.

## Mechanism
Amy Edmondson's research identifies psychological safety as whether people believe speaking up, making mistakes, and taking initiative will be met with curiosity rather than punishment. Most interpersonal fear constraining creative execution is anticipated, not confirmed — the person has never tested whether the audience/collaborator/client will actually judge harshly, but assumes they will. Untested assumptions generate the same attentional cost as confirmed threats, but without the informational validity. The audit separates signal from noise.

## Protocol

1. For each significant relationship or audience in your current work, ask: "If I produced something imperfect here, what would happen?" Write the honest answer.
2. Identify which answers generate physical tightening (chest, jaw, stomach). These are your active interpersonal fear sites.
3. For each fear site, classify:
   - **Real threat**: this person/context has actually punished mistakes before (evidence exists)
   - **Anticipated threat**: I've never tested this, but I assume judgment (assumption only)
4. For **anticipated threats**: design a small experiment — produce something incomplete, ask a "dumb question," share an unpolished draft — and observe the actual response. Most anticipated judgment is not confirmed by evidence.
5. For **real threats**: note them as environmental design information. Some contexts genuinely require social field adjustment ([[social-field-alignment]]) or structural protection (closed-door drafting sessions, separation of drafting from review roles).

## When to Use
When execution feels self-conscious; when drafts feel risk-laden; when the gap between "what I produce alone" and "what I produce when others might see it" is large. Also useful at the start of a new collaboration or client relationship, to surface any interpersonal fear that might constrain work quality.

## Key Insight
> "The inner critic has an audience. Find out who it's performing for."
