# Affect Labeling

> Name the emotion with one or two words the moment it appears — the label reduces amygdala activation and frees attentional bandwidth before the spike can capture the system.

## Mechanism
Naming an emotion with a word reduces its intensity and duration. Neuroimaging confirms: affect labeling increases prefrontal cortex activity (specifically the right ventrolateral PFC) and reduces amygdala activation (Lieberman et al., 2007, *Psychological Science*). The emotion doesn't disappear — it becomes a processed signal rather than an overwhelming one. This is not suppression: suppression requires its own attentional cost and often amplifies the emotion. Labeling engages the regulatory system before the spike fully captures resources.

## Protocol

1. When you notice an affective spike during a session — frustration, excitement, worry, disappointment — briefly name it internally with a simple, accurate label:
   - "Frustration."
   - "Excitement."
   - "That's disappointment."
   - "Anxiety about the result."
2. One or two words is sufficient. Do not add narrative or judgment:
   - Not: "I shouldn't be frustrated."
   - Not: "This is frustrating because the deadline is tomorrow."
   - Just: "Frustration." The narrative expands the spike; the label closes it.
3. Return to work. The emotion may still be present — the goal is not elimination but non-capture.

**Building the reflex**: Practice affect labeling throughout the day in non-work contexts — in conversation, while reading, while commuting. The reflex becomes faster and more automatic with repetition. By the time a session-critical spike appears, the label fires before the spike has fully landed.

## When to Use
Any time an emotional spike breaks the surface during a work session. The earlier the label, the smaller the spike it interrupts. Particularly valuable for recurring emotional patterns (the same frustration that appears at the same point in creative work, the same anxiety before sending output for review).

## Key Insight
> "The gap between feeling an emotion and being hijacked by it is the word you give it."
