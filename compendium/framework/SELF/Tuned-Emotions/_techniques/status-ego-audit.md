# Status Ego Audit

> A structured check for whether accumulated status or expertise in one domain is generating ego armor that blocks learning in adjacent ones.

## Mechanism

Ego monitoring during performance is one form of self-consciousness. A subtler and more durable form emerges from *accumulated status*: when you've built expertise, reputation, or identity around a domain, the ego treats new information as a threat to what it has already earned. The result is selective blindness — you can no longer easily say "I don't know" in areas adjacent to your expertise, because that would imply your existing status doesn't transfer. The mechanism is fear: not performance anxiety, but the more entrenched fear of losing what you've already built. This creates the stagnation pattern — people who were learners become defenders. The diagnostic is simple: can you sit across from someone who knows more than you in a specific area and genuinely receive from them?

## Protocol

1. **List your status domains**: Where do you currently have expertise, reputation, or social identity? (job title, skill, community, knowledge area, life experience)
2. **For each domain, list adjacent areas** where you interact but haven't built equivalent competence
3. **Run the reception test**: In the last month, have you said "I don't know, teach me" to anyone operating in those adjacent areas? Or have you instead offered your existing status-domain knowledge as a substitute?
4. **Check the deflection patterns**: When someone with adjacent expertise challenges something you believe, do you engage their point directly — or do you route around it by reasserting your domain status?
5. **Identify the specific fear**: What exactly would you lose if it turned out your existing identity was wrong in this area? Name it.
6. **Set a deliberate reception rep**: In the next interaction with someone who has relevant expertise, try the posture of a beginner. Notice the discomfort and stay with it rather than covering it.

## When to Use

Quarterly or when you notice yourself defending more than inquiring. High-signal situations: when feedback lands as threat rather than information; when you find yourself explaining your credentials before engaging with someone's point; when a challenge to your existing model provokes irritability rather than curiosity. Also useful after any domain achievement — recognition or success often kicks the mechanism into higher gear.

## Key Insight

> "Their status in one domain doesn't transfer to every other domain. They still have massive blind spots, but the ego won't let them see it."
