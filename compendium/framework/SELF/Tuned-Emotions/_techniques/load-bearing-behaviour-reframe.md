# Load-Bearing Behaviour Reframe

> Reframes persistent "bad" behaviours as load-bearing structures that cannot be removed without replacing their function — shifting from willpower-based correction to structural replacement.

## Mechanism
Behaviours that persist under pressure are not failures of discipline — they are carrying load. They reduce tension, resolve contradiction, and keep the system running, even at long-term cost. Short-term stability is being purchased with long-term damage. If you remove a load-bearing behaviour by force (willpower, rules, shame), the load has nowhere to go. The system compensates — often by collapsing entirely or substituting something worse. This is why willpower-based self-correction produces oscillation: suppress, build pressure, catastrophic release, repeat. The reframe: you are not fighting a bad habit. You are identifying a load-bearing function that needs a replacement structure before the current one can be removed.

## Protocol

1. **Identify the persistent behaviour.** What keeps showing up despite your intention to stop? (Procrastination, compulsive checking, avoidance, numbing, over-eating, conflict-ending concessions.)
2. **Ask: "What is this behaviour doing for the system right now?"** Look for:
   - Immediate tension reduction
   - Collapse of contradiction (makes two conflicting things stop fighting)
   - Restoration of short-term coherence (makes things feel okay, briefly)
3. **Confirm it's load-bearing.** Test: if you removed this behaviour right now, would tension/instability increase? If yes, it's carrying load.
4. **Name the function, not the form.** "This behaviour provides X" — where X is the function (e.g., "relief from decision fatigue," "escape from evaluation pressure," "resolution of identity conflict"). The function is what needs replacing, not the behaviour itself.
5. **Design or identify a replacement.** The replacement must serve the same function through a less destructive mechanism. It can be biological (exercise, sleep, breath), behavioural (a different action that serves the same function), or structural (changing the environment so the load doesn't arise).
6. **Install the replacement before removing the original.** Or, if no replacement is available, reduce the load first so the original behaviour is no longer needed.
7. **Never force removal without replacement.** The critical rule: removing load-bearing behaviour without replacing its function concentrates instability and produces catastrophic failure rather than distributed, manageable collapse.

## When to Use
When you find yourself repeatedly failing at the same self-correction. The pattern of "commit → hold → break → shame → recommit" is the signature of willpower applied to a load-bearing behaviour. Also applicable to others: when someone's behaviour seems irrational or self-destructive, ask what load it's carrying before attempting to change it.

**Edge case**: In immediate threat conditions (addiction crisis, safety risk), removal may need to precede replacement. Perform replacement post-removal to restore stability.

## Key Insight
> "You are not fighting a bad habit — you are replacing a load-bearing function."
