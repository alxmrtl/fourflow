# Load-Capacity Diagnostic

> Assesses whether the system is operating above its current capacity under load — and prescribes reduction or descent rather than willpower.

## Mechanism
Under pressure, systems don't act on what they believe — they act on what they can sustain. When load exceeds capacity at the current operating level, the system will descend to whatever level can carry the load. This is not failure of will; it is physics. The traditional challenge-skill model asks "is the task at the right difficulty?" — this technique adds the second variable: "what load is the system already carrying?" A task calibrated perfectly for a rested person becomes overwhelming when the system is already loaded from other sources (emotional, physiological, cognitive, environmental). Repeated descent under load increases future load and reduces future capacity — making the next descent more likely.

## Protocol

1. **Name the load sources.** Scan four categories: emotional (unresolved conflict, grief, anxiety), physiological (sleep debt, illness, hunger), cognitive (open loops, decisions pending, information overload), environmental (noise, interruptions, social pressure). Don't analyze — just list.
2. **Estimate current capacity.** Ask: "What is the highest level of performance I can realistically sustain right now without forcing?" Not ideal, not yesterday — right now.
3. **Compare load to capacity.** If load > capacity: the system will descend. This is not optional.
4. **Choose the intervention:**
   - **Reduce load**: remove or defer load sources (close tabs, reschedule the conversation, eat, nap, simplify the environment)
   - **Lower the level**: reduce the ambition of the current task to something the system can actually sustain ("not today" rather than "never again"; one paragraph rather than the full chapter)
5. **Do not force operation above capacity.** Forcing produces oscillation (repeated attempts and collapses) or catastrophic failure (system crashes entirely). Neither is recoverable quickly.
6. **Rebuild upward only when lower levels are stable.** Once the system can sustain the current level without strain, step up one level. Not two. Not the original target. One.

## When to Use
Before any session where you notice pre-existing load — tiredness, emotional residue, cognitive overwhelm. Especially valuable on days when the gap between "what I should be able to do" and "what I can actually do" is wide. That gap is load, not laziness. Also use as a mid-session diagnostic when performance is degrading: the system may have been at capacity at the start and accumulated session load has pushed it over.

**Contraindication**: When load is genuinely low and you're avoiding starting — that's not a capacity issue, it's resistance. Use [[anxiety-suppression]] or [[ego-monitoring-removal]] instead.

## Key Insight
> "You cannot skip layers under load. Higher levels only hold once the lower ones can carry the load."
