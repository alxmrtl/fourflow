# Pre-Session Equanimity Practice

> A 3-minute pre-session practice that witnesses whatever emotional weather is already present — reducing mid-session interruptions by processing the backlog before work begins.

## Mechanism
Unacknowledged emotions are louder than acknowledged ones — they push for attention precisely because they have not been given it. The practice doesn't remove emotions; it processes whatever is already present before the session begins, preventing emotional backlog from accumulating during the session. Research in contemplative psychology defines equanimity as a "dispositional tendency toward all experiences regardless of affective valence" — a cultivatable state, not an innate trait. 90 seconds of witnessing the pre-existing emotional landscape significantly reduces mid-session hijacks that landscape would otherwise generate.

## Protocol

1. **Settle (60–90 seconds)**: Sit quietly. Let the day settle — don't try to clear your mind, just stop adding to it. Breathe naturally.
2. **Witness**: Notice what emotional weather is already present, following the observer stance. Name each one without judgment: "There's some anticipation. Some residual tension from the morning. A little restlessness."
3. **Acknowledge each**: "That's here. I see it." Not: "That needs to go away before I can work." Not: "That shouldn't be here." Just: witnessed, seen, acknowledged.
4. **Establish orientation**: Silently state what the session is for: "For the next [time period], my attention goes to [specific task]. Whatever arises, arises. The work continues."
5. **Begin immediately** after step 4. Do not pause to assess readiness.

## When to Use
Before every significant work session, especially:
- Days when emotional carry-over from prior events is high (conflict, bad sleep, anxiety about an external outcome)
- Before creative sessions where emotional openness matters
- As the mental element within a [[pre-session-entry-ritual]]

The practice takes 3 minutes. The return on that time is reduction in mid-session emotional interruptions, each of which costs more than 3 minutes to recover from.

## Key Insight
> "Emotions that are acknowledged before you begin are far less likely to interrupt you while you're working."
