# Anxiety-Excitement Reframe

> Anxiety and excitement are physiologically identical — elevated heart rate, cortisol, norepinephrine. The difference is the label. The label determines the performance outcome.

## What It Is

Research by Alison Wood Brooks (Harvard) found that telling yourself "I am excited" before a high-stakes performance consistently outperforms telling yourself "I am calm" — and significantly outperforms trying to suppress the arousal. The reason: anxiety and excitement share the same physiological substrate. Attempting to move from anxiety to calm requires reducing arousal. Moving from anxiety to excitement requires only re-labeling it — keeping the arousal and redirecting its meaning.

This is a cognitive appraisal intervention: the arousal signal is the same; the appraisal step (threat vs. opportunity) is what shifts.

## The Mechanism

High arousal interpreted as **threat** → defensive narrowing → reduced options → performance declines.
High arousal interpreted as **opportunity** → approach motivation → expanded options → performance improves.

The brain routes the same neurochemical state through entirely different circuits depending on the label attached to it. The somatic experience (racing heart, heightened alertness) becomes resource rather than interference when framed as excitement.

## Protocol

The four-step somatic anchor (from source material):
1. **Name it as excitement** — say it out loud three times: "I am excited." Not "I will be calm." Not "It's fine." The arousal stays; the frame shifts.
2. **Check information sufficiency** — "Do I have what I need to act?" This activates cognition, reducing pure emotional reactivity.
3. **Identify the upside** — "What's the value of meeting this challenge?" Shifts attention from threat to opportunity.
4. **Anchor somatically** — pair the reframe with a physical gesture (specific posture, breath pattern, hand position). Repeated use builds an automatic association.

## Important Distinction

This is not toxic positivity or suppression. The arousal is acknowledged and used, not denied. The intervention works precisely because it channels rather than suppresses — which is why "calm down" is less effective than "get excited."
