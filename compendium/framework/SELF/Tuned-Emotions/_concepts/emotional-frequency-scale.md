# Emotional Frequency Scale

> Emotions exist on a spectrum from survival-mode (low frequency, narrow aperture) to elevated states (high frequency, wide aperture). Flow requires sufficient baseline elevation to be accessible.

## What It Is

Emotions differ not just in content but in energetic quality. Survival emotions — fear, guilt, shame, resentment — are characterized by contracted attention, threat-orientation, and narrow perceptual range. They activate stress pathways that consume cognitive resources for survival rather than releasing them for creation.

Elevated emotions — gratitude, curiosity, joy, love — are characterized by expanded attention, openness, and broader perceptual range. Neurochemically, they support the dopaminergic and serotonergic states that make flow accessible.

The "frequency" framing (from energy medicine traditions, also mapped in Hawkins' consciousness research) is a useful metaphor: low-frequency states create more cognitive friction, higher-frequency states reduce it.

## Link to Flow

Flow requires a minimum emotional baseline to be accessible. This isn't about being happy — it's about whether the nervous system is in a state that permits the openness and risk-tolerance that flow demands.

Chronic residence in survival-mode emotions (high baseline anxiety, persistent shame, unresolved resentment) effectively lowers the ceiling on flow depth. Not because flow requires happiness, but because survival-mode narrows the aperture — less information, less pattern recognition, less creative access per moment.

## Application

Pre-session emotional elevation isn't about forced positivity — it's about shifting the baseline enough to make the aperture available. Common methods:
- Brief gratitude practice (shifts from scarcity orientation to sufficiency orientation)
- Physical movement (breaks somatic contraction associated with fear/shame states)
- Connecting with intrinsic motivation (anchors to elevated emotion before the session starts)

The goal isn't to suppress survival emotions when they arise — it's to avoid making them the habitual baseline from which flow is expected to emerge.
