# Yerkes-Dodson Law

> Performance follows an inverted-U curve against arousal: too little = inert, too much = overwhelmed, optimal = flow-accessible.

## What It Is

The Yerkes-Dodson Law (1908) describes the empirical relationship between physiological/psychological arousal and task performance. It produces an inverted-U: as arousal increases from flat, performance rises — until a peak, after which further arousal degrades performance.

The peak is not the same for all tasks. Simple or well-rehearsed tasks tolerate higher arousal. Complex, creative, or cognitively demanding tasks — the kind most associated with flow — require lower, more precise arousal calibration.

## The Neurochemistry

**Under-aroused**: Dopamine and norepinephrine are low. The default mode network dominates. Mind wanders. Tasks feel unrewarding. This is the boredom-side failure mode.

**Optimally aroused**: Moderate norepinephrine sharpens attention and working memory. Dopamine signals relevance and pattern recognition. The prefrontal cortex is fully online. This is the flow-accessible zone — the peak of the curve.

**Over-aroused**: Cortisol surges. Amygdala hijack narrows perception to threat detection. The prefrontal cortex — responsible for complex reasoning, creativity, and perspective-taking — goes partially offline. Performance degrades. This is the anxiety-side failure mode.

## Link to the Flow Channel

The flow channel (challenge-skills balance) is the behavioral expression of the Yerkes-Dodson peak. The 4% rule is a practical heuristic for staying at the top of the inverted-U rather than sliding down either slope.

Tuning emotions is partly tuning arousal: anxiety tools (breathing, scope reduction) move you back down the right slope; boredom tools (raised stakes, novelty, constraints) push you up the left slope.

## Application

Use arousal level as a diagnostic: is focus absent because I'm under-stimulated, or is it absent because I'm over-stimulated? The interventions are opposite. Getting this wrong — using stimulants when already over-aroused, or reducing challenge when already bored — makes the state worse.
