# Disturbance Log

> A running log of anything that breaks your calm state — the raw material for re-patterning work.

## Mechanism
You cannot transform a pattern you haven't noticed. The act of logging a disturbance immediately creates a small gap between you and the reaction — you shift from being the pattern to observing it. Over time, the log builds pattern literacy: you begin to see which circumstances consistently activate which responses, making the invisible architecture of anxiety visible and workable.

## Protocol

1. Keep a dedicated notebook or note (the "Freedom Log") accessible throughout the day.
2. When anything takes you out of a calm, relaxed state, record three things: (a) what happened externally, (b) how you reacted internally — emotion, thought, body sensation, (c) a brief pattern label if obvious (e.g., "needing to be right," "fear of judgment").
3. Don't analyze in the moment. Just note and continue. The full re-patterning work happens later in a dedicated session.
4. At the end of the day or before a re-patterning session, review the log. Notice patterns across entries — the same label appearing under different circumstances reveals a core pattern.

## When to Use
Throughout the day, any time focus breaks or a negative internal shift occurs. Especially useful during work sessions — when the urge to check a phone, avoid a task, or spiral into doubt appears, that moment is logging material. The log is not a place to dwell; it's a dispatch system. Write it and return to work.

## Key Insight
> "The log itself begins shifting your relationship to disturbances — you become an observer rather than being fully identified with the reaction."
