# Feedback Loop

> The fundamental mechanism of self-correction: output from a system becomes input back into it, allowing the system to adjust toward a target state.

## What It Is

A feedback loop is a closed causal circuit where the result of an action is fed back into the system that produced it, allowing for continuous adjustment. The concept originates in cybernetics (Norbert Wiener) and is one of the most fundamental structures in biology, engineering, and behavior.

There are two types: **negative feedback loops** reduce deviation from a target (a thermostat, a body regulating temperature) — these are stabilizing. **Positive feedback loops** amplify deviation — these create momentum (viral growth, skill compounding, addiction). Both are operative in flow: negative loops calibrate skill-challenge balance; positive loops drive the deepening absorption once flow begins.

In the context of flow, "feedback" means any signal that tells you whether what you just did worked — a correct keystroke, a solving of a sub-problem, the feel of a ball landing where aimed. These micro-signals are what the brain uses to steer action without conscious oversight.

## Why It Enables Flow

Flow's self-correcting quality — the sense that action adjusts fluidly to demands — is only possible when feedback loops are fast and tight. Long lag between action and signal (delayed feedback) breaks the loop. The brain must then fall back on planning and monitoring (prefrontal, DMN-activating processes) rather than real-time sensorimotor adjustment. The loop is the mechanism that makes action feel effortless.

## Application

Designing feedback loops means asking: "How quickly will I know if this worked, and what will that signal look like?" In knowledge work — where feedback is naturally slow — the practice is manufacturing artificial loops: visible checklists, word counts, test suites, daily check-ins. Make the abstract concrete and the lagging immediate.
