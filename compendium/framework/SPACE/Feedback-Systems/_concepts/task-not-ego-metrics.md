# Task-Not-Ego Metrics

> Measuring the task rather than your identity — feedback that answers "is this working?" rather than "am I good enough?" keeps the signal informational rather than threatening.

## What It Is

Task-not-ego metrics is the distinction between two types of performance measures: those that evaluate the task objectively (words written, bugs resolved, time under tension, distance covered) and those that implicitly evaluate the self (how good am I?, will people respect this?, what does this say about my ability?).

The distinction matters neurologically. Ego-referential metrics activate the Default Mode Network and self-monitoring circuits. They trigger threat responses — performance anxiety, ego protection, evaluation apprehension. Task-referential metrics remain in the executive attention system; they are processed as navigational data, not as verdicts.

The same metric can be ego or task depending on how it's framed: "3,000 words today" (task) vs. "I'm the kind of person who writes 3,000 words" (ego). The first provides a target and a signal; the second loads identity onto the outcome.

## Why It Enables Flow

Flow requires that errors feel informational rather than threatening. If your metrics are ego-referential, every deviation from target is a threat to identity — activating defensive responses that compete directly with absorption. Task-referential metrics allow the same deviation to be processed as course-correction data, which is exactly what the self-correcting quality of flow requires.

## Application

Audit your current performance measures: what are you actually tracking, and what does a "bad" result imply about you? Reframe each metric from a judgment ("I failed") to a signal ("that approach didn't work; adjust"). The reframe isn't just semantic — it's a literal shift in which neural circuits process the feedback.
