# Minimal Effective Feedback

> The smallest amount of feedback signal needed to steer accurately — enough to course-correct, not so much that the monitoring itself becomes the task.

## What It Is

Minimal Effective Feedback (MFF) is the calibration question at the heart of feedback system design: how much signal do you actually need to stay on track? More feedback is not always better. Excess feedback creates its own load — you end up monitoring the monitoring, checking dashboards instead of doing work, and using the feedback process as a form of productive-feeling avoidance.

The concept mirrors the medical principle of minimal effective dose: the smallest amount of an intervention that produces the target result. The same logic applies to feedback frequency, granularity, and format. Surgery requires immediate tactile feedback on every move. Writing a book requires feedback at the paragraph level, not the sentence level. Matching the feedback granularity to the task type is the craft.

## Why It Matters for Flow

Flow requires attention to stay on the task. Feedback that arrives too frequently, at too fine a granularity, or in formats requiring interpretation pulls attention away from work and toward meta-monitoring. The ideal feedback signal is peripheral — felt rather than analyzed, informing action without interrupting it.

The retro-casting exercise is useful: review your last three flow states and notice how often you received feedback, and in what form. The pattern will reveal your MFF threshold.

## Application

Ask three questions: (1) What's the minimum signal I need to know if I'm on track? (2) How frequently does that signal need to arrive? (3) What format makes it most legible without requiring interpretation? Design your feedback system to answer these three, and no more.
