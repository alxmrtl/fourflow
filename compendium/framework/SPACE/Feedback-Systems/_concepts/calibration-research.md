# Calibration Research

> The scientific study of correspondence between confidence and accuracy. Most people are systematically overconfident — confident in areas of low actual performance. Calibration training — deliberately comparing predictions to outcomes — measurably improves accuracy and enables genuine feedback integration.

## What It Is

Calibration is the property of a probability estimate where the stated confidence corresponds to actual accuracy: a well-calibrated person who says "I'm 80% sure" should be right about 80% of the time across many such judgments. Calibration research emerged from cognitive psychology and decision science, associated with landmark work by Kahneman and Tversky on heuristics and biases, and operationalized by Philip Tetlock in his studies of expert forecasting.

The consistent finding across decades of research is that most people are overconfident — they assign higher confidence to their judgments than the judgments warrant. Lichtenstein, Fischhoff, and Phillips (1982) documented this systematically: for general knowledge questions, when people said "I'm 90% sure," they were right about 70% of the time. The gap between stated confidence and actual accuracy is the miscalibration. Dunning and Kruger (1999) showed a particularly stark version: the least competent performers in a domain had the highest miscalibration — they dramatically overestimated their performance because they lacked the metacognitive skill to recognize their own errors.

The critical finding for practice: calibration can be improved through deliberate feedback. Tetlock's superforecasting research showed that people who systematically compared their predictions to outcomes, scored their accuracy, and updated their confidence accordingly became measurably well-calibrated over time. The mechanism is direct: predictions + outcome comparison = confidence adjustment. Without the comparison, confidence floats free of evidence.

## Why It Enables Flow

Honest feedback requires not just receiving information about performance but accurately weighting it — knowing when to update significantly (the signal was strong, the evidence is clear) versus when to discount (noise event, isolated data point). Overconfidence produces systematic under-updating: the person already "knows" they're skilled, so feedback contradicting that belief is discounted. Poor calibration makes the feedback system noisy at the interpretation layer even when the raw signals are clean.

Calibration training strengthens the honest quality by ensuring that when feedback arrives — positive or negative — its weight in updating beliefs and behaviors corresponds to its actual evidential strength. Well-calibrated performers adjust faster, learn faster, and maintain accurate challenge-skill assessments.

## Application

The core calibration practice is prediction logging: before any session, project, or decision, write down a specific prediction and confidence level. After the outcome, compare. Do not interpret or explain — just score. Over time, patterns of systematic over- or under-confidence in specific domains become visible and correctable. This is the same practice used by Tetlock's superforecasters.
