# Signal vs. Noise Filter

> Not all feedback is about you — most of it is about the person giving it.

## Protocol
1. After receiving any piece of feedback, pause before internalizing it. Ask: "Is this feedback tracking my actual output, or is it tracking this person's state?"
2. Apply two filters in sequence. First: did the person have direct observation of the specific output being evaluated? If no, file as anecdote, not signal. Second: is the feedback tied to a specific observable behavior or result? If not, it is general impression — useful as one data point, not as calibration.
3. Identify the feedback's source category: (a) task-adjacent — person saw the work; (b) outcome-adjacent — person saw the downstream result; (c) relational — person is responding to the relationship dynamic; (d) projected — person is responding to their own state. Only (a) and (b) count as performance signal.
4. Log signal-category feedback separately from noise-category feedback. Review only signal-category when assessing your actual performance level.
5. Do not discard noise-category feedback — it contains relational and social information. But keep it in a separate register from performance data.

## When to Use
- After receiving praise or criticism that produced a strong emotional reaction — strong reactions indicate the evaluation circuits are already compromised
- Before making any skill-level assessment or deciding whether to change approach
- After a performance event (presentation, published piece, completed project) when multiple sources of feedback arrive simultaneously

## Key Insight
> Honest feedback is rare not because people are dishonest, but because most feedback is not actually about your performance.
