# Progress Dashboard

> Abstract effort is motivationally inert — make the signal tangible and the motivation follows.

## Protocol
1. Identify one to three behaviors that, performed consistently, will produce your target outcome. These must be completely within your control and observable immediately after doing them. No lagging indicators — sessions, words, reps, captures, calls made. Maximum three metrics to prevent dashboard complexity from becoming its own overhead.
2. Choose a display medium that lives in your primary work environment. Physical: a whiteboard, a paper tracker on the wall, a notepad on your desk. Digital: a pinned note, a simple spreadsheet open at session start, a widget on your desktop. The medium does not matter; visibility does. You must encounter the display every time you work.
3. Update the dashboard immediately after each session — not at the end of the day, not when you remember. Immediate update preserves the behavioral coupling between action and recorded progress.
4. Add a weekly summary row: the cumulative number for the week. This creates a second feedback loop (weekly) alongside the daily one, which provides perspective when daily numbers are low.
5. Do not add complexity. Resist the urge to track more metrics, add color coding, or build analysis layers. The dashboard's function is visibility, not analytics. If it takes more than 30 seconds to update, it will be abandoned.

## When to Use
- At the start of any sustained practice period — the dashboard should be in place before the first session, not added after motivation has already dipped
- When effort is high but motivation is low — often a symptom of invisible progress, not insufficient results
- When you have multiple competing projects and need to maintain parallel momentum in more than one domain

## Key Insight
> Progress you can see compounds differently than progress you know about — the brain needs the display, not the data.
