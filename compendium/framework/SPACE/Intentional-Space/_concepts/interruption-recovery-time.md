# Interruption Recovery Time

> The empirical finding that returning to a task after interruption takes significantly longer than the interruption itself — due to context reloading, cognitive set re-establishment, and suppression of the interrupting task's residual activation.

## What It Is

Interruption recovery time is the measured gap between the end of an interruption and the restoration of pre-interruption task engagement. Gloria Mark's research quantified this at approximately 23 minutes for a full return to the original task — a figure that surprises most people because the interruption itself may have lasted only seconds or minutes. The recovery cost is not proportional to the interruption duration; it is determined by the cognitive work required to restore the full task context.

The recovery process has three components. First, context reload: working memory must re-establish the task representation that was active before the interruption — the current state of the problem, the subgoals in progress, the relevant constraints. This is cognitively expensive because working memory does not hold state across interruptions. Second, cognitive set re-establishment: the specific attentional configuration, perceptual filters, and processing mode appropriate to the task must be re-activated. Third, suppression: the interrupting task (and its emotional residue, pending decisions, and unresolved threads) continues to compete for attentional resources after the interruption ends — Sophie Leroy's research on "attention residue" demonstrates that incompletely resolved interruptions leave stronger residual activation than completed ones.

The non-linearity is the critical finding: multiple small interruptions compound into recovery costs vastly exceeding their apparent duration. A workflow with 10 brief interruptions across a four-hour block may produce less than one hour of effectively focused work.

## Why It Enables Flow

Flow cannot survive frequent interruption — not because flow is fragile in the moment, but because the recovery time required after each interruption makes re-entry impossible within any realistic work session. A two-hour block with three interruptions contains, in effect, no flow: each interruption consumes more recovery time than the interval between interruptions allows.

Understanding recovery time reframes the value of protection. It is not about avoiding inconvenience — it is about the mathematics of attention: each interruption has a fixed minimum recovery cost, and that cost is paid whether or not the interruption felt significant. Protected time is not a luxury; it is the only condition under which flow has room to occur.

## Application

The practical implication is that partial protection — reducing interruptions from 10 to 7 — produces only partial recovery cost reduction. The effective strategy is complete protection for defined windows: all interruptions off, all communication channels closed, with a communicated re-availability time. Short complete blocks outperform long partial ones.
