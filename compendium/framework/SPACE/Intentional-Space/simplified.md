# Simplified
*The Restore quality of Intentional Space*

## What it is

When this quality is present, the room is doing its job quietly. Nothing in the visual field is demanding categorization, maintenance, or attention. The environment isn't a source of competing signals — it's a container for the work. When absent, the space is working against you before the session starts: each object in the peripheral field is a small claim on processing, each piece of clutter a background tracking obligation. It's not dramatic. It's just always there, raising the floor that deep work has to get above.

## Science

The physical environment is not a neutral container — it's a constant source of signals the brain must process, prioritize, and track. Saxbe and Repetti (2010) found that individuals in cluttered home environments had elevated cortisol levels throughout the day compared to those in tidy environments — a physiological stress response operating continuously and independently of conscious experience of the space.

Cognitive load theory (Sweller) established that extraneous load — processing that isn't directly required by the task — consumes working memory capacity that would otherwise go to learning and performance. A cluttered environment generates extraneous load through continuous background categorization: the brain automatically monitors and tracks objects in the visual field regardless of whether they're attended consciously.

The restorative path is permanent subtraction. Unlike behavioral interventions (which require ongoing effort to maintain), removing an object eliminates its cognitive overhead forever. Three levels of implementation calibrate this: aggressive minimalism (only tools in the space), tempered minimalism (flow wins conflicts), and mild minimalism (conscious trade-offs). The restore event is a possession purge — a structured single-session audit that corrects accumulated drift before it becomes entrenched.
