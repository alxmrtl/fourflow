# Interruption Control

> Prevent attention capture from external sources.

## Why It Enables Flow

An interruption doesn't merely pause a flow state — it may prevent another from beginning for hours. The attention residue left by an external capture persists long after the interrupting event has passed, leaving the mind partially fragmented even when nominally on-task. Flow requires a minimum of 15–20 minutes of uninterrupted focus to emerge; a single intrusion resets that accumulation. Interruption control protects not just the current state but the session's capacity for depth.

The research quantifies what this feels like: Gloria Mark, Daniela Gudith, and Ulrich Klocke (2008) found that the average knowledge worker takes 23.15 minutes to return to the original task after an interruption — not because the interruption itself is long, but because re-engagement requires re-establishing context, suppressing residue from the interrupting event, and rebuilding the attentional momentum that was broken. Sophie Leroy's attention residue research (2009) explains the mechanism: switching to a new context — even briefly — generates active background processing of the prior task that persists into the next context. An interruption is a forced switch that creates residue in both directions: residue from whatever you were doing, and residue from the interrupting content itself. The cumulative effect across a day of frequent interruptions is not eight interrupted sessions — it is a mind that never fully inhabits any of them.

In aperture terms, an interruption forces the aperture to a different focal point and resets the accumulation needed for flow. A flow state is not just a point in time — it is the product of sustained coherent aperture direction over 15–25 minutes. Each interruption returns the aperture to zero. Protecting the session is not a comfort preference; it is the minimum structural requirement for flow to emerge at all. A session without interruption protection is structurally incapable of producing flow, regardless of motivation, skill level, or task clarity.
