# Friction and Behavior

> BJ Fogg's behavioral design research and the broader friction literature: small increases in effort required to perform a behavior dramatically reduce its frequency; small decreases dramatically increase it. Friction is not inconvenience — it is a behavioral force.

## What It Is

BJ Fogg's Behavior Model (2009) proposes that behavior occurs when three elements converge: motivation, ability, and a prompt. Of these, ability — which Fogg operationalizes as simplicity, or the inverse of friction — is often the most tractable variable. You cannot always raise motivation, but you can almost always reduce friction. And friction operates as a genuine behavioral force: even tiny increases in the effort required to perform an action can reduce its frequency to near zero; tiny decreases can bring dormant behaviors back to high frequency.

The friction-behavior relationship follows a non-linear curve analogous to activation energy in chemistry: behaviors below a certain effort threshold happen easily and frequently; behaviors above that threshold require deliberate motivation and are avoided by default. The threshold is not fixed — it shifts with motivation levels and fatigue — but friction reliably raises the threshold and frictionlessness reliably lowers it.

This insight extends far beyond Fogg. Daniel Kahneman's System 1/System 2 framework implies the same: effortful actions require System 2 engagement, which is limited and depleting. Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein's nudge architecture operates on friction: making healthy options the default (lower friction) dramatically increases healthy choices without changing incentives or information. The research on medication adherence, exercise initiation, savings enrollment, and healthy eating all converge: friction is the dominant behavioral variable in most real-world choice contexts.

## Why It Enables Flow

Flow entry has an activation energy cost — the struggle phase must be traversed before absorption occurs. Anything that raises the friction of beginning work raises this activation energy and increases the probability of not starting, stopping early, or choosing a lower-resistance alternative. Tool friction compounds: each additional step required to start (locating the app, navigating to the file, choosing the tool, remembering the workflow) is a micro-barrier that depletes willpower before the real work has begun.

Removing friction from the path to flow entry directly increases the probability of flow occurring. It is one of the highest-leverage interventions available: no motivation required, no skill development required — just systematic removal of small obstacles between intention and action.

## Application

The friction audit: for your most important daily deep work, map every step from "decision to work" to "actually working." Identify and eliminate each step that can be eliminated. Reduce the path to one deliberate trigger: a single keypress, a single physical cue. For distraction behaviors, add friction: log out of social platforms, add friction to phone access, require a deliberate multi-step override before checking non-essential channels.
