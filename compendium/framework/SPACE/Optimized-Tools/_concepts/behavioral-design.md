# Behavioral Design

> Engineering your environment so that desired behaviors are the path of least resistance — making deep work the default, not the exception.

## What It Is

Behavioral design applies insights from behavioral economics and psychology (Thaler, Sunstein, BJ Fogg) to the design of personal environments and systems. The core insight: behavior follows the path of least resistance. Most of what we do is triggered by environmental cues and made easy or hard by the friction present. People aren't lazy — they follow design.

The implication: you can engineer your own behavior by designing your environment deliberately. Put the phone in another room → you check it far less. Remove social media apps → you scroll far less. Keep the guitar by the couch → you play far more. Set up your workspace the night before → you start work far faster. None of this requires willpower in the moment because the decision has already been made in the design.

At the digital level, behavioral design means curating phone settings, notification architecture, home screen layout, and app placement so that focus tools are easy to reach and distraction tools require deliberate effort.

## Why It Enables Flow

Flow's struggle phase requires the critical transition from "haven't started yet" to "in motion." The higher the friction at that transition — apps scattered, workspace unset, phone within reach — the more willpower it costs. Willpower is a finite resource that depletes through the day. Behavioral design reduces the cost of starting and sustaining flow to near zero by removing friction before it can be encountered.

The converse is equally important: adding friction to distraction. Making the undesired behavior harder (logging out of social media, putting the phone in a drawer, using app blockers) dramatically reduces its frequency because most impulses die in the presence of even small resistance.

## Application

The full-environment audit: for every behavior you want to increase, reduce the friction by one step. For every behavior you want to decrease, add one step of friction. Run this systematically across your phone, desktop, physical workspace, and daily schedule. The compounded effect is significant.
