# Neurochemical Reward Test

> Before a desire-driven acquisition, distinguish pleasure (temporary dopamine spike from owning something new) from enjoyment (sustained reward from meaningful work) — and decline acquisitions that trade permanent attention cost for temporary reward.

## Mechanism
Pleasure and enjoyment feel similar at the point of acquisition but have opposite downstream effects on flow capacity. Pleasure (acquisition-dopamine): a temporary neurochemical spike that degrades within hours to days, provides no skill development or growth, and leaves a permanent ongoing attention cost in the form of the object's cognitive overhead. Enjoyment (flow-derived): the sustained reward from meaningful work and deep engagement that builds skills, increases flow accessibility over time, and reinforces intrinsic motivation. Csikszentmihalyi: pleasure doesn't lead to increased skills, growth, or a better future; enjoyment, through flow, does.

## Protocol

1. When an acquisition feels compelling, pause before purchasing and ask: "Is this pleasure or enjoyment?"
2. Diagnose:
   - **Pleasure signal**: excitement is about acquiring the new thing; you can already imagine the satisfaction of owning it; the desire fades when you imagine having owned it for a year
   - **Enjoyment signal**: the item directly enables or deepens the work you find meaningful; you can point to the specific capability it adds to your craft
3. If it's pleasure: decline the acquisition. You're buying a brief dopamine hit at the cost of permanent cognitive overhead.
4. If it's enjoyment: proceed — but still apply [[one-in-one-out]].
5. If uncertain: wait 48 hours. If the desire is pleasure-based, it will have significantly reduced. If enjoyment-based, the specific functional value will remain clear.

## When to Use
Before any non-essential acquisition driven by desire rather than clear functional need — particularly: technology upgrades, luxury goods, collectibles, and aspirational purchases ("the kind of person I want to be would own this"). Also useful when reviewing subscriptions and recurring commitments.

## Key Insight
> "You're not buying the object — you're buying a temporary dopamine hit at the cost of permanent attention overhead."
