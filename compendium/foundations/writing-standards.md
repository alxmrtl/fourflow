# Compendium Writing Standards

The FlowCompendium serves two readers at once: the FourFlow Agent (which needs precision, structure, and consistent metadata) and human learners (who need warmth, clarity, and content they can actually feel). This document defines how to write for both without compromising either.

---

## The Dual-Purpose Contract

**For the agent:** Frontmatter fields are always present and correctly typed. Section headings match the template exactly. Wikilinks use consistent kebab-case slugs. The `definition` field is a single clean sentence.

**For the human:** Every section reads like it was written by someone who has lived what they're describing, not someone who has catalogued it. The reader should feel understood before they feel informed.

Neither purpose excuses the other. Precision without warmth is a database. Warmth without precision is a journal. The compendium is neither.

---

## The Tone Target

The reference voice is the READY articles — not the current framework files.

**What the READY articles do well:**
- Open with something specific and felt: *"You know that feeling when your mind is racing like a hamster on espresso?"*
- Translate before they explain: *"The Japanese concept of 'Shoshin' translates to 'beginner's mind'"*
- Use direct address naturally: *"You're probably above your window"*
- Vary sentence length. Short sentences land. Longer ones develop.
- Trust the reader: they're intelligent, not experts. Assume curiosity, not background knowledge.

**What the current framework files do instead:**
- Lead with mechanism: *"Reduce anticipatory threat signals that fragment focus"*
- Stack abstract nouns: *"challenge-skill ratio calibration"*, *"anticipatory threat fragmentation"*
- Use passive constructions: *"Flow requires X"* (×36 = mechanical)
- Name concepts without translating them: *"DMN activation", "BIS/BAS systems", "construal-level theory"*

---

## The Translation Rule

Any scientific term, researcher name, or technical model must be introduced with a plain-English follow-up in the same sentence or the one immediately after.

**Wrong:**
> The DMN suppression mechanism explains why flow and anxiety are mutually exclusive.

**Right:**
> Flow suppresses the Default Mode Network — the brain's self-referential rumination system — which is why it's impossible to genuinely worry and be in flow at the same time.

Named researchers get the same treatment:
> Dan Siegel's Window of Tolerance model describes a band of optimal arousal — inside it, thinking, feeling, and acting remain integrated; outside it, in either direction, they fall apart.

## Forbidden Patterns

In addition to the BRAND_BIBLE's banned words, the following patterns are specifically prohibited in the compendium:

### Abstract noun stacks
The compression of multiple abstract concepts into a dense phrase that requires decoding before it means anything.

- ❌ *"anticipatory threat signal fragmentation"*
- ❌ *"affective state mis-calibration"*
- ❌ *"self-referential evaluation loop activation"*
- ✅ *"the loop where you evaluate your own performance while performing it"*

### Untranslated acronyms and jargon
Any term that a smart non-expert would have to look up to understand.

- ❌ *"DMN", "RAS", "BIS/BAS", "HPA axis", "TPN"*
- ✅ Name it, then explain it in one clause: *"the Default Mode Network (the brain's default rumination circuit)"*

### The "flow requires X" construction
This appears in nearly every quality file. Repeated 36 times, it becomes invisible.

- ❌ *"Flow requires the nervous system to be regulated"*
- ✅ *"Regulated nervous systems don't have to manage background noise — they can give everything to the work"*

### "The failure mode is..." as an opener
Leads with the negative in a way that feels clinical rather than helpful.

- ❌ *"The failure mode isn't feeling emotion. It's misreading it."*
- ✅ *"Emotions don't disrupt flow — misreading them does."*

### Three-adjective stacks
Borrowed from the BRAND_BIBLE.

- ❌ *"calm, regulated, open-hearted presence"*
- ✅ Pick one. *"a calm, open heart"* or *"regulated presence"*

### Passive "experience" language
Describes phenomena without placing the reader inside them.

- ❌ *"The narrative frame you're operating inside shapes whether challenge is experienced as engagement or threat"*
- ✅ *"The story you're telling yourself about the challenge determines whether it feels like a test or an invitation"*

## Good vs. Bad — Side by Side

### Quality file `definition` field

| ❌ Before | ✅ After |
|-----------|----------|
| *"Reduce anticipatory threat signals that fragment focus"* | *"When the nervous system is settled, everything else becomes possible"* |
| *"Reduce background cognitive noise"* | *"A clear environment gives your attention nowhere to leak"* |
| *"Specify why the action matters"* | *"Knowing what's at stake turns effort into intention"* |
| *"Preload direction before action begins"* | *"A compelling vision pulls you toward the work instead of pushing you through it"* |

### `## What it is` section

**❌ Before (Quiet quality):**
> Anxiety is resistance to life unfolding as it is — when anticipatory threat patterns activate, the heart closes, attention fragments, and the calm, relaxed ground that flow requires disappears.

**✅ After:**
> When the nervous system is quiet, the body isn't competing with the work. You can feel the edges of a challenge without bracing against them. The moment anxiety activates — even subtle, background anxiety — part of your attention goes to managing it, and that part is no longer available for the work.

---

### Technique one-liner

**❌ Before:**
> One sentence: what this technique IS and what it does for flow.

**✅ After (example for observer-redirect):**
> When the inner critic activates during a session, this three-move sequence shifts your identification from the emotion to the observer of it — and then redirects attention to what the work is actually doing.

## Section-by-Section Guidance

### `definition` (frontmatter)
One sentence. Warm and specific. Describes what the quality *is* (its positive state), not what it removes or suppresses. Should make a practitioner nod, not take notes.

### `## What it is`
2–3 sentences. Write from the inside of the experience. What does it feel like when this quality is present? What quietly shifts when it's absent? The reader should recognize themselves in this description.

### `## Science`
150–300 words. This is where the research lives — translated. Name the models that matter, explain each one in plain language, then connect to why this quality matters for flow. This section is the "why it's real" section, not the "how to do it" section.

### `## Techniques` (quality files)
A linked list. One line per technique: the wikilink and a one-line description of what the technique does. The description should make the technique sound useful, not just name it.

### `## Protocol` (technique files)
Numbered steps. Concrete enough to follow without reading the source. Active verbs. If a step requires explanation, include a sub-bullet — but keep it tight. The goal is that a reader can execute this without re-reading.

### `## Recall`
The training-facing section. Write the Hook as something a student could say to a friend. Write the Mechanism as a one-sentence answer to "why does this work?" Write the Anchor as something physical, habitual, or spatially located — not abstract.

---

## The Final Check

Before saving any compendium file, ask:

1. **Could an intelligent non-expert understand every sentence without Googling?**
2. **Does the `definition` field describe a state I'd want to be in?**
3. **Does the `## What it is` section feel like something a human wrote?**
4. **Have I translated every scientific term I've used?**
5. **Would I be embarrassed to read this out loud to someone I respect?**

If any answer is no — rewrite before saving.
